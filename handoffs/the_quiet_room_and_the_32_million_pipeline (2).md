# The Quiet Room and the $32 Million Pipeline

## Inside Vancouver Public Schools' dependency on private staffing corporations during a historic fiscal crisis

---

A school day can begin normally and still end in a quiet room.

At Hough Elementary, an autistic student named Colin Bailey, mostly nonverbal, was restrained almost daily during the 2015-16 school year, according to OPB and InvestigateWest reporting. On one occasion, he was isolated in a small room for more than two hours. His mother, Cara Bailey, later alleged the district failed to provide the one-on-one paraeducator required by her son's individualized education plan, substituting less specific "additional adult support" instead. A psychiatrist diagnosed Bailey with PTSD attributed to what he experienced at school.

That same year, the district documented 1,110 physical restraints of students across its schools. OPB and InvestigateWest later described the rate as roughly once an hour of every school day.

Bailey's case is not a metaphor. It is the most concrete answer to the question at the center of this investigation: when a school district cannot staff its classrooms with trained, stable adults, what happens to the children who need those adults most?

The answer, in Vancouver Public Schools, is now measurable in both human suffering and public dollars.

---

### The numbers behind the crisis

On March 12, 2024, the Vancouver Public Schools Board of Directors voted unanimously to slash $35 million from the district's annual budget. The specifics were staggering: 50 elementary teachers, 63 secondary teachers, 33 teachers on special assignment, 10 counselors, 9 librarians, 29 custodians. More than 260 positions eliminated from a district serving roughly 22,000 students.

The stated cause was a structural deficit. Enrollment declining. State funding insufficient. Federal pandemic relief dollars expired. The district told parents there was simply no other way.

Paraeducator Christopher Smith challenged administrators at a public meeting that night. "As you burn out your staff, there are limited replacements," he said. "This is not a renewable resource. Have you been in a classroom with 40 or more students?"

Fort Vancouver High School students walked out in protest. The teachers' union president, Jamie Anderson, told the board: "Cutting 100-plus classroom teachers. I don't see how that minimizes impact to the classroom."

The story the district told was familiar in public education: a system caught between rising costs and static revenues, making unavoidable and painful choices.

There was one problem with that narrative. It was incomplete.

In the same 2024-25 fiscal year, the austerity year, the year of 260 lost positions and shuttered library programs, Vancouver Public Schools processed payments totaling $13,326,622 to private healthcare staffing corporations. Of that, $10,970,973 flowed to a single vendor: Amergis Healthcare Staffing.

Its previous name was Maxim Healthcare Services.

In 2011, under that name, the company agreed to pay the United States government $150 million to settle federal criminal and civil charges of systematic Medicaid fraud. The charges included billing for nursing services never performed and paying illegal kickbacks. The DOJ called it one of the largest healthcare fraud settlements in history. The company entered a federal criminal guilty plea. Its founder and multiple executives were individually indicted.

In 2022, Maxim quietly rebranded its staffing division as Amergis.

By 2025, the transfer of Vancouver's payments was complete. Maxim's receivables from VPS dropped to $15,716. Amergis's hit $10.97 million.

No press release announced the change. In an analysis of 53 board meetings spanning 2016 through 2025, this investigation found no public board discussion of the vendor's fraud settlement history.

### Five years, $32 million

The trajectory of payments from Vancouver Public Schools to private staffing agencies is documented in 25,578 unique payment records extracted from 153 warrant register PDFs, the financial documents the board approves in bulk at the end of each monthly meeting.

In fiscal year 2020-21, the district paid staffing vendors $349,709. The relationship was modest: supplemental nursing for a small number of medically fragile students.

By 2022-23, Maxim alone received $5.9 million. An 1,743% increase in three years.

By 2024-25, the total across all staffing vendors reached $13,326,622, consuming 28.16% of the district's entire Object 7 (Purchased Services) budget. In 2020-21, that share was 1.17%.

The five-year cumulative total: $32,189,236.

Amergis and Maxim, which are the same corporate entity through a rebrand, together captured $28,074,510 of that total, or 87.2% of all staffing vendor payments. The remaining vendors, ProCare Therapy ($2.9 million), Soliant Health ($1.07 million), Aveanna Healthcare ($695,200), and Pioneer Healthcare ($119,213), filled in the margins.

To place the $13.3 million in proportion: while the district eliminated classroom teaching positions, librarians, and counselors, it simultaneously increased its purchased services expenditures by nearly $3.9 million in a single year. A district that could not afford to retain a middle school librarian, a position costing roughly $85,000 annually with benefits, found the capacity to increase its external vendor spending by sixty times that amount in twelve months.

Object 7 did not bleed during the austerity year. It grew.

### A contract designed for dependency

The scale of the spending is extraordinary. The contract that authorized it is, in some respects, more so.

Buried in the VPS BoardDocs system, attached to a consent agenda item approved without separate debate on or about July 13, 2021, is the Master Service Agreement between the district and Maxim Healthcare Staffing. Its provisions read like a vendor-controlled pricing architecture.

Auto-renewal: the agreement renews annually unless terminated. To exit, the district must provide 90 days' advance written notice. The original 2016 agreement required only 30 days.

Rate increases: Maxim retained the right to change rates with 30 days' notice and no ceiling on such changes. The district pre-agreed to automatic annual rate increases, compounding yearly, with no expiration.

No spending cap: the agreement contains no maximum annual expenditure. No "not to exceed" clause governs the total volume of staffing the vendor may bill.

And if the district wanted to directly hire a worker placed by the vendor, eliminating the agency markup, the contract imposed a conversion fee: the greater of $5,000 or 30% of the worker's annualized salary. For a school psychologist billed at $100 per hour, that penalty would exceed $43,000.

The 2021 rate schedule specified hourly rates ranging from $37-$45 for paraprofessionals to $85-$120 for school psychologists and BCBAs. By 2024, the range had widened to $45-$180, a 50% increase at the high end.

For a relationship that grew from $349,709 to $13.3 million in four years, these terms are not merely unfavorable. They are the financial architecture of dependency.

### The $3 million estimate

The governance question distills into a single data point.

The BoardDocs agenda item for the 2024-25 Amergis contract states, verbatim: "Approximate Cost to District: $3,000,000 for the 24-25 SY."

Actual payments to Amergis that year totaled $10,970,973. More than three times the estimate presented to the board. The $3 million figure was described as an "Approximate Cost," not a contractual cap, and the underlying agreement contained no spending ceiling. The gap may reflect expanded service needs as new IEP referrals and mid-year enrollments grew. But the governance question is not whether needs grew. It is whether any mechanism existed for the board to review and authorize such a significant expansion beyond what was presented.

The full Amergis Master Service Agreement for 2024-25 was never publicly attached to BoardDocs. The board approved the contract based on a summary description.

### No competitive bidding. No emergency declaration. No debate.

Washington state law requires competitive bidding for school purchases of furniture, supplies, equipment, and construction exceeding $75,000. Professional staffing services are not among the enumerated categories.

The exemption, codified in RCW 28A.335.190, allowed Vancouver Public Schools to route $32 million in staffing contracts without the competitive procurement required for physical goods. No public bid was required. No competitive alternative was solicited.

The district also never invoked the emergency waiver mechanism available under the same statute, which requires a board resolution declaring conditions likely to result in immediate physical injury to persons or property. A search of 5,367 embedded text segments from board packets, contract documents, and minutes found zero instances of an emergency procurement declaration for staffing.

The district revised its procurement policy, Board Policy 6220, at least five times during the period when vendor payments grew from $349,709 to $13.3 million. None of those revisions added competitive bidding requirements or oversight mechanisms for professional staffing services.

Staffing vendor contracts were instead approved through the consent agenda, the block of routine items voted on collectively without individual discussion. In earlier years, when the relationship was modest, contracts appeared as Action Items requiring separate debate. As spending grew into the millions, the contracts migrated to the consent agenda.

### The premium: what outsourcing costs beyond the invoice

A central error in public debate is comparing an internal hourly wage to an agency bill rate. A district does not pay "$28 per hour" for a direct hire. It pays salary plus benefits plus retirement plus payroll taxes, and it pays them even when the employee is sick, in training, or performing non-service duties.

Using publicly available data from VEA and VAESP salary schedules, SEBB employer contributions ($1,178 per month), DRS pension rates (9.11%), and payroll taxes (7.65%), this investigation constructed a fully loaded internal cost baseline for three core roles, using 1,440 productive hours per year.

A paraeducator at VAESP Step 3 costs the district approximately $56,842 fully loaded, or $39.47 per hour. The vendor rate for the same role: $45 per hour.

A SPED teacher at VEA BA+0 Step 5 costs $87,869 fully loaded, or $61.02 per hour. The vendor rate: $75 per hour.

An SLP or RN at VEA MA+0 Step 5 costs $97,581 fully loaded, or $67.76 per hour. The vendor rate: up to $180 per hour.

At the SPED teacher midpoint, the $13.3 million in vendor spending purchases roughly 177,688 vendor hours, equivalent to about 123 vendor FTEs. If the district could have recruited and retained those roles internally at negotiated CBA rates, the same money would have funded approximately 152 internal FTEs. The difference is roughly 28 SPED-teacher-equivalent positions, or about $2.5 million in "lost efficiency."

Even at the absolute floor rate in the model, the district loses $1.64 million to the vendor premium.

The district cut 260 positions for lack of money. The agency markup consumed the equivalent of dozens more.

### The trap: why the dependency persists

The staffing agencies exist because the shortage exists. The regional scarcity of special education teachers, SLPs, and related specialists is widely documented. Vancouver's HR department has been operating inside a genuinely constrained labor market. This investigation does not dispute that.

But shortage explains why the district buys contracted labor. It does not explain why the district buys contracted labor under a structure that costs millions more than internal staffing. Four forces interact to create what amounts to a systemic trap.

Market failure: SPED teachers and specialists are scarce. Vacancies occur. An NCTQ analysis of 148 large districts found only six offer SPED teachers additional pay exceeding $5,000 per year, and that over 90% pay more for master's degrees than for filling special education vacancies.

State underinvestment: fully loaded internal staffing is expensive. SEBB benefit mandates, pension contributions, and payroll taxes add a significant second layer to every direct hire. Washington's Joint Legislative Audit and Review Committee found districts paid an estimated 31-44% more per month for health benefits under SEBB.

Incentive misalignment: a district can reduce headcount on payroll and still sustain service delivery by purchasing labor through Object 7. That is not a conspiracy claim. It is an accounting fact. Object 7 is "Purchased Services," not "Salaries." In austerity, districts face pressure to cut salary lines. Object 7 becomes the pressure-release valve.

IDEA compliance: special education is federally mandated. When a student's IEP requires services, the district cannot legally respond with "we tried to hire." Federal regulations are explicit: even if a private entity delivers services, compliance responsibility remains with the school district. Contracting with a staffing firm after postings go unfilled is easier to defend as "reasonable steps" than leaving services uncovered.

Under those conditions, the premium is not an anomaly. It becomes the price of staying afloat.

The public question is whether the district treated that premium as an unavoidable emergency cost or as a normalized operating model.

### Is Vancouver an outlier?

To test whether Vancouver is unusually dependent on purchased services, a budget-to-budget comparison against neighboring Evergreen School District isolates what each district planned to spend before the year unfolded.

Evergreen budgeted $47.1 million for Object 7 in FY24-25, or $2,161 per pupil, representing 10.89% of its general fund. Vancouver budgeted $36.7 million, or $1,766 per pupil, representing 8.67%.

On paper, Vancouver budgeted about $395 less per pupil for purchased services than Evergreen.

But Vancouver's actual Object 7 spending reached $47.3 million, exceeding its own budgeted ceiling by $10.6 million. That overspend is larger than the entire $13.3 million in staffing vendor payments, a comparison that underscores scale without proving causation.

Vancouver did not budget big. Its Object 7 spending blew past its own ceiling.

### Who was watching?

The staffing vendor payments occurred against the backdrop of a district whose cash reserves had been drawn down to near zero.

Three consecutive years of emergency borrowing: $15.5 million in 2023, $18.5 million in 2024, $21.4 million requested in 2025, of which only $8.7 million was approved by OSPI, the largest single request the agency has ever received from any Washington district.

When the state said no to the full amount, the board authorized borrowing from Clark County Treasurer Alishia Topper through interest-bearing registered warrants. Topper agreed but delivered a pointed warning: county treasurers are not designed to provide ongoing operating loans to school districts, she said, and the district's own decisions contributed to the situation.

The fund balance, once $7 million, was drawn to $195,180 when the board approved the 2025-26 budget, functionally zero for a district of this size.

Brett Blechschmidt, the district's chief financial officer during the entire period of vendor spending growth, was appointed interim superintendent when Jeff Snell departed in May 2025. Blechschmidt now holds authority over both the budget and the organizational response to questions about the budget. He agreed from the outset not to seek the permanent role.

Jeff Fish, the district's executive director of human resources since spring 2022, oversees the five-person department responsible for recruiting a workforce of roughly 2,500. His department's recruitment outcomes are central to the investigation: every unfilled position becomes an additional billing opportunity for the vendor pipeline.

This investigation found zero media quotes, zero attributed board presentations, and zero public statements from Fish across four years of local news coverage. Coverage consistently attributes staffing and bargaining commentary to the communications director or the superintendent.

The Board of Directors voted unanimously on the $35 million reduction package. Board materials contained at least one agenda item identifying Amergis as "formerly Maxim." But the board never publicly debated whether a vendor relationship that had grown from $349,709 to $13.3 million warranted independent procurement scrutiny. Five revisions to the procurement policy during the spending growth period added no staffing-specific oversight.

The State Auditor's Office issued audit findings in 2021 and 2024, breaking a 13-year clean streak. VPS has received OSPI financial health warnings for two consecutive years. It remains the only Clark County district under financial warning.

### What the churn does to classrooms

The compliance record tells the story the spreadsheets cannot.

Beyond the Bailey case, OSPI citizen complaint decision SECC 21-32 found the district delivered 960 of 1,575 weekly special education minutes required by a student's December 2020 IEP, a deficit of 615 minutes per week. The district admitted there was not a "concerted plan" to supplement instruction when staffing fell short. The parent reported that substitute teachers made it difficult to address the student's needs, causing confusion because of the number of substitutes. Six of twelve IEP goals showed no progress.

Washington banned prone and supine restraint in September 2021. OPB and InvestigateWest later reported prohibited restraint tactics continued after that ban. As recently as 2022-23, the district recorded 776 restraints.

The research record has moved beyond correlation. A 2025 CALDER working paper using Washington State data provides the first causal estimates showing special education teacher turnover has an outsized negative impact on test scores for students with disabilities specifically. The U.S. Government Accountability Office reported that teachers and staff who used seclusion and restraint in abusive ways had more often not been properly trained.

In September 2025, the classified staff union representing paraeducators authorized a strike by roughly 80% of its members, with reporting describing workforce churn, training gaps, staffing levels, and employee injuries as central grievances. A tentative agreement was reached on September 15, averting a walkout.

The VEA filed a formal grievance in June 2025 alleging "secret" staffing cuts affecting 20 additional positions beyond the original 262. A separate VEA grievance documented core high school classes exceeding 40 students and middle school ELA classes topping 42, costing the district $2.75 million in contractual overload payments in a single year.

On March 4, 2026, approximately half of Fort Vancouver High School's teachers walked out of a staff meeting over safety concerns tied to an anonymous social media account posting threatening content. Teachers stated that "the lack of appropriate action from either school or district administration in unsafe situations make us feel unsafe in this school."

The death-spiral dynamic is documented in national research. ASHA's 2024 Schools Survey found school-based SLPs reported actual caseloads 25% above manageable levels. A peer-reviewed study of 490 special educators found over 60% scored at "dangerous" levels on the emotional exhaustion scale; about 38% met clinical criteria for generalized anxiety disorder. Washington State University's Josh Taylor told the Washington State Standard: "Districts hire teachers without special education certifications because turnover and vacancies are so high. That becomes this nasty, cyclical problem."

### The cascading crises of 2026

The fiscal distress has not abated. If anything, it has compounded.

The projected deficit for 2026-27 stands at $20.15 million. On March 9, 2026, VPS disclosed $4.8 million in settlements resolving two sexual abuse lawsuits filed by former students. The settlement's impact on the already strained budget has not been publicly detailed.

The U.S. Department of Education opened a Title IX investigation into the district in January 2026 over its policy on transgender student athletes. Federal funding could be at risk.

More than 85% of VPS's budget goes to staffing, leaving almost no room for non-personnel cuts. The district closed Flex Academy, an alternative high school serving 86 students, more than half with IEPs, prompting another student walkout in December 2025.

A critical but underreported factor may be shielding VPS from formal state takeover: a temporary legislative rule exempting districts from OSPI's binding conditions framework for 2024-25 and 2025-26 when interfund loan proceeds mask deficit fund balances. If that exemption expires and VPS cannot produce a balanced 2026-27 budget, the consequences escalate.

The 2025 legislative session brought partial relief. SB 5263, signed in May 2025, eliminates the 16% enrollment cap on special education funding, a provision that had forced districts to absorb costs for every student above that threshold out of general funds. For VPS, officials estimated an additional $850,000 annually, a meaningful but modest dent in a district that spends roughly $5 million in local levy dollars on mandated special education services. ESHB 2049 expanded local levy authority by $500 per pupil. Both represent genuine progress on structural underfunding. Neither comes close to closing the gap.

### What comes next

Dr. Ricardo "Rocky" Torres-Morales begins as superintendent on July 1, 2026. He started his career as a bilingual special education teacher in New York City. He holds a doctorate in urban education. The board approved his three-year contract at $305,000 annually.

He inherits a contractor pipeline that consumed roughly $32 million over five years, a compliance record with documented service-minute failures and restraint crises, and a state funding environment that has, at least on paper, widened the possibility of hiring permanent staff.

Other districts have found exit strategies. San Francisco USD's February 2026 tentative agreement with its teachers' union explicitly committed to reducing reliance on non-union contractors while setting caseload goals and increasing paraeducator pay. A state fact-finding report for Oakland USD crystallized the financial logic: "When you contract out that position, you're actually costing the district nearly double the amount of money." Hawaii implemented a $10,000 annual special education differential in 2020; research found it reduced the proportion of vacant SPED positions by 32% relative to general education. Chicago's residency model trains roughly 200 teachers per year by recruiting current classroom aides into teaching pathways.

The tools exist. The question for Torres-Morales and for the board is whether they will use them.

The stakes are not a spreadsheet. They are a child in a quiet room, waiting for the adults in the building to have enough training, enough support, and enough stability to do the job safely.

### How we know what we know

Every dollar figure in this investigation is derived from primary source documents. Vendor payment totals are extracted from 25,578 unique, deduplicated payment records compiled from 153 warrant register PDFs obtained via the VPS BoardDocs portal. Fiscal year bucketing follows the September 1 through August 31 cycle. The deduplication audit of February 23, 2026 identified and purged 29,613 duplicate records caused by multi-vendor warrant register overlaps.

Object 7 budget figures are sourced from OSPI F-195 reports. Contract terms are quoted verbatim from the 2021 Maxim Healthcare Master Service Agreement obtained from BoardDocs. Cost comparisons use VEA and VAESP salary schedules, SEBB employer contribution rates, DRS pension rates, and the Maxim Attachment A bill rates. The Maxim fraud settlement is documented in the U.S. Department of Justice press release dated October 7, 2011. Personnel data is drawn from OSPI S-275 reports. The Colin Bailey case relies exclusively on published OPB and InvestigateWest reporting.

All claims are independently verifiable by any journalist starting from public sources through a multi-layer verification process including independent recalculation, adversarial review, and manual spot-checks against original documents.

This investigation does not allege fraud, corruption, or criminal conduct by any VPS official. It does not claim the staffing contracts were illegal. The RCW 28A.335.190 exemption makes them technically lawful. It does not claim personal motive or intent. It documents a crisis of institutional inertia, incentive misalignment, and governance failure, and it asks whether the public was told the full story about where their money went during the worst budget crisis in the district's history.

Right-of-reply questions were submitted to all relevant parties in late February 2026. Responses, when received, will be incorporated.
