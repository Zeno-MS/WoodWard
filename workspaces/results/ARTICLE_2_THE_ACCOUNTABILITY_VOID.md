# THE ACCOUNTABILITY VOID

## How a $32 Million Vendor Relationship Grew Without Competitive Bidding, Without Separate Board Debate, and Without a Single Emergency Declaration

*By WoodWard | Project WoodWard — Investigative Series, Part 2 of 3*

---

## I. THE LEDE

In Part 1 of this series, *The Austerity Paradox*, this investigation documented the financial trajectory: Vancouver Public Schools' payments to private staffing agencies grew from $349,709 in fiscal year 2020-21 to $13,326,622 in fiscal year 2024-25 — the same year the district eliminated more than 260 positions (headcount, not FTE), citing a $35 million structural deficit. **[PROOF — staffing figures: verified payment records, canonical table; position eliminations and deficit: VPS Board of Directors public budget presentation, 2024]**

That spending consumed **28.16%** of the district's entire Object 7 (Purchased Services) actual expenditures by the austerity year. In 2020-21, the figure was **1.17%**. **[PROOF — verified payment records and OSPI F-195 budget data, actual/actual methodology]**

> *FY 2021-22 staffing share is calculated against budgeted purchased services because audited actual expenditures are not available in the F-195 series for that year. All other years use actual expenditures for both numerator and denominator.* **[MANDATORY DISCLOSURE]**

But dollar amounts describe only the scale. They do not explain how. They do not explain why no one asked the questions that public stewardship demands.

A $32 million cumulative vendor relationship does not build itself. It requires contracts. It requires approvals. It requires — or, in this case, lacks — oversight. **[PROOF — $32,189,236 grand total, verified payment records]**

This is the story of the governance that wasn't.

## II. THE CONSENT MECHANISM

Washington State's public school boards approve routine administrative business through a mechanism called the Consent Agenda — a block of items voted on collectively, typically without individual discussion. The Consent Agenda is a legitimate governance tool. It exists to prevent boards from spending hours debating supply orders and bus surplus declarations.

It is also the mechanism through which Vancouver Public Schools authorized the single largest vendor relationship in the district's purchased services budget.

This investigation reviewed the full text of all publicly available VPS board meeting agendas, minutes, and committee records housed on the district's BoardDocs portal, spanning August 2020 through August 2025. The review identified **at least 17 distinct board agenda items** across four staffing vendors — Maxim Healthcare, Amergis Healthcare, Soliant Health, and ProCare Therapy. **[PROOF — BoardDocs review, 204 meetings, 1,852 agenda items]**

The governance pattern is documented in the district's own records:

In **earlier board actions** available through BoardDocs, staffing vendor contracts appeared as **Action Items** — the board's formal mechanism for items requiring individual discussion and separate votes. The contract values at that time were modest: board materials from the period describe agreements serving three to five students, with estimated annual costs of approximately $220,000 per approval. **[PROOF — BoardDocs agenda item text]**

By **2024**, as the vendor relationship approached $13 million annually, the approval mechanism had shifted. On July 9, 2024, the board approved the Amergis staffing contract for the 2024-25 school year via the **Consent Agenda**. On the same consent vote, the board also approved a CrowdStrike endpoint security software purchase and a school mascot selection for Vancouver Innovation, Technology & Arts. **[PROOF — BoardDocs meeting record, July 9, 2024]**

The board did acknowledge the corporate continuity. The June 18, 2024 Committee of the Whole agenda explicitly identified the vendor as "Amergis Education (formerly Maxim Healthcare)." **[PROOF — BoardDocs agenda item title, verbatim]**

What the board did not do — in any publicly available meeting record this investigation reviewed — was separately debate whether a vendor relationship that had grown from $349,709 to $13.3 million warranted independent procurement scrutiny. The approvals accumulated. The consent agenda counted the votes. And the spending grew. **[STRONG INFERENCE — based on exhaustive review of 204 meetings; absence of evidence in public record]**

## III. THE CONTRACT ARCHITECTURE

The scale of the spending is documented. The contract that authorized it explains how the scale became structurally inevitable.

The 2021 Maxim Healthcare Master Service Agreement — obtained from board packet attachments on the VPS BoardDocs portal — contains provisions that a procurement specialist would recognize as a vendor-controlled pricing architecture. **[PROOF — Primary Source: 21-22 Maxim Healthcare Contract, BoardDocs]**

**Section 1.1 — Auto-Renewal:**

> *"This Agreement will be in effect for one (1) EDUCATIONAL INSTITUTION calendar year and will be automatically renewed at the end of the first year and each subsequent year unless terminated."*

**[PROOF — Primary Source, Section 1.1, 21-22 Maxim Healthcare Contract]**

**Section 1.2 — Termination:** The 2021 agreement requires **90 days' advance written notice** from either party to terminate. **[PROOF — Primary Source, Section 1.2]**

**Section 5.4 — Rate Change:** Maxim retained the right to change rates with only 30 days' notice, with no stated ceiling on such changes. **[PROOF — Primary Source, Section 5.4]**

**Section 5.5 — Annual Rate Increases:** The district pre-agreed to automatic annual rate increases compounding annually, with no expiration. **[PROOF — Primary Source, Section 5.5]**

The contract contains no maximum annual expenditure cap. No "not to exceed" clause governs the total volume of staffing the district may request — or that the vendor may bill for. **[PROOF — Primary Source, full contract text]**

### The Placement Fee

If the district sought to convert an agency position to a direct hire — bringing a contracted worker onto the district payroll — the contract imposed a financial penalty:

> *"If [the District] hires Provider Personnel within 12 months... [the District agrees] to pay Maxim a placement fee equal to the greater of: five thousand dollars ($5,000) or the sum of thirty percent (30%) of such personnel's annualized salary (calculated as weekday hourly bill rate x 1,440 hours x 30%)."*

**[PROOF — Primary Source, Section 1.1, 21-22 Maxim Healthcare Contract]**

For a school psychologist billed at $100/hour, the placement fee would exceed **$43,000** — a cost imposed on the district for the act of hiring an employee directly rather than continuing to pay through the agency pipeline. **[PROOF — mathematical derivation from contract formula: $100 × 1,440 × 0.30 = $43,200]**

### Rate Escalation: 2021 to 2024

The rate schedule attached to the 2021 Maxim agreement specified hourly rates ranging from $37-$45 for paraprofessionals to $85-$120 for school psychologists and BCBAs. **[PROOF — Maxim Attachment A, 2021]**

By 2024, the board recommendation for the Amergis contract specified hourly rates ranging from **$45 to $180** — a 50% increase at the top end for the highest-billed roles. **[PROOF — BoardDocs agenda item text, July 9, 2024]**

## IV. THE $3 MILLION ESTIMATE

The BoardDocs agenda item for the 2024-25 Amergis contract contains a detail that distills the governance question into a single data point.

The board recommendation states, verbatim:

> *"Approximate Cost to District: $3,000,000 for the 24-25 SY"*

The same recommendation states that Amergis Education Staffing Services would provide services for **11 identified students**. **[PROOF — BoardDocs agenda item text, July 9, 2024]**

Board materials estimated an approximate annual cost of $3 million for Amergis staffing, justified by the needs of 11 identified students — though agency staff serve broader caseloads district-wide. The 11 students cited are the most intensive IEP cases requiring dedicated one-on-one staffing, such as full-time nursing or behavioral support. Roles like SLP, OT, and school psychologist typically carry broader caseloads serving many additional students. **[PROOF — board filing verbatim + [STRONG INFERENCE] on caseload context from role descriptions]**

The actual payments to Amergis in FY24-25 totaled **$10,970,973** — more than three times the cost estimate presented to the board. The $3 million figure was described as an "Approximate Cost," not a contractual spending cap — and the underlying contract, as documented in Section III, contained no "not to exceed" clause. The gap may reflect expanded service needs during the school year, as new IEP referrals and mid-year student enrollments can increase required staffing levels. The governance question is not whether needs grew, but whether any mechanism existed for the board to review and authorize such a significant expansion beyond the presented estimate. **[PROOF — verified payment records; STRONG INFERENCE — mid-year service expansion]**

The full Amergis Master Service Agreement for 2024-25 was never publicly attached to BoardDocs. The board approved the contract based on a summary description. The full contract terms — including any auto-renewal, placement fee, or indemnification clauses — were not made publicly available. **[PROOF — negative finding from exhaustive review of BoardDocs attachments]**

## V. THE PROCUREMENT GAP

State law requires competitive bidding for certain categories of public school purchases. Professional staffing contracts are not among them.

RCW 28A.335.190 mandates competitive bidding for "furniture, supplies, equipment, building, improvements, or repairs" exceeding $75,000. For purchases exceeding $40,000, the statute requires awards on a "competitive basis." Section 6 provides an emergency waiver mechanism — but only where the board passes a formal resolution declaring "a condition likely to result in immediate physical injury to persons or to property." **[PROOF — RCW 28A.335.190, certified 7/12/2024]**

Professional staffing services — nurses, therapists, psychologists, paraprofessionals — are not enumerated in the statute. **[PROOF — statutory text analysis]**

A critical distinction must be acknowledged: many of these staffing positions exist because federal law demands them. The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) mandates that school districts provide special education services to every student with an Individualized Education Plan, regardless of whether the district can recruit the necessary personnel directly. When a student's IEP requires a speech-language pathologist, a behavior technician, or a dedicated nurse, the district must provide that professional or face federal compliance action. In a regional labor market where certified SLPs and BCBAs are in critical shortage, agencies are often the available option — not a preference, but a compliance mechanism. **[PROOF — IDEA statutory requirement; STRONG INFERENCE on labor market conditions]**

The federal mandate, however, does not dictate the *procurement terms* under which those services are acquired. The question is not whether the district needed outside staffing. The question is whether the governance and procurement architecture that governed a $28 million vendor relationship was adequate to the scale of the spending.

State law requires competitive bidding for equipment and supplies purchases exceeding $75,000. Professional staffing contracts fall outside that mandate. Vancouver Public Schools chose not to seek competitive bids on a vendor relationship that has accumulated more than $28 million in payments since FY20-21 — a decision permitted by state policy but not required by it. **[PROOF — RCW 28A.335.190 and VPS Board Policy 6220]**

### No Emergency Waivers Declared

This investigation conducted an exhaustive search of 5,367 embedded text chunks from board packets, contract documents, meeting minutes, and warrant registers — for any evidence that the district invoked the RCW 28A.335.190(6) emergency waiver for staffing procurement. **[PROOF — document corpus search]**

| Search Term | Matches | Finding |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| "emergency waiver" | 5 chunks | All relate to COVID-19 graduation credit waivers, not procurement |
| "emergency" + "staffing" | 7 chunks | All inside Maxim contract language (health/safety protocols) |
| "emergency" + "amergis" | 0 | No matches |
| "board resolution" + "staffing" | 0 | No matches |
| "emergency declaration" | 0 | No matches |

The district never invoked the emergency waiver for staffing procurement. The professional services exemption — codified in VPS Board Policy 6220 — was the sole mechanism used throughout. **[PROOF — negative finding from exhaustive search]**

### Policy 6220: Five Revisions, No Change

VPS Board Policy 6220 allows the district to waive bid requirements for purchases:

1. Clearly and legitimately limited to a single source of supply
2. Involving special facilities or market conditions
3. In the event of an emergency
4. Of insurance or bonds

Fourteen text sections across **five separate P6220 draft revisions** (2021, 2022, 2023, 2024, 2025) confirm this waiver language has remained consistent. The district revised its procurement policy at least five times during the period when staffing vendor payments grew from $349,709 to $13.3 million — without adding competitive bidding requirements for professional staffing services. **[PROOF — document corpus search, 14 sections across 5 drafts]**

## VI. THE CASH FLOW CRISIS

The staffing vendor payments did not exist in isolation. They occurred against the backdrop of a district whose cash reserves had been drawn down to the point of requiring emergency borrowing — a mechanism that, under Washington state rules, can trigger state oversight of a district's finances.

Board records document the district's use of emergency cash-flow mechanisms for three consecutive fiscal years: **[PROOF — BoardDocs resolutions and agenda items]**

| Date | Mechanism | Category |
|------|-----------|----------|
| July 11, 2023 | Resolution #906 — Short-Term Interfund Loan | Consent Agenda |
| June 4, 2024 | Registered Warrants (Clark County Treasurer) | Regular Business |
| August 13, 2024 | Resolution #924 — Registered Warrants | Consent Agenda |
| June 3, 2025 | Registered Warrants | Consent Agenda |
| June 26, 2025 | Resolution #934 — Interfund Loan (Capital Projects → General Fund) | Regular Business |

The district's own rationale, stated verbatim in all three warrant authorization items:

> *"Given our strategic drawdown of cash reserves we are no longer able to mitigate the discrepancies between our normal expenditure patterns and the timing of our most significant state and local revenue collections."*

**[PROOF — BoardDocs agenda item rationale text, verbatim]**

In the 2024-25 fiscal year, the district required registered warrants from the Clark County Treasurer to meet payroll obligations — while simultaneously processing $13.3 million in payments to private staffing vendors. **[PROOF — timeline overlap documented in BoardDocs resolutions and verified payment records]**

Amergis Healthcare Staffing and Maxim Healthcare Services are the same corporate entity through a rebrand. Combined, they received **$28,074,510** — 87.2% of all staffing vendor payments — across five fiscal years. Records reviewed show no overlap between Maxim and Amergis warrant numbers; the warrant numbering is sequential and chronologically ordered across fiscal years. **[PROOF — cross-vendor warrant verification, 10/10 sample distinct, manual PDF spot-checks exact match]**

The corporate history of the district's dominant vendor is a matter of public record. On October 7, 2011, the U.S. Department of Justice announced that Maxim Healthcare Services, Inc. had agreed to pay $150 million to resolve criminal charges and related civil claims arising from a federal investigation into systematic Medicaid fraud — including allegations of billing for home health nursing services that were never performed and paying illegal kickbacks to patient referral sources. The resolution included a guilty plea by the company. Maxim subsequently rebranded its staffing division as Amergis. *(Source: U.S. Department of Justice, Office of Public Affairs, Press Release, October 7, 2011)* **[PROOF — DOJ public record]**

This investigation raises no allegation that the conduct underlying the 2011 settlement is connected to VPS's staffing contracts. The relevance is one of vendor due diligence: when a school district's single largest private contractor carries a criminal fraud conviction on its corporate record, the question of whether the board was formally briefed on that history is a legitimate governance inquiry. **[STRONG INFERENCE — governance due diligence question]**

ProCare Therapy, at $2.9 million over the same period, is the third-largest staffing vendor. Its presence confirms that the district's reliance on external staffing was not limited to a single vendor relationship — it was systemic. **[PROOF — verified payment records]**

## VIII. THE AUSTERITY CONTRADICTION

During the same period that staffing vendor payments consumed an increasing share of Object 7, the district's budgeted Object 7 total itself was growing — even as the district declared austerity.

Budgeted Object 7 spending rose from $24,288,629 in FY20-21 to $36,738,206 in FY24-25 — a **51.2% increase** during the austerity cycle. Object 7 encompasses all purchased services — not only staffing vendors, but also utilities, transportation contracts, technology services, professional development, and facilities maintenance. The 51.2% growth reflects the category as a whole, not solely staffing agency payments. The staffing-specific trajectory is documented in the canonical table above. **[PROOF — F-195 budget data]**

The district eliminated over 260 positions, citing a $35 million structural deficit. During that same period, its purchased services budget grew by more than $12 million. The staffing vendor payments that consumed 28.16% of Object 7 actual expenditures were not a static category absorbing a fixed share of a stable budget. They were a rapidly growing category within a rapidly growing budget line — in a year the district described as a fiscal emergency.

## X. A NOTE ON WHAT THIS INVESTIGATION DOES NOT CLAIM

This investigation does not claim that anyone in the district's administration acted with corrupt intent. It does not claim that the staffing vendor contracts were illegal. It does not claim that the consent agenda was used to deliberately conceal spending. It does not claim that the board was unaware of the vendor relationships — the record shows they were.

What it documents is a governance gap: a $32 million cumulative vendor relationship that grew without competitive bidding, without a formal board debate on vendor concentration, without a single emergency procurement declaration, and without the full contract terms being publicly available — all during a period when the district was borrowing emergency funds to make payroll and eliminating hundreds of classroom positions.

The public asks who is accountable when a school district in declared fiscal emergency routes $13.3 million to private staffing vendors in a single year. The current governance structure does not provide a clear answer.

## METHODOLOGY

Every dollar figure in this report is derived from primary source documents and has been verified through a five-layer verification architecture:

1. **Payment database** — 25,578 unique deduped warrant register payment records extracted from VPS Board Warrant Register PDFs, FY20-21 through FY25-26 (partial). Fiscal year bucketing by entry date (Sept 1 – Aug 31).
2. **OSPI F-195 Budget Reports** — Object 7 denominators extracted with explicit BUDGETED/SPENT labeling.
3. **Arithmetic verification** — Independent recalculation of all shares and totals by adversarial reviewer.
4. **Structural verification** — Cross-vendor warrant number overlap testing (76/76 distinct in FY24-25) and cross-period warrant sequence analysis (10/10 random sample, non-overlapping chronological series).
5. **Primary document spot-checks** — Manual visual confirmation of specific warrant records against source PDF documents (Maxim warrant #405802 and Amergis warrant #412835 both exact matches).

Contract terms (Sections 1.1, 1.2, 5.4, 5.5, and Attachment A) are quoted verbatim from the 2021 Maxim Healthcare Staffing Agreement obtained from VPS BoardDocs.

All figures were locked on February 25, 2026 and independently verified on February 26, 2026.

No claim in this article relies on anonymous sources, internal documents not available through public channels, or data that cannot be independently verified by any competent journalist starting from public sources.
