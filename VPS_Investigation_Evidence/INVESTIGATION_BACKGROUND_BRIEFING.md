# INVESTIGATION BACKGROUND BRIEFING
*Vancouver Public Schools Investigation — Confidential Handoff Document*

## 1. The District at a Glance
- **Entity:** Vancouver Public Schools, District #37, Clark County, Washington
- **Scale:** ~22,000 students, ~2,500 FTE staff, ~$324M annual budget (FY24-25 General Fund expenditures)
- **Financial Status:**
  - Only Clark County district to receive a state financial warning
  - Only district to require emergency borrowing for three consecutive years
  - Largest OSPI apportionment advance request ever received from any WA district ($21.4M requested, $8.7M approved)
  - Fund balance: from ~$7M to $195,180
  - Projected deficit for FY26-27: $20.15M

## 2. The Crisis Timeline (2014–Present)
1. **2014:** VPS enrollment ~23,065; staffing ~2,225 FTE.
2. **2016:** Original Maxim Healthcare staffing contract executed (30-day termination clause).
3. **2020-21:** Maxim payments total $320,529 (modest, serving ~3-5 students).
4. **2021 (Feb):** Steve Webb placed on administrative leave; receives $455K separation payout with mutual non-disparagement clause.
5. **2021 (May 5):** New Maxim Master Service Agreement signed — 90-day termination clause, auto-renewal, uncapped rate increases with 30-day notice, 30% conversion fee penalizing direct hiring.
6. **2021 (July 13):** Board approves Maxim MSA on consent agenda without separate debate.
7. **2021:** Washington State Auditor's Office (SAO) issues audit finding — first in 13 years — regarding procurement controls.
8. **2022:** Jeff Fish hired as Executive Director of Human Resources.
9. **2022:** Maxim rebrands its staffing division as Amergis Healthcare Staffing.
10. **2022-23:** Maxim payments reach $5.9M (1,743% increase from 2020-21 baseline).
11. **2023:** VPS requires first emergency apportionment advance from OSPI ($15.5M).
12. **2023-24:** Payments to both Maxim ($4.2M) and Amergis ($1.85M) — transition year as contracts shift to the rebranded entity.
13. **2024 (March 12):** Board of Directors votes unanimously on $35M in reductions (~260 positions).
14. **2024 (Spring):** Students at Fort Vancouver High School walk out in protest of staffing cuts.
15. **2024:** SAO issues second audit finding in four years — breaking the 13-year clean streak definitively.
16. **2024:** VPS requests second emergency advance ($18.5M).
17. **2024 (April/May):** Reduction In Force (RIF) notices issued. Total staff count drops from 2,736.83 FTE to 2,519.90 FTE (−216.93 FTE).
18. **2024-25 Academic Year:** Despite cutting 217 FTEs, VPS expenditures on external staffing vendors reach $13.3M. Of this, $10.97M flows to Amergis alone. Object 7 actual spending exceeds budget by $10.59M ($47.3M actual vs. $36.7M budgeted).
19. **2025 (May):** Superintendent Jeff Snell announces departure for WASA. Brett Blechschmidt appointed Interim Superintendent.
20. **2025 (June 3):** VEA files formal grievance challenging "secret" staffing cuts affecting 20+ additional positions beyond the original 262.
21. **2025 (August):** Board approves budget drawing fund balance down to $195,180.
22. **2025 (September 15):** VAESP classified staff authorize strike by ~80% vote; tentative agreement averts walkout.
23. **2025 (Fall):** Third emergency apportionment advance request ($21.4M) — only $8.7M approved by OSPI (largest single request in state history).
24. **2025 (December 9):** Board authorizes borrowing from Clark County Treasurer via registered warrants. Treasurer Alishia Topper issues public warning about the borrowing pattern.
25. **2025 (December 18):** Flex Academy students walk out to protest school closure.
26. **2026 (January):** U.S. Department of Education Office for Civil Rights opens Title IX investigation into VPS.
27. **2026 (January 21):** Torres-Morales selected as incoming superintendent (starts July 1, 2026).

## 3. Core Findings (One Paragraph Each)

1. **The Spending Trajectory:** From $349,709 in FY2020-21 to $13,326,622 in FY2024-25 — a 3,711% increase in five years. Cumulative five-year total: $32,189,236 paid to private staffing agencies.

2. **Vendor Concentration:** Amergis Healthcare ($15.7M all-time) and its corporate sibling Maxim Healthcare ($12.3M) together captured 87.2% of all staffing vendor payments — $28.07M of the $32.2M total. This represents a purchasing monoculture unprecedented in comparable Washington school districts.

3. **The Corporate Fraud History:** Maxim Healthcare Services entered a federal criminal guilty plea in 2011 and paid $150 million to resolve DOJ charges of systematic Medicaid fraud — billing for nursing services never performed and paying illegal kickbacks. Multiple executives were individually prosecuted. VPS signed a new Master Service Agreement with Maxim just ten years after this settlement.

4. **The Contract Architecture:** The May 2021 Maxim MSA contains provisions structurally disfavoring the district: auto-renewal (annual, unless 90-day notice), uncapped rate increases (30-day notice, no ceiling), no maximum annual expenditure cap, and a 30% conversion fee penalizing the district for directly hiring any worker placed by the vendor.

5. **The Governance Gap:** Contracts were approved through consent agendas without competitive bidding or separate board debate. The board approved a "$3,000,000 approximate cost" estimate for Amergis in one year; actual payments exceeded $10.9M to the same vendor in the same year. Five revisions to the district's procurement policy during the spending growth period added no staffing-specific oversight mechanism.

6. **The Salary-Tier Asymmetry:** The district publicly claimed "the highest percentage" of austerity cuts fell on Central Office (Location 1014 contracted by 10.13%). S-275 data reveals the reductions were structurally concentrated among low-paid support staff (~$48K average salary). The executive tier (~$183K average) contracted moderately (6.89% of its compensation pool), preserving its architectural salary structure.

7. **The Procurement Exemption:** Under RCW 28A.335.190, Washington's competitive bidding requirements explicitly exempt "professional services" and "personal services." This statutory carve-out allowed VPS to route $32M in staffing contracts without the competitive bidding required for physical goods or construction above $75,000.

8. **The Compliance Record:** In a single year (2015-16), the district documented 1,110 physical restraints of students. OSPI SECC 21-32 found IEP service-minute failures. Post-ban violations of restraint practices were identified. These compliance failures contextualize the reliance on transient, agency-supplied staff for the district's most vulnerable students.

9. **The Cost Premium:** Internal cost models (using VEA/VAESP salary schedules, SEBB at $1,178/month, DRS at 9.11%) establish that fully-loaded direct hires cost $39-$68/hour depending on role. Vendor rates range from $45-$180/hour. At the SPED Teacher midpoint ($75/hour vendor rate vs. $61/hour internal), FY24-25 vendor spending represents ~$2.5M in "lost efficiency" — approximately 151 FTEs the district could have hired directly for the same money.

10. **The Emergency Borrowing Spiral:** Three consecutive years of emergency borrowing — $15.5M (2023), $18.5M (2024), $21.4M (2025, only $8.7M approved) — driven in significant part by uncapped vendor spending that routinely exceeded budgeted amounts. Clark County Treasurer Alishia Topper publicly warned that "county treasurers are not designed to provide ongoing operating loans to school districts."

## 4. Key Regulatory and Legal Framework
- **RCW 28A.335.190** — Competitive bidding requirements and the professional/personal services exemption
- **IDEA / 34 C.F.R. § 300.325(c)** — Non-delegable duty to provide IEP services
- **WAC 392-172A-04085** — Washington parallel state requirement for IEP services
- **WAC 392-123-060** — OSPI financial health scoring and escalation framework (binding conditions, Financial Oversight Committee)
- **RCW 42.56** — Washington Public Records Act
- **VPS Board Policy 6220** — Bid or Request for Proposal Requirements (five revisions during spending growth period; none added staffing oversight)
- **VPS Board Policy 6210** — Purchasing: Authorization and Control
- **SB 5263 (2025)** — Eliminates special education funding cap
- **HB 2049 (2025)** — Increases local levy authority

## 5. What the District Will Say (Anticipated Defenses)
1. **"We had no choice."** They will cite IDEA mandates and the labor shortage, arguing that failure to provide an SLP or RN would violate federal law and invite lawsuits. *Counter: True, but the reliance on cost-premium vendors is the result of years of failing to build internal recruitment capacity. Peer districts like Evergreen budget more realistically for Object 7, acknowledging the reality rather than operating via unplanned variance.*

2. **"This is not just our problem."** They will point to statewide underfunding, the SEBB mandate, and macro-economic workforce shortages. *Counter: True, but VPS is the only Clark County district requiring emergency borrowing, the only one with a state financial warning, and the only one budgeting Object 7 at 8.67% of General Fund when peers budget 10-13%. Object 7 includes more than staffing agencies, but staffing agencies constitute 28.16% of actual Object 7 spending.*

3. **"We cut Central Office by the highest percentage."** They will cite the 10.13% Location 1014 reduction. *Counter: Geographically true, economically deceptive. S-275 data proves the reductions were overwhelmingly concentrated in the $48K support tier (Root 40: −17.58 FTE; Root 95: −15.28 FTE), not the $183K executive tier (Root 13: −4.59 FTE). The executive compensation pool contracted only 6.89%.*

## 6. What This Investigation Does NOT Claim
- **No allegation of fraud, corruption, or criminal conduct** by any VPS official
- **No claim that staffing vendor contracts were illegal** — the RCW 28A.335.190 exemption makes them technically lawful
- **No claim that the consent agenda was used to deliberately conceal spending** — though the structural effect is the same
- **No claim about personal motive or intent** — the investigation documents governance gaps, not crimes
- The investigation documents a crisis of **institutional inertia, incentive misalignment, and governance failure** — not a conspiracy
