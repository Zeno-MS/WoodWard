# Citizen Concern: Financial Controls and Board Visibility — Vancouver Public Schools

*Submitted to the Washington State Auditor's Office*
*Regarding: Vancouver School District #37, Clark County*

---

## Summary of Concern

This submission requests that the State Auditor's Office examine whether Vancouver Public Schools maintained adequate financial controls and board-level oversight over a staffing vendor relationship that grew from $349,709 in FY2020-21 to $13,326,622 in FY2024-25 — a 3,711% increase over five fiscal years — during a period of emergency borrowing, reserve depletion, and a declared fiscal emergency.

The concern is not that vendor contracts are unlawful. The RCW 28A.335.190 personal-services exemption permits them. The concern is whether the district's internal controls, contract-management practices, and board reporting were adequate for a vendor relationship of this scale and growth rate, particularly given the district's concurrent financial distress.

---

## Specific Control Questions

### 1. Board Estimate vs. Actual Expenditure

On July 9, 2024, the VPS Board of Directors approved a Client Services Agreement with Amergis Education (formerly Maxim Healthcare) at an "approximate cost" of $3,000,000 (Consent Agenda, Item #9).

Warrant register data shows actual payments to Amergis in FY2024-25 totaled $10,970,973 — approximately 366% of the board-approved estimate.

**Control question:** Did any internal control mechanism — budget monitoring, purchasing threshold, variance reporting, or management escalation — flag this $7.97 million variance? Was additional board authorization obtained before expenditures exceeded the approved estimate?

### 2. Contract Structure and Spending Controls

The governing Master Service Agreement (signed May 2021) contains:

- Annual auto-renewal unless 90-day written notice is given
- Vendor rate increases permitted on 30-day notice with no contractual ceiling
- No maximum annual expenditure cap
- A 30% conversion fee penalizing the district for directly hiring workers placed by the vendor

**Control question:** Were these contract terms reviewed by the district's purchasing or legal staff for adequacy given the scale of expenditure? Does the district maintain any internal spending cap, purchase-order ceiling, or periodic review mechanism for contracts that lack a contractual spending limit?

### 3. Procurement and Competitive Process

No competitive bidding process was conducted for this vendor relationship. The district relies on the RCW 28A.335.190 exemption for personal and professional services. VPS Board Policy 6220 (Bid or Request for Proposal Requirements) was revised five times during the period of spending growth; none of the revisions added staffing-specific oversight provisions.

**Control question:** For a vendor relationship that reached $13.3 million in a single year, did the district evaluate whether a competitive process — even if not legally required — would have been prudent? Did any internal policy, procedure, or guideline address oversight of high-dollar personal-services contracts?

### 4. Consent Agenda Governance

From 2014 through 2019, staffing agency contracts were presented as individual Action Items requiring board discussion. The July 2024 Amergis approval was placed on the Consent Agenda — authorized without separate debate alongside a school mascot vote, a software purchase, and a facility fee schedule.

**Control question:** What criteria govern whether a multi-million-dollar contract is placed on the consent agenda versus presented as a standalone action item? Was the board provided with spending-to-date information when approving this item?

### 5. Object 7 Budget Variance

The district budgeted $36,738,206 for Object 7 (Purchased Services) in FY2024-25. Actual spending reached $47,331,056 — a $10,592,850 overage (28.8% over budget). Staffing vendors constituted 28.16% of actual Object 7 spending.

By comparison, peer Clark County districts budget Object 7 at 10.89% (Evergreen SD) and 13.49% (Battle Ground SD) of General Fund expenditures. VPS budgeted 8.67%.

**Control question:** Did the district's budget-monitoring process identify the Object 7 overage during the fiscal year? Were any mid-year budget adjustments or board notifications made? Does the pattern of underbudgeting then overspending in Object 7 reflect a systemic forecasting gap?

### 6. Emergency Borrowing and Vendor Spending Visibility

VPS required emergency apportionment advances in three consecutive years: $15.5M (2023), $18.5M (2024), and $21.4M requested / $8.7M approved (2025). The Clark County Treasurer publicly warned that "county treasurers are not designed to provide ongoing operating loans to school districts" (December 9, 2025).

**Control question:** When the district applied for emergency advances, did its submissions to OSPI and the County Treasurer include analysis of the drivers of the cash shortfall — specifically whether growth in vendor spending was identified as a contributing factor? Did any oversight body request that the district explain how a $13.3M annual vendor expenditure related to its cash-flow distress?

## What This Submission Does Not Allege

This submission does not accuse any individual of fraud, corruption, criminal conduct, or intentional concealment. It concerns **the adequacy of financial controls and board reporting** for a large, rapidly growing vendor relationship during a period of documented fiscal distress.

## Prior SAO Engagement

The SAO issued audit findings for VPS in 2021 and 2024, breaking a 13-year clean audit streak. The 2021 finding addressed procurement controls. This submission asks whether the scope of the next scheduled audit should include specific examination of staffing vendor contract management, budget variance reporting, and board-level visibility over Object 7 expenditures.
