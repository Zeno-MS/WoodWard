# EXECUTIVE SUMMARY: Vancouver Public Schools
*Finalized Investigative Findings — Vancouver Public Schools (District #37)*

### The Headline
Vancouver Public Schools paid $32 million to private staffing agencies over five years — $13.3 million in a single year — while cutting 260+ positions and declaring a $35 million fiscal emergency. The dominant vendor is the rebranded successor to a company that paid $150 million to settle federal fraud charges.

### The Numbers (Verified via OSPI State Records)
- **$13,326,622:** Total taxpayer funds paid to staffing vendors in FY24-25 alone.
- **$10,970,973:** Amount sent to a single vendor (Amergis, formerly Maxim) in FY24-25.
- **$32,189,236:** Cumulative payments to staffing vendors over five years.
- **28.16%:** The portion of the district's entire Purchased Services (Object 7) budget consumed by staffing agencies in FY24-25.
- **87.2% ($28.07M):** The combined market share captured by a single corporate entity (Maxim Healthcare / Amergis).
- **$3 million vs. $11 million:** The board approved an "approximate cost" estimate of $3M for Amergis in FY24-25; actual payments exceeded $10.9M without mid-year authorization for the overage.

### The Contract
Operating under a critical exemption in state procurement law (RCW 28A.335.190), Vancouver Public Schools routed these expenditures as "personal services," bypassing statutory competitive bidding. The Master Service Agreement features:
- **Auto-renewal:** Annually, unless 90-day notice is given.
- **Uncapped rate increases:** Vendors can raise rates with 30-day notice, with no contractual ceiling.
- **No maximum:** No contractual spending cap.
- **30% conversion fee:** Penalizing the district for directly hiring any worker placed by the vendor.
- **No competitive bidding:** Ever conducted for these mass-scale labor contracts.

### The Governance
The district authorized multi-million-dollar obligations to Amergis through obscured "Consent Agendas," preventing public, itemized debate by the Board of Directors. The board approved an initial $3M estimate, but actual payments ballooned to nearly $11 million in the same year. Despite the exploding costs, five revisions to the district's procurement policy during the growth period failed to add any staffing-specific oversight.

### The Context
Only the Clark County district to receive a state financial warning.
Three consecutive years of emergency borrowing.
Fund balance drawn to $195,180 (from $7M).
County Treasurer issued a public warning about the borrowing pattern.
