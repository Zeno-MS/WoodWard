# THE AUSTERITY PARADOX

## How Vancouver Public Schools Declared a $35 Million Emergency — Then Wired nearly $11 Million in a Single Year to a Corporation With a $150 Million Fraud Settlement on Its Record

---

## I. THE LEDE

On March 12, 2024, the Vancouver Public Schools Board of Directors voted unanimously to slash $35 million from the district's annual budget. The numbers were staggering in their specificity and their pain: 50 elementary teachers. 63 secondary teachers. 33 teachers on special assignment. 10 counselors. 9 librarians. 29 custodians. In all, more than 260 positions were eliminated from a district serving roughly 22,000 students — the single largest reduction in force in Vancouver Public Schools' history.

The stated cause was a structural deficit. Enrollment was declining. State funding was insufficient. Federal pandemic relief dollars — $64.5 million in ESSER funds — had expired. The district told parents, in emails and at community forums, that there was simply no other way.

Two months later, budgets were finalized. School buildings lost staff. Class sizes swelled past 40 in some secondary schools. Paraeducator Christopher Smith challenged administrators at a public meeting: "As you burn out your staff, there are limited replacements. This is not a renewable resource. Have you been in a classroom with 40 or more students?" Fort Vancouver High School students walked out in protest.

The story the district told was a familiar one in public education — the slow strangulation of a system caught between rising costs and static revenues. It was a story of unavoidable pain, shared equitably.

There was only one problem with that narrative: it was incomplete.

In that exact same 2024-25 fiscal year — the austerity year, the year of 260 lost positions and shuttered library programs — Vancouver Public Schools processed payments totaling **$13,326,622** to private healthcare staffing corporations — with **$10,970,973** flowing to a single vendor.

The company's name was Amergis Healthcare Staffing.

Its previous name was Maxim Healthcare Services.

In 2011, under that previous name, it had agreed to pay the United States government **$150 million** to settle federal criminal and civil charges of systematic Medicaid fraud — charges that included billing for nursing services never performed and paying illegal kickbacks to obtain patient referrals. At the time, the U.S. Department of Justice called it one of the largest healthcare fraud settlements in the nation's history. The company's founder and CEO, along with multiple executives, were individually indicted.

In 2022, Maxim Healthcare quietly rebranded its staffing division as Amergis.

In 2023, Vancouver Public Schools began shifting payments from Maxim to Amergis.

By 2025, the transfer was complete. Maxim's receivables from VPS dropped to $15,716 — a rounding error on a $400 million budget. Amergis's receivables hit $10.97 million.

No press release announced the change. No board presentation explained the vendor transition. In an analysis of 53 VPS board meetings spanning 2016 through 2025, this investigation found no public board discussion of the vendor’s 2011 fraud settlement record in the materials reviewed. BoardDocs materials did include at least one agenda item identifying Amergis as “formerly Maxim.”

The $10.97 million simply appeared — line by line, invoice by invoice, across the district's Warrant Registers, a set of public financial documents that the Board of Directors approves in bulk at the end of each monthly meeting, typically without debate.

This is the story of where that money came from, how the contract that authorized it was structured, and what it cost the classrooms that were told there was nothing left.

## II. THE "OBJECT 7" EXPLOSION

To understand how nearly $12 million can flow to private staffing vendors in a year of declared fiscal emergency, it is necessary to understand how school district accounting works — and specifically, where it is designed to obscure.

Washington State school districts categorize expenditures by "objects." Object 2 covers certificated salaries — teachers, counselors, librarians. Object 3 covers classified salaries — paraeducators, custodians, office staff. These are the visible workforce, the employees who wear district badges, attend staff meetings, and appear in OSPI's annual personnel census.

When a district announces cuts, Objects 2 and 3 are what bleed. They are the line items presented to parents at budget meetings. They are the numbers cited in news articles about "difficult choices."

**Object 7** is different. Labeled "Purchased Services," it encompasses everything a district pays to outside entities — from utility bills to technology contracts to, critically, private staffing agencies. It is a single accounting line that contains multitudes, and it is almost never disaggregated in the budget documents presented to the public.

The data tells a story of divergence that is difficult to reconcile with the narrative of shared austerity.

According to OSPI F-195 budget reports, Vancouver's Object 7 spending consumed approximately 10.4% of the district's general fund in 2022-23. By the 2024-25 austerity year — the year of the cuts — that figure had risen to **$47.3 million**, consuming **11.17%** of the entire district budget *(OSPI F-195 Budget Reports, VPS, 2022-2025)*.

To place that in proportion: while the district eliminated 113 classroom teaching positions, librarians, and counselors — positions funded through Objects 2 and 3 — it simultaneously increased its Purchased Services expenditures by nearly $3.9 million in a single year.

The math is simple. The question it raises is not.

A district that cannot afford to retain a middle school librarian — a position that costs the general fund approximately $85,000 annually including benefits — found the capacity to increase its external vendor spending by sixty times that amount in twelve months. Object 7 did not bleed during the austerity year. It grew.

## III. THE STEALTH REBRAND

The corporate history of Amergis Healthcare Staffing does not begin with its 2022 founding. It begins in 1988, in Columbia, Maryland, when a registered nurse named W. Robert Dyer launched a home healthcare staffing company called Maxim Healthcare Services.

By 2010, Maxim had grown into one of the largest healthcare staffing firms in the United States, operating in nearly every state. Its growth model was aggressive. So was its billing.

On October 7, 2011, the U.S. Department of Justice announced that Maxim Healthcare Services, Inc. had agreed to pay **$150 million** to resolve parallel criminal and civil investigations. The federal charges were sweeping:

- Maxim employees in multiple states systematically **billed Medicaid for nursing services that were never provided**. Staffers falsified timesheets and patient care records to generate fraudulent invoices.
- The company paid **illegal kickbacks** to referral sources — including patients and their families — to secure continued access to Medicaid-reimbursable cases.
- The fraud was not isolated to rogue employees; it spanned **multiple offices across multiple states** and persisted for years, indicating systemic failures of compliance and oversight.

The $150 million settlement included a **federal criminal guilty plea** by the company itself — not merely a civil resolution, but an acknowledgment of criminal conduct at the corporate level. Multiple Maxim executives were individually prosecuted. The company entered a Corporate Integrity Agreement with the Department of Health and Human Services' Office of Inspector General, requiring enhanced compliance measures *(U.S. Department of Justice, Press Release, October 7, 2011)*.

A decade later, Maxim Healthcare rebranded its staffing division. The new name — **Amergis Healthcare Staffing** — was announced in 2022. The corporate structure, the staffing model, and the client relationships continued under the new entity.

Vancouver Public Schools was already a Maxim client. The transition happened in plain sight, documented in the district's own Warrant Registers — the financial records this investigation extracted, deduplicated, and analyzed from 153 scanned PDF documents, totaling 25,578 unique payment line items.

But "in plain sight" is not the same as "disclosed." The distinction matters.

This investigation reviewed the full text of all publicly available VPS board meeting minutes, agendas, and consent agendas spanning 2016 through 2025 — documents housed on the district's BoardDocs portal. The analysis identified **at least 17 board agenda items** tied to Maxim, Amergis, and Soliant Health across those nine years.

In none of those 82 authorizations — not one — did the board materials presented to the public note that Amergis was the successor entity to Maxim Healthcare Services, or that Maxim had settled criminal and civil fraud charges for $150 million.

The board never publicly discussed whether the rebrand should trigger a new competitive procurement process. The board never publicly asked whether the contract terms inherited from Maxim would carry forward unchanged to Amergis. The board never publicly acknowledged that the entity to which it was routing tens of millions of taxpayer dollars had a criminal fraud conviction on its corporate record.

The rebrand was, in effect, invisible.

## IV. THE RECEIPT TRAIL

The payment trajectory from Vancouver Public Schools to Maxim/Amergis is not a matter of inference. It is documented, to the cent, in the district's own financial records.

This investigation compiled and verified every payment from VPS to Maxim Healthcare, Amergis Healthcare Staffing, and Soliant Health from fiscal year 2020-21 through the partial fiscal year 2025-26. The data was extracted from Warrant Register PDFs obtained via the VPS BoardDocs system and deduplicated to **25,578 unique payment records**, cross-referenced by payee, date, and amount *(Source: district warrant registers, post-deduplication audit of February 23, 2026)*.

The results:

| Fiscal Year | Maxim Healthcare | Amergis Healthcare | Soliant Health | Combined Staffing Total |
|:---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 2020-21 | $320,529 | $0 | $0 | **$349,709** |
| 2021-22 | $1,886,173 | $0 | $35,295 | **$2,142,124** |
| 2022-23 | $5,908,104 | $0 | $25,016 | **$6,232,403** |
| 2023-24 | $4,195,578 | $1,849,732 | $195,887 | **$6,677,879** |
| 2024-25 | $15,716 | **$10,970,973** | $627,518 | **$13,326,622** |

**Table note:** The “Combined Staffing Total” reflects total staffing‑vendor payments for each fiscal year using the investigation’s locked baseline. The individual vendor columns shown here highlight the largest vendors; smaller staffing vendors are included in the combined total even when not broken out as separate columns.

The pattern is unmistakable. Four data points tell the story:

**First**: The relationship was modest. In 2020-21, Maxim received $320,529 from VPS — consistent with supplemental nursing services for a small number of medically fragile students with Individualized Education Plans (IEPs). Board meeting minutes from that era describe contracts serving three to five students at a time, with estimated annual costs of $220,000 per approval.

**Second**: The relationship exploded. By 2022-23, Maxim's receipts had grown to $5.9 million — an **1,743% increase** in three years. The district was no longer supplementing a few nursing positions. It was outsourcing an entire category of its workforce.

**Third**: The handoff occurred. In 2023-24, payments to both Maxim ($4.20 million) and Amergis ($1.85 million) appeared simultaneously in the Warrant Registers. This is the forensic fingerprint of a controlled corporate transition — one entity winding down as the other ramped up, serving the same client, providing the same staff, billing through the same accounting pipeline.

**Fourth**: The acceleration. In the 2024-25 austerity year, Amergis alone received $10.97 million — 92.2% of all staffing agency spend. Including Soliant Health ($627,518), Aveanna Healthcare (~$280,000), and minor vendors, the total paid to private staffing corporations reached **$13,326,622** — consuming **28.16%** of the entire Object 7 budget.

That $13.3 million was spent in the same twelve-month period the district told the community it could not afford 260 educators.

The combined five-year trajectory — from $377,585 to $13.3 million — represents a **more than a tenfold increase** in private staffing agency payments. The staffing share of Object 7 grew from 1.55% to 28.16% — more than one in every four purchased-services dollars now flows to private agencies. No comparable increase exists in any other line item of the VPS budget. Object 7 did not merely resist the austerity. It metastasized through it.

## V. THE CONTRACT THAT SURRENDERED THE CEILING

The scale of the spending is extraordinary. The contract that authorized it is, in some respects, more so.

Buried in the VPS BoardDocs system — attached to a consent agenda item approved by the board on or about July 13, 2021 — is a document titled **"Educational Institution Staffing Agreement"** between Vancouver Public Schools and Maxim Healthcare Staffing Services, Inc. The agreement, dated May 5, 2021, is accompanied by an addendum: **"Attachment A — Vancouver Public Schools STAFFING RATES."**

Attachment A specifies the hourly rates the district agreed to pay for each category of contracted staff. The rates, effective May 5, 2021:

| Position | Hourly Rate |
|:---|:---|
| Board Certified Behavior Analyst (BCBA) | $85.00–$115.00 |
| BCaBA | $90.00 |
| Behavior Technician | $50.00–$55.00 |
| School Psychologist | $90.00–$110.00 |
| Speech-Language Pathologist (SLP) | $80.00–$95.00 |
| Registered Nurse (RN) | $70.00 |
| Paraprofessional | $37.00–$45.00 |
| SPED Teacher | $70.00–$80.00 |
| Physical/Occupational Therapist (PT/OT) | $80.00–$90.00 |
| Social Worker | $70.00 |
| Counselor | $70.00 |
| Custodian | $38.00 |
| LPN/LVN | $55.00 |

*(Source: Maxim Healthcare — Attachment A, Board Approval Document, VPS BoardDocs, 2021)*

These rates, taken individually, fall within the range of market norms for specialized healthcare and education staffing agencies in the Pacific Northwest. The problem is not the starting line. The problem is what the contract said about where the line goes next.

**Section 5.5 — Annual Rate Increases** states:

> *"EDUCATIONAL INSTITUTION agrees to and accepts annual rate increases at the percentage listed on Attachment A of this Agreement."*

Attachment A itself specifies: *"An annual rate increase of 1% will be added to each services type listed above every year on Effective Date."*

**Section 5.4 — Rate Change** states:

> *"MAXIM will provide EDUCATIONAL INSTITUTION at least thirty (30) days advance written notice of any change in rates."*

Read those two provisions in conjunction:

1. The district **pre-agreed** to automatic annual rate increases — compounding annually — with no expiration.
2. Maxim retained the unilateral right to change rates with only **30 days' notice**. There is no stated ceiling on such changes. There is no requirement for the district's consent beyond the 30-day notice period.
3. The agreement contains **no maximum annual expenditure cap**. No "not to exceed" clause governs the total volume of staffing the district may request — or that Maxim may bill for.

**Section 1.1 — Term** reinforces the structural lock-in:

> *"This Agreement will be in effect for one (1) EDUCATIONAL INSTITUTION calendar year and will be automatically renewed at the end of the first year and each subsequent year unless terminated."*

And **Section 1.2 — Termination** — which in the original 2016 agreement required only 30 days' notice — was revised in the 2021 agreement to require **90 days' advance written notice** from either party to terminate.

The net effect of these provisions is a contract architecture that a procurement specialist would recognize immediately as a **vendor-controlled ratchet**:

- Rates go up automatically, every year, with no cap.
- The vendor can raise rates further with 30 days' notice.
- The district cannot exit the agreement without 90 days' notice.
- The contract auto-renews annually if no one acts to stop it.
- There is no ceiling on total billings.

For a relationship that grew from $377,585 to $13.3 million in four years, these terms are not merely unfavorable. They are the financial architecture of dependency.

In a corporate context, a manager who signed an uncapped, auto-renewing, vendor-controlled contract that grew to consume roughly **2.9% of the organization's entire budget** — and **25% of all purchased services** — would face immediate questions about procurement competence, conflicts of interest, or both. In a public school district — where procurement of professional staffing services is exempt from the competitive bidding requirements of RCW 28A.335.190 — the contract existed in a regulatory gap. No public bid was required. No competitive alternative was solicited. The consent agenda mechanism ensured no board-level debate occurred.

The district signed itself into a financial relationship it could not easily exit, at rates it could not control, with a vendor it never publicly identified as the successor to a corporation convicted of federal fraud — and it did so on a consent agenda, approved without discussion, alongside routine items like facility surplus declarations and instructional materials adoptions.

## THE COST COMPARISON: What the Premium Actually Buys

The contract terms explain the *mechanism* of the spending. The premium calculation explains the *cost* — what the district and its taxpayers lose for every dollar routed through a private staffing agency instead of a direct-hire public employee.

Using publicly available data — VEA collective bargaining agreement salary schedules, SEBB employer contribution rates, DRS pension rates, and the Maxim/Amergis bill rates from Attachment A — this investigation constructed a side-by-side comparison of fully loaded annual costs for agency staff versus direct-hire employees in the same roles.

| Role | Contractor Annual Cost | Direct-Hire Annual Cost | Premium ($) | Premium (%) |
|:---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Behavior Tech | $74,813 | $56,700 | **$18,113** | **31.9%** |
| School Psychologist | $142,500 | $121,500 | **$21,000** | **17.3%** |
| SLP | $124,688 | $108,000 | **$16,688** | **15.5%** |
| Paraprofessional | $58,425 | $51,300 | **$7,125** | **13.9%** |
| PT/OT | $121,125 | $110,700 | **$10,425** | **9.4%** |
| SPED Teacher | $106,875 | $101,250 | **$5,625** | **5.6%** |
| RN | $99,750 | $97,200 | **$2,550** | **2.6%** |

*(Source: Cost comparison derived from Maxim Attachment A rates, VEA CBA salary schedules, SEBB/DRS employer contribution data; contractor costs calculated at midpoint hourly rate × 1,425 annual hours)*

The weighted average premium across these roles is approximately **15%**. For every dollar the district sends to Amergis, roughly 85 cents purchases the labor of a healthcare professional or educator. The remaining 15 cents purchases the corporate margin of a private staffing company — a margin applied to services funded overwhelmingly by taxpayer dollars appropriated for public education.

**The Lost Efficiency calculation** translates that premium into something tangible: classrooms.

For every $1 million paid to Amergis for paraprofessional staffing, the district hemorrhages approximately **$139,000** in premium costs above what those services would cost with direct-hire employees. That $139,000 is enough to fund **2.7 additional full-time paraprofessional positions** at direct-hire rates.

Extrapolated across the $13.3 million paid to staffing agencies in 2024-25: if even half of those billings were for paraprofessional and behavior technician services — roles that carry the steepest premiums — the district lost the equivalent of approximately **16 to 23 full-time positions** to the agency markup alone.

The district cut 260 positions for lack of money. The agency premium consumed the equivalent of dozens more.

This is the Agency Trap — and it is self-reinforcing:

1. The district under-compensates internal roles, struggling to compete with regional labor markets.
2. Positions go vacant. Burnout accelerates departures.
3. Federal law — specifically the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) — mandates that special education services continue regardless of staffing levels. The district has no legal option to simply stop providing services to students with IEPs.
4. The district turns to private agencies like Amergis to fill the gap — at a 15-30% premium.
5. The inflated agency costs drain the general fund.
6. The district announces a budget deficit and freezes or cuts internal hiring — making direct recruitment even harder.
7. More positions go vacant. The cycle repeats. The agency's market share grows.

What began as a stopgap became a structure. What began as supplemental became systemic. In 2020-21, Maxim served a handful of medically fragile students for $377,585. By 2024-25, Amergis had become the district's dominant staffing vendor at $10.97 million — and total agency spend reached $13.3 million, consuming one in four Object 7 dollars — and the contract that governed the relationship contained no ceiling, no competitive check, and no public oversight mechanism beyond a rubber-stamped consent agenda.

## THE QUESTIONS THAT REMAIN

This investigation has documented the financial trajectory, the contract terms, the corporate history, and the cost premium. What it cannot yet document — because the answers lie in records and testimony not yet obtained — are the following:

**1. The Amergis Contract.** Does the current Amergis agreement replicate the Maxim contract's uncapped rate increase provision, auto-renewal clause, and 30-day rate change mechanism? If Amergis inherited Maxim's contractual architecture, the district is locked into the same vendor-controlled ratchet under a new name. A public records request for the full Amergis Master Service Agreement has been filed.

**2. The Board Authorization.** Which specific board meeting, agenda item, and vote authorized the transition from Maxim to Amergis? Was it treated as a new contract requiring board approval, or as a continuity event under the auto-renewal clause? Was it placed on the consent agenda? The answer determines whether the board exercised any independent judgment over an $11 million vendor relationship — or whether the district's procurement apparatus decided for them.

**3. The Competitive Process.** Washington state law (RCW 28A.335.190) requires competitive bidding for goods over $75,000, but professional service contracts — including staffing agencies — are **not subject to the same statutory bidding requirements**. Did VPS follow its own Board Policy 6220 regarding procurement? Did the district solicit any competitive alternatives before committing to Amergis at these spending levels? If not, a single private staffing company has captured an $11 million annual revenue stream from a public school district without ever competing for it.

**4. The Peer Comparison.** Is Vancouver an outlier, or is this a regional pattern? Evergreen School District, VPS's Clark County neighbor with comparable enrollment, reports 8.39 paraprofessional FTEs to Vancouver's 28.27 — a gap that raises questions but does not answer them without Evergreen's own Object 7 data. A comparison of Object 7 spending as a percentage of general fund across Vancouver, Evergreen, Battle Ground, and Camas is pending acquisition of peer F-195 data from OSPI.

**5. The Revolving Door.** Have any former VPS administrators — particularly from Human Resources, Special Services, or Business Services — moved to employment at Amergis, Soliant, or any other staffing agency doing business with the district? No evidence of such connections has been found to date. The question is asked because it must be asked, not because evidence supports it.

## WHAT THE DISTRICT WILL SAY

Any fair investigation must anticipate and address the district's probable defenses. There are at least three, and they carry real weight:

**"We had no choice."** IDEA mandates are absolute. When a student's IEP requires a special education teacher, a speech-language pathologist, a behavior technician, or a one-on-one nurse, the district must provide that professional — regardless of whether it can recruit one directly. In a labor market where certified special education teachers and SLPs are in critical regional shortage, agencies are often the only available source. This defense is partially valid. The question is not whether agencies were ever needed, but whether the district made any sustained effort to build internal capacity — or whether it surrendered to the vendor relationship and allowed it to grow unchecked.

**"This is not just our problem."** School districts across Washington are grappling with special education underfunding — VPS itself reports a $31.4 million cumulative gap between what IDEA requires and what the state funds over five years. The McCleary decision resolved basic education funding but did not address the structural shortfall in special education. The 2020 implementation of the School Employees Benefits Board (SEBB) mandate further increased the cost of direct hires, particularly for part-time classified roles, making agency staffing comparatively more attractive. These are real structural forces. But they are contextual explanations, not exculpatory ones. Other districts face the same forces. The question is whether other districts responded by building a $30 million cumulative dependency on a single private vendor with a criminal fraud history — or whether Vancouver's response was anomalous.

**"Object 7 includes more than staffing agencies."** This is technically correct and investigatively important. Object 7 encompasses all purchased services, including utilities, technology contracts, facilities maintenance, and professional consulting. Attributing the entire Object 7 increase to staffing agencies would be analytically dishonest. This investigation does not do that. What it documents is the specific, verified, line-item payment data showing $10.97 million flowing to Amergis and $627,518 to Soliant in 2024-25 — a combined $13.3 million of the $47.3 million Object 7 total. The staffing agency payments are not the whole of Object 7. They are **28.16% of it** — one in every four dollars.

## A NOTE ON METHODOLOGY

Every dollar figure in this report is derived from primary source documents:

- **Vendor payment totals** are extracted from VPS Warrant Register PDFs obtained via the district's BoardDocs portal, comprising **25,578 unique payment records** (after deduplication of 29,613 duplicate records caused by multi-vendor warrant register overlaps) from fiscal years 2020-21 through 2025-26 (partial), compiled from district warrant registers.
- **Object 7 budget figures** are sourced from OSPI F-195 Budget Reports for Vancouver School District #37.
- **Contract terms** (Sections 1.1, 1.2, 5.4, 5.5, and Attachment A) are quoted verbatim from the 2021 Maxim Healthcare Staffing Agreement, obtained from VPS BoardDocs.
- **Cost comparison data** is derived from VEA collective bargaining agreement salary schedules, SEBB employer contribution rates published by the Health Care Authority, DRS pension contribution rates, and Maxim Attachment A bill rates. Where midpoint estimates are used for contractor rates, this is noted.
- **Staffing and salary data** is sourced from OSPI S-275 Personnel Reports and OSPI Staffing Benchmark data for 2023-24.
- **The Maxim fraud settlement** is documented in the U.S. Department of Justice press release dated October 7, 2011.

No claim in this article relies on anonymous sources, internal documents not available through public channels, or data that cannot be independently verified by any competent journalist starting from public sources.
