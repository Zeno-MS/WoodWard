# ARTICLE 4 — THE CLASSROOM
## What contractor dependency does to students and staff

## A child inside a staffing system: the Bailey case (cold open)

A school day can begin normally and still end in a “quiet room.”

At **Hough Elementary**, an autistic student — **Colin Bailey**, mostly nonverbal — was restrained **almost daily** during the 2015–16 school year, according to OPB/InvestigateWest reporting.

On one occasion, the reporting describes Bailey being isolated in a “quiet room” for **more than two hours**.

After that year, Bailey’s mother, **Cara Bailey**, alleged the district frequently failed to provide parent notifications required after restraints, and that the district failed to provide the **one‑on‑one paraeducator** required by her son’s IEP (substituting less specific “additional adult support”).

A psychiatrist later diagnosed Bailey with **PTSD** attributed to what he experienced at school.

Those facts are not a metaphor. They are the most concrete answer to the question this article asks: when a district cannot staff consistently with trained adults, what happens in the rooms where the highest‑need students spend their day?

## The churn machine: a national workforce problem that becomes a local accountability problem

Across the country, districts increasingly rely on staffing agencies to fill special education and related service roles — a workforce churn dynamic documented by national surveys and research.

The AASA Staffing Survey reported that **over 60% of school districts** used staffing agencies to fill special education and related service positions in 2023, with agencies charging districts **$70–$125 per hour**.

That context matters, but it is not an alibi. A shortage can explain why districts reach for contractors; it does not answer whether a school board and executive leadership built controls strong enough to prevent a crisis from becoming a pipeline.

In Vancouver, the pipeline is measurable in dollars.

District warrant registers show **about $32.1 million** paid to staffing vendors over the period covered by this investigation’s warrant-register records. In FY 2024–25 alone, staffing‑vendor payments totaled **$13,326,622**.
## Vancouver’s compliance record: documented harm

The clearest evidence of “what happens to the kids” is not theoretical. It is recorded in state findings and investigative reporting.

**Restraint volume.** OPB/InvestigateWest reported Vancouver Public Schools restrained students **1,110 times** in 2016–17 — “about once an hour every school day” — and later ranked among the top districts statewide for restraint use across multiple years, including **776 restraints in 2022–23**.

**Post‑ban violations.** Washington banned prone and supine restraint in September 2021; OPB/InvestigateWest later reported prohibited restraint tactics continued after that ban.

**Service minutes not delivered.** In OSPI citizen complaint decision **SECC 21‑32**, OSPI found the district delivered **960 of 1,575 weekly special‑education minutes** required by a student’s December 2020 IEP — a deficit of **615 minutes per week** — and recorded the district’s admission that there was not a “concerted plan” to supplement in‑person instruction with remote special‑education services when staffing fell short.

The parent reported that “substitute teachers made it difficult” to address the student’s needs and caused confusion because of the number of substitutes. OSPI recorded that **six of twelve IEP goals showed no progress**.

These are not interchangeable failures. Restraint events, missed IEP minutes, and post‑ban practices are different categories of breakdown — but they share a common precondition: unstable staffing and uneven training in high‑need classrooms.

## The death spiral: how shortages consume the remaining staff (and why stability becomes a safety issue)

When staffing instability becomes normal, it does not simply create vacancies. It turns remaining staff into the training department, the coverage plan, the crisis team, and the compliance officer — on top of their jobs.

In September 2025, the classified‑staff union representing paraeducators and other frontline roles authorized a strike by roughly **80% of members** (representing **800+** staff), with reporting describing workforce churn, training gaps, staffing levels, and employee injuries as central grievances; a tentative agreement averted a strike days later. The tentative agreement was reached on **September 15, 2025**.

The significance of that moment is operational: a district that cannot keep and train paraeducators and support staff cannot reliably deliver IEP minutes, implement behavior plans, or reduce restraint events — because stability is the prerequisite for everything else.

The evidence on the weight carried by remaining staff is not abstract:

- **ASHA’s 2024 Schools Survey** found school‑based SLPs reported a median **actual caseload of 50 students** against a **manageable caseload of 40** — a **25% overload**.
- A **2023 peer‑reviewed study** of **490 special educators** (ScienceDirect) found **over 60%** scored at the “dangerous” level on the Maslach Burnout Inventory’s emotional‑exhaustion scale; about **38%** met clinical criteria for generalized anxiety disorder and **38%** for major depressive disorder.
- A University of Washington study found about **20%** of vacant teaching positions were for special education — **three times** the rate of elementary education — and Washington State University’s **Josh Taylor** told the Washington State Standard: “Districts hire teachers without special education certifications because turnover and vacancies are so high… **That becomes this nasty, cyclical problem.**”

In Vancouver, the death‑spiral dynamic entered public record when the board approved major budget cuts in March 2024. Special education paraeducator **Christopher Smith** told the board: “As you burn out your staff, there are limited replacements. **This is not a renewable resource** at this point.”

The same pattern appears in workload math. A VEA grievance alleged large classes, including core high‑school classes exceeding 40 students and middle‑school ELA classes topping 42 — costing the district **$2.75 million** in contractual overload payments in a single year.

Structural cost pressure is part of this story, too. Washington’s Joint Legislative Audit and Review Committee found districts paid an estimated **31–44% more per month** for health benefits under SEBB.

None of those pressures remove responsibility for outcomes; they explain why the outcomes compound when leadership does not build a durable staffing plan.

## What students experience when adults churn (and why the evidence is no longer theoretical)

Students with disabilities often rely on consistency for progress: predictable routines, stable relationships, and adults who know what works before behavior escalates.

A seminal study by **Sulek et al. (2017)** in *Research in Autism Spectrum Disorders* concluded that inconsistent staffing is “problematic in organisations, with the potential to impact both staff and services provided,” describing cascading effects for autistic students when adults change repeatedly.

The research record has also moved beyond correlation:

- A **2025 CALDER working paper** using Washington State data provides the first causal estimates showing special‑education teacher turnover has an **outsized negative impact on test scores for students with disabilities specifically**.
- The U.S. **Government Accountability Office (GAO)** reported in 2009 that “more often than not, teachers and staff who used seclusion and restraint in abusive ways **had not been properly trained**.”
- Courts have rejected an “impossibility of performance” defense based on inability to hire qualified personnel in special‑education compliance disputes.

Vancouver’s own OSPI record provides the most concrete local illustration: when staffing collapsed, the district did not deliver hundreds of minutes of required service per week to a single student — and OSPI recorded parent testimony describing rotating substitutes as a source of confusion and instability.

For older students, instability is not confined to special‑education classrooms. In December 2025, Vancouver announced the closure of **Flex Academy**, an alternative high school serving **86 students**, more than half with IEPs, as part of planned reductions; parents launched a GoFundMe to hire an attorney and described the change as destabilizing for students who struggle with anxiety and routine disruption.

This is the “classroom” version of the accountability problem. Contracts can become routine. Payments can become invisible. But the consequences arrive on a schedule: minutes promised, minutes delivered; staff trained, staff replaced; crises prevented, crises managed.

## After the cap repeal: new funding capacity, old outcomes 

In 2025, Washington enacted SB 5263, expanding special‑education funding capacity statewide.

The legislative mechanics are documented elsewhere in this series. The question here is narrower: after the state widened the lane for permanent hiring, did Vancouver produce a public conversion plan — a measurable strategy to replace contractor labor with trained, retained staff?

This is where systemic context sharpens governance: when a district cites structural shortages and mandates, the public response is not “okay.” The public response is: show the plan. Show the conversion targets. Show the timeline.

## Leadership churn: why turnover matters for students

Special Services leadership changed repeatedly over the last seven years.

The consequence matters more than the names: turnover at the top weakens training continuity, service‑minute tracking, and the feedback loops that tell leadership when classrooms are failing. In special education, instability is not an inconvenience; it becomes a safety risk.

## Districts that found a way out: proven exit strategies (specific, not generic)

The system is not immutable. Other districts have documented exit strategies — not as theory, but as budget choices and labor agreements.

**Conversion commitments (payroll over invoices).** **San Francisco USD** estimates it will pay **$42.1 million in 2025–26** to non‑public programs, independent agencies, and consultants for special education; its February 2026 tentative agreement with United Educators of San Francisco explicitly committed to **reducing reliance** on non‑union contractors while setting caseload goals and increasing paraeducator pay.

**“Nearly double” math in public record.** A state fact‑finding report for **Oakland USD** crystallized the financial logic: “When you contract out that position, you’re actually costing the district nearly double the amount of money.”

**Pay differentials for shortage roles.** Hawaii implemented a **$10,000 annual special‑education differential** in 2020; CALDER research found it reduced the proportion of vacant SPED positions by **32%** relative to general education (with the caveat that much of the gain came from general‑education teachers switching into SPED roles).

**Residency pipelines and “grow your own.”** Chicago’s residency model trains roughly **200 teachers per year** by recruiting current classroom aides into teaching pathways — an example of converting “coverage” workers into licensed educators.

**Teletherapy for related services.** Teletherapy does not replace the need for on‑site paraprofessionals, but it can reduce reliance on contracted related‑service providers (SLPs/OT) when implemented with supervision and continuity guardrails.

**Stop paying more for credentials than for vacancies.** An **NCTQ** analysis of 148 large districts found only **six** offer SPED teachers additional pay exceeding **$5,000 per year**, and that over 90% pay more for master’s degrees than for filling special‑education vacancies despite limited evidence of effectiveness gains from master’s degrees alone.

The point is not to praise other districts. It is to document that exit strategies exist — and to sharpen the question of why Vancouver has not publicly adopted and measured one.

## The question for Torres‑Morales (landing)

Dr. Torres‑Morales begins July 1, 2026.

He inherits:
- a contractor pipeline that consumed **about $32 million over five years**,
- a compliance record with documented service‑minute failures and restraint crises,
- and a state funding environment that has, at least on paper, widened the possibility of hiring permanent staff.

The tools exist. The question is whether Vancouver Public Schools will use them.

**Right‑of‑reply question (core):** Will VPS publish a conversion plan — role by role — that sets targets for replacing contractor labor with trained, retained employees; reports routinely on IEP‑minute delivery; and commits to transparency on purchased‑services labor before the next budget cycle?

Because the stakes are not a spreadsheet. They are a child in a quiet room, waiting for the adults in the building to have enough training, enough support, and enough stability to do the job safely.
