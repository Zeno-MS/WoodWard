# Editorial Analysis

The reporting package is strong on evidence and weak on market fit.

The core problem is not that the reporting lacks substance. The records, numbers, timeline, and sourcing are substantially stronger than what most local reporters receive from outside sources. The problem is that the package often reads like a completed prosecution brief rather than a story a local newsroom can quickly own, verify, and publish.

## What is working

- The evidence chain is unusually strong. The package is grounded in warrant registers, board materials, OSPI records, contract documents, public reporting, and a reproducible methodology.
- The central contradiction is legible: a district that cut 260-plus positions during a declared fiscal emergency still routed $13.3 million in one year to staffing vendors, with $10.97 million going to Amergis alone.
- The contract story is real, concrete, and easy to understand. Auto-renewal, uncapped rate changes, no spending cap, and a conversion fee are the kind of facts readers and reporters can grasp quickly.
- The classroom consequences are not abstract. The Bailey reporting, SECC 21-32 service-minute failures, restraint history, overload grievances, and walkouts provide the human stakes.
- The current 2026 context creates urgency. The $20.15 million projected deficit, the county borrowing arrangement, the OCR investigation, the $4.8 million settlement, and the incoming superintendent all make this a live story.

## What is not working

- The original four-part series leads too often with structure before consequence. The audience has to read too far before understanding why they should care now.
- The titles are analytic rather than reportorial. They sound like white papers, not local investigations.
- The articles repeat defensive disclaimers so often that the caution starts to compete with the findings.
- The series repeats context across installments instead of moving with momentum. That is appropriate for a dossier, but not ideal for publication.
- The package asks a reporter to absorb an entire finished architecture instead of taking hold of one clean, urgent story line.

## Why reporters may hesitate

- Local outlets are time-constrained. A long, four-part investigation is expensive to digest and expensive to publish.
- An anonymous source carrying a highly developed package creates trust friction, even when the documents are real.
- The work can feel too complete. Reporters may worry they are being asked to validate and front someone else's finished narrative rather than build their own.
- The broader fiscal crisis at VPS is already public. Without a sharper hook, editors may read this as an extension of a known story rather than a new revelation.

## What the strongest hook actually is

The strongest hook is not simply that VPS is in financial trouble. That is already known.

The strongest hook is that during the same period VPS said it had to cut more than 260 positions, it routed $13.3 million in one year to private staffing vendors, including $10.97 million to Amergis, the rebranded successor to Maxim Healthcare, under a contract structure with no spending cap and no public competitive check.

That hook works because it contains:

- a concrete number,
- a direct contradiction,
- a corporate identity issue,
- a governance issue, and
- immediate consequences for students and staff.

## Best narrative base in the repo

The strongest existing draft is not the original four-part series. It is the consolidated handoff draft in the handoffs folder, which opens with Colin Bailey and then moves into the money trail. That structure better reflects how readers process institutional stories: human consequence first, then mechanism, then accountability.

## Editorial recommendation

The package should be treated as three distinct products instead of one:

1. A reporter-facing tip package with one sharp lead, a one-page fact sheet, and document links.
2. A publication-ready article set with stronger narrative sequencing and more journalistic titles.
3. A civic action package aimed at auditors, public officials, and outside watchdogs rather than journalists.

The rewrites in this folder are built around that second goal. They preserve the facts while changing the reading experience.
