# They Cut 260 Positions. Then the Contractor Payments Hit $13.3 Million.

On March 12, 2024, the Vancouver Public Schools Board of Directors voted unanimously to cut $35 million from the district's budget. The cuts were specific and painful: 50 elementary teachers, 63 secondary teachers, 33 teachers on special assignment, 10 counselors, 9 librarians, 29 custodians, and more than 260 positions overall in a district serving roughly 22,000 students.

District leaders said the cuts were unavoidable. Enrollment was down. State funding was inadequate. Federal pandemic relief money had expired. Parents, students, and staff were told there was simply no other way.

But that is only part of the story.

In the same 2024-25 fiscal year, Vancouver Public Schools processed $13,326,622 in payments to private staffing vendors. Of that total, $10,970,973 went to Amergis Healthcare Staffing.

Amergis was previously known as Maxim Healthcare Services.

In 2011, under that earlier name, the company agreed to pay $150 million to resolve federal criminal and civil cases involving Medicaid fraud, including allegations that employees billed for nursing services never provided and paid illegal kickbacks. The U.S. Department of Justice described it as one of the largest healthcare fraud settlements in the country. The company entered a criminal guilty plea, and multiple executives were individually indicted.

In 2022, Maxim rebranded its staffing division as Amergis. By 2025, Vancouver's payments had largely shifted from one name to the other. Maxim's receipts from the district fell to $15,716. Amergis's climbed to nearly $11 million.

No public announcement explained the transition. In the BoardDocs materials reviewed for this investigation, there is at least one agenda item identifying Amergis as "formerly Maxim," but no public board discussion in the reviewed materials of the company's 2011 fraud history.

The district's own payment records show how dramatic the growth became.

Those figures come from 25,578 deduplicated payment records extracted from 153 warrant register PDFs. Across five years, staffing-vendor payments totaled $32,189,236.

Amergis and Maxim, treated together as one corporate lineage through the rebrand, captured $28,074,510 of that total, or 87.2%.

The contrast with the district's public austerity message is difficult to ignore.

While payroll positions were being eliminated, Object 7, the accounting category called Purchased Services, kept growing. By 2024-25, staffing-vendor payments consumed 28.16% of the district's entire Object 7 actual expenditures. In 2020-21, that figure had been 1.17%.

That does not mean every Purchased Services dollar was a staffing dollar. It does mean that by the austerity year, more than one in four Object 7 dollars was going to staffing vendors.

The district's likely defense is straightforward: special education services are federally mandated, labor markets are tight, and districts often use agencies because they cannot leave IEP-required roles unfilled. That is a real part of the story. But it does not erase the contradiction. A district that said it could not afford more than 260 public positions still found room for $13.3 million in contractor spending in the same year.

That contradiction is the center of this story.

The next question is how the district ended up in a vendor relationship this large, under a contract this open-ended, with so little visible public scrutiny.
