# Why the Pipeline Kept Growing

There is an easy version of the Vancouver Public Schools contractor story and a harder one.

The easy version is that the district made bad choices and paid too much.

The harder version is that the district got caught in a system that makes contractor dependence expensive, legally defensible, and hard to unwind all at once.

That harder version is the one the public needs to understand, because it explains why a district can know the contractor model is costly and still keep feeding it.

Start with the labor market.

Special education teachers, speech-language pathologists, behavior specialists, nurses, and related service providers are difficult to recruit across the region. Agencies exist because there is demand for exactly those hard-to-fill positions. That shortage is real.

But shortage alone does not explain why the district paid such a large premium for labor it might otherwise have bought through its own payroll.

To understand that premium, this investigation compared agency rates with a fully loaded internal cost model using VEA and VAESP salary schedules, SEBB employer contributions, DRS pension rates, and payroll taxes. The model uses 1,440 productive hours per year and mid-career baseline steps rather than entry or top-of-scale compensation.

| Role | Fully Loaded Annual Cost | Internal Hourly Cost |
| --- | ---: | ---: |
| Paraeducator | $56,842 | $39.47 |
| SPED Teacher | $87,869 | $61.02 |
| SLP or RN | $97,581 | $67.76 |

Against those internal make-costs, the vendor rate schedule showed rates ranging from $45 per hour to as high as $180 per hour, depending on role.

The midpoint example is the clearest. At a $75-per-hour SPED teacher rate, the district's $13,326,622 in staffing-vendor spending buys about 177,688 vendor hours, or roughly 123.4 vendor FTEs. If those same productive hours had been purchased internally at the modeled direct-hire SPED teacher cost of $61.02 per hour, that same money would have represented about 151.6 internal FTEs.

The difference is roughly 28 SPED-teacher-equivalent positions, or about $2.48 million in lost purchasing power.

Even the conservative end of the model shows a cost premium. At the low-rate scenario, the premium still exceeds $1.6 million.

So why keep paying it?

Because four forces push in the same direction.

The first is market failure. The district cannot hire people who are not applying.

The second is state underinvestment. A direct hire costs more than base salary. Benefits, retirement, and payroll taxes all matter, and Washington districts have been hit hard by structural cost drivers including SEBB. The district's internal labor is not cheap just because it is not purchased through an agency.

The third is accounting pressure. When labor moves through Object 7 instead of Objects 2 and 3, the district is still buying people, but the spending sits in a different part of the budget. In an austerity cycle, that matters politically and administratively. Payroll headcount can fall even while labor is still being purchased elsewhere.

The fourth is federal compliance pressure. IDEA does not let a district say that a vacancy excuses non-delivery of services. If a student's IEP requires a special education teacher, therapist, nurse, or behavior support, the district must provide those services or face compliance risk. Contracting can become the fastest way to demonstrate that the district took action when direct recruitment failed.

That is the trap.

Vacancies appear. Services still have to be delivered. Agencies can fill roles quickly. The district pays a premium. The premium drains money from the general fund. Budget stress intensifies. Hiring becomes harder. Dependency deepens.

The district's own broader budget pattern reflects that stress. Vancouver budgeted $36,738,206 for Object 7 in FY24-25. Actual spending reached $47,331,056, an overage of $10,592,850.

That does not prove staffing vendors alone caused the overspend. Object 7 covers many categories besides staffing. But it does show that the district did not simply budget large and live within that plan. It budgeted lower than neighboring Evergreen on a per-pupil basis, then blew past its own ceiling.

| District | FY24-25 Object 7 Budget | General Fund Budget | Enrollment | Object 7 Per Pupil | Object 7 Share |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Evergreen SD | $47,125,176 | $432,593,222 | 21,807 | $2,161.01 | 10.89% |
| Vancouver SD | $36,738,206 | $423,538,605 | 20,807 | $1,765.67 | 8.67% |

On paper, Vancouver looked more restrained than Evergreen. In practice, Vancouver's actual Object 7 spending reached $47.3 million, slightly above Evergreen's budget on a per-pupil basis.

That gap between plan and reality is where the staffing story matters most. The contractor pipeline was not just expensive. It was operating inside a district already facing a recurring mismatch between revenues and costs, shrinking reserves, and repeated emergency borrowing.

The important point is not that contractor use was irrational from the start. It is that what may have begun as emergency problem-solving hardened into a costly operating model.

That is why the district's likely defense, while partly true, is not enough. Yes, the labor market is hard. Yes, IDEA requires services. Yes, hiring internally is expensive. But those realities do not answer the public's central question: what was the district's plan to stop the stopgap from becoming the system?
