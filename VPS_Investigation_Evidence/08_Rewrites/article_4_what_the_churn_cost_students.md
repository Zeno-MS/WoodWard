# What the Churn Cost Students

A school day can begin normally and still end in a quiet room.

At Hough Elementary, an autistic student, Colin Bailey, was restrained almost daily during the 2015-16 school year, according to reporting by OPB and InvestigateWest. On one occasion, he was isolated for more than two hours. His mother, Cara Bailey, later alleged that the district failed to provide the one-on-one paraeducator required by his IEP and instead substituted less specific adult support. A psychiatrist later diagnosed him with PTSD attributed to his school experiences.

That case matters because it turns an abstract budget question into a classroom one. When a district cannot reliably staff high-need settings with trained, stable adults, the consequences are measured in more than invoices.

Vancouver's own public record shows a pattern of serious strain.

In 2015-16, the district documented 1,110 physical restraints. Later reporting found Vancouver remained among the districts with high restraint counts statewide, including 776 restraints in 2022-23.

Washington banned prone and supine restraint in September 2021. OPB and InvestigateWest later reported that prohibited restraint tactics continued after that ban.

In OSPI complaint decision SECC 21-32, the state found that Vancouver delivered only 960 of 1,575 weekly special education minutes required by one student's December 2020 IEP, a shortfall of 615 minutes per week. OSPI recorded the district's admission that there was not a concerted plan to supplement in-person special education instruction when staffing fell short. The parent reported that substitute teachers caused confusion because of how often adults changed. Six of twelve IEP goals showed no progress.

Those are different failures, but they point in the same direction: unstable staffing and weak continuity impose costs on students first.

The workforce conditions described by staff and outside research help explain why.

In September 2025, the classified staff union representing paraeducators and other frontline employees authorized a strike by roughly 80% of members before a tentative agreement was reached days later. Reporting tied the dispute to workforce churn, training gaps, staffing levels, and injuries.

The district's certificated union also documented classroom strain. A grievance alleged large classes, including high school courses above 40 students and middle school English language arts classes above 42, resulting in $2.75 million in overload payments.

At the March 12, 2024 meeting where the board approved $35 million in cuts, paraeducator Christopher Smith put the staffing problem in plain language: "As you burn out your staff, there are limited replacements. This is not a renewable resource."

National and state research shows the same pattern.

ASHA's 2024 Schools Survey found that school-based speech-language pathologists reported median actual caseloads of 50 students against a manageable caseload of 40.

A 2023 study of 490 special educators found more than 60% at dangerous levels of emotional exhaustion and about 38% meeting clinical criteria for generalized anxiety disorder and major depressive disorder.

Washington State University's Josh Taylor summarized the special education labor cycle this way: districts hire teachers without special education certifications because turnover and vacancies are so high, and "that becomes this nasty, cyclical problem."

The research record is no longer limited to correlation. A 2025 CALDER working paper using Washington data found that special education teacher turnover has outsized negative effects on academic outcomes for students with disabilities. The U.S. Government Accountability Office has also reported that abusive use of seclusion and restraint is associated with insufficient staff training.

This is where the contractor pipeline connects back to the budget story.

The district used agencies because it needed adults in classrooms and service settings. But staffing through vendors does not automatically create continuity. A district can buy coverage and still lose stability. Students can still cycle through substitutes, short-term placements, and adults with uneven preparation for the specific needs in front of them.

By 2026, those pressures had become part of a wider district crisis.

The projected deficit for 2026-27 reached $20.15 million. The district disclosed $4.8 million in settlements over sexual abuse lawsuits. The U.S. Department of Education opened a Title IX investigation. Flex Academy, an alternative high school serving 86 students, more than half with IEPs, was closed. Teachers at Fort Vancouver High School walked out over safety concerns.

Incoming superintendent Ricardo Torres-Morales inherits all of it on July 1, 2026.

He also inherits a changed policy environment. SB 5263 eliminated Washington's 16% cap on special education funding, and ESHB 2049 expanded local levy authority. Those steps do not solve VPS's budget crisis, but they do make one public question harder to avoid: if the district now has more room to build permanent capacity, where is the plan to convert unstable contractor dependence into direct, trained, retained staffing?

Other districts have at least described exit strategies. San Francisco USD committed in a 2026 tentative agreement to reduce reliance on outside contractors while improving pay and caseload conditions. Oakland's public fact-finding record described contracted positions as costing nearly double. Hawaii used a special education pay differential to reduce vacancies. Chicago built residency pathways that turned classroom aides into licensed teachers.

Vancouver does not need a slogan. It needs a conversion plan the public can measure: role by role, target by target, year by year.

Because the final measure of this story is not whether Object 7 went up or down. It is whether students who need stable adults actually get them.
