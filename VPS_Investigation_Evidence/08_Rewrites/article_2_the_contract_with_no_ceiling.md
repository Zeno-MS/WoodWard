# The Contract With No Ceiling

The most revealing number in Vancouver Public Schools' staffing-vendor story may not be the $13.3 million the district paid in one year. It may be the $3 million figure the board was told to expect.

In the BoardDocs agenda item for the 2024-25 Amergis contract, the recommendation states: "Approximate Cost to District: $3,000,000 for the 24-25 SY."

Actual payments to Amergis that year totaled $10,970,973.

That does not prove the district acted improperly. The $3 million figure was described as an approximate cost, not a legal cap, and service needs can grow midyear as student caseloads change. But the gap forces a governance question: what system existed to review and control a vendor relationship that expanded to more than three times the figure presented to the board?

The contract architecture helps explain why the answer may be very little.

The 2021 Maxim Healthcare Staffing agreement, obtained from VPS BoardDocs, contains a set of terms that pushed leverage toward the vendor.

The agreement renews automatically each year unless terminated.

It requires 90 days' advance written notice to terminate, a longer exit period than the 30-day notice in the original 2016 agreement.

It allows rate changes on 30 days' notice.

It builds in automatic annual rate increases.

And it contains no maximum annual expenditure cap. There is no not-to-exceed clause setting a ceiling on what the district might ultimately pay.

The agreement also makes it expensive for the district to bring agency workers onto its own payroll. If VPS hires a person placed by the vendor within 12 months, the contract imposes a placement fee equal to the greater of $5,000 or 30% of the worker's annualized salary. For a school psychologist billed at $100 per hour, that fee would exceed $43,000.

Those rates, standing alone, do not prove overspending. Many fall within published market ranges for specialized staffing. The issue is the combination of high-volume usage and contract terms that offered the district few visible restraints once dependency deepened.

The oversight structure around that dependency was thin.

Washington law requires competitive bidding for certain categories of school purchases, such as equipment, supplies, and construction above specified thresholds. Professional staffing contracts sit outside those enumerated bidding rules under RCW 28A.335.190. That exemption made the contracts lawful without a formal bid.

But lawfulness is not the same thing as robust oversight.

This investigation found no public evidence in the reviewed board materials that the district invoked the emergency waiver process for staffing procurement. A text search across board packets, contracts, minutes, and related records found no staffing-specific emergency declaration or board resolution under the statutory emergency mechanism.

The district also revised Board Policy 6220 multiple times during the years vendor spending was rising from $349,709 to $13.3 million. None of those revisions added a staffing-specific competitive review or reporting mechanism.

Meanwhile, the approvals themselves became harder for the public to see. Earlier staffing contracts appeared as Action Items requiring separate debate and votes. As the relationship grew, contract approvals moved into the consent agenda process, where routine items are approved in a block without individual discussion unless a board member pulls one out.

The board's own materials show that it was not completely blind to continuity. At least one agenda item identified Amergis as formerly Maxim. But there is no public debate in the reviewed materials over whether a relationship that had grown into an eight-figure annual pipeline deserved a different level of scrutiny.

That is the accountability issue here.

This article does not claim the district broke the law by using a professional-services exemption. It does not claim the board intended to conceal anything. It documents something narrower and more concrete: a vendor relationship that had no visible spending ceiling, few public friction points, and no clear mechanism for the public to see when a multimillion-dollar estimate became an $11 million reality.
