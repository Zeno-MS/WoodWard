# The Structural Erosion of Vancouver Public Schools: A Forensic Audit of Privatization, Administrative Insulation, and Fiscal Insolvency

## Executive Summary

Vancouver Public Schools (VPS) is currently navigating a period of profound institutional instability, characterized by a convergence of fiscal mismanagement, strategic privatization of core educational services, and a deepening schism between central administration and school-site operations. As the district faces a structural deficit estimated at \$35 million for the 2024-25 fiscal year, requiring budget reductions of approximately 9%, the administration has publicly attributed these challenges to external pressures: the inequities of the post-McCleary state funding model, the expiration of federal Elementary and Secondary School Emergency Relief (ESSER) funds, and systemic enrollment declines.^1^ However, a rigorous, evidence-based analysis of the district's financial instruments, board minutes, and operational patterns reveals that these external factors have been significantly exacerbated by internal policy decisions. Specifically, the district has pursued a strategy of aggressive privatization within its special education department, insulating high-cost administrative roles through bureaucratic restructuring, and effectively managing the district toward the brink of state intervention.

This report provides an exhaustive examination of the operational health of VPS. It scrutinizes the mechanisms of privatization, detailing how the district has shifted from a direct-hire model to a reliance on high-cost third-party vendors like Soliant Health, Maxim Healthcare, and Pioneer Healthcare. This shift, categorized under \"purchased services\" (Object 7), has resulted in a fragmented system of care that prioritizes contractual flexibility over student safety and educational continuity, precipitating a crisis of workplace safety and labor grievances. Simultaneously, the report investigates the administrative response to the deficit, uncovering a pattern of \"repurposing\" rather than reducing executive roles---a \"shell game\" that preserves the central office hierarchy while austerity measures erode classroom supports. Finally, the analysis assesses the probability of a state takeover by the Office of Superintendent of Public Instruction (OSPI), comparing VPS's current financial health indicators against the statutory triggers for \"Binding Conditions\" and \"Financial Oversight\" that have recently engulfed neighboring districts.

The findings indicate that VPS is operating in a perilous \"pre-insolvency\" phase. The depletion of fund reserves to mask structural deficits, combined with the refusal to structurally align administrative costs with declining enrollment, has placed the district on a trajectory that increasingly points toward loss of local control. This report serves as a critical audit of the decisions that have led to this juncture, offering a detailed roadmap of the fiscal and operational realities facing the district.

## Part I: The Fiscal Architecture of Crisis

To fully comprehend the magnitude of the instability within Vancouver Public Schools, it is necessary to dissect the financial architecture that underpins the current crisis. The deficit is not merely a shortfall of revenue; it is the result of a long-term decoupling of operational expenditures from the district's actual revenue base. This section analyzes the \"fiscal cliff\" created by the reliance on one-time federal funds, the mechanics of enrollment-based funding loss, and the opaque expansion of \"purchased services\" as a primary driver of the deficit.

### 1.1 The Post-ESSER Fiscal Cliff and the Illusion of Solvency

The conclusion of the ESSER funding era has served as a catalyst, exposing structural deficits that were temporarily obscured by the influx of federal pandemic relief. Like many districts, VPS utilized these one-time funds not merely for pandemic recovery, but to plug recurring operational holes in the general fund.^2^ This practice created a \"fiscal cliff\"---a sudden drop in revenue without a corresponding reduction in the recurring expenses that the federal funds had been supporting.

The district's budget for the 2024-25 school year reflects the harsh reality of this transition. With the expiration of ESSER, the district must now fund ongoing operations solely through state apportionment and local levies. However, the cost structures built during the pandemic---including enhanced staffing ratios in certain areas and expanded vendor contracts---have calcified. The district's attempt to maintain these structures without the federal subsidy has necessitated the drastic \$35 million reduction, a figure that represents nearly 10% of the operating budget. This is not a marginal adjustment; it is a fundamental contraction of the district's economic footprint.

### 1.2 The Enrollment-Funding Death Spiral

Compounding the loss of federal funds is a steady and systemic erosion of the district's student base. In Washington State, school funding is primarily driven by enrollment. The \"prototypical school model\" allocates resources---teachers, administrators, and classified staff---based on specific ratios derived from the number of Full-Time Equivalent (FTE) students. When enrollment declines, funding drops swiftly and linearly. However, the costs associated with running a school district are largely fixed or \"step-variable.\" A decline of 50 students across a grade level reduces revenue by hundreds of thousands of dollars, but it does not immediately allow the district to close a building, turn off utilities, or reduce insurance premiums.

For VPS, enrollment decline has become a chronic condition. Recent data indicates a significant drop in student numbers, driven by demographic shifts, housing affordability issues in Vancouver, and the migration of families to alternative educational options.^3^ This decline triggers a \"death spiral\" in funding: as the district cuts programs to balance the budget, it becomes less attractive to families, leading to further enrollment declines and further funding cuts. The district has attempted to mitigate this through the use of local levy funds, but these are capped by state law and are intended for \"enrichment,\" not basic education. The over-reliance on local levies to backfill basic education deficits has strained the district's relationship with taxpayers and left it vulnerable to levy failures.

### 1.3 The \"Object 7\" Explosion: Purchased Services Analysis

A critical, yet often under-scrutinized, component of the VPS budget is \"Object 7,\" the accounting code designated for \"Purchased Services.\" This category captures expenditures for contracted work, including third-party staffing agencies, consultants, and external service providers. Unlike \"Object 2\" (Salaries) and \"Object 3\" (Benefits), which fund the district's direct workforce, Object 7 expenditures represent a transfer of public funds to private entities.

Analysis of the district's financial trends reveals a strategic shift toward outsourcing core educational functions, particularly in special education and nursing. This shift is often justified by the administration as a necessary response to labor shortages. However, the financial implications are profound. When the district hires a direct employee, it pays a negotiated salary and state-mandated benefits (SEBB). When it contracts with a firm like Soliant or Maxim, it pays a premium \"bill rate\" that covers the worker's wage, the agency's administrative overhead, and the agency's profit margin.

In the healthcare and educational staffing sectors, these premiums typically range from 40% to 100% above the cost of a direct hire. For the 2023-24 and 2024-25 budget cycles, the inflation of Object 7 expenditures has contributed disproportionately to the district's structural deficit. By locking itself into expensive vendor contracts, the district has reduced its financial flexibility. These contracts often contain minimum hour guarantees and buyout clauses, making them difficult to shed quickly. The result is a budget structure where a growing percentage of funds is diverted to private corporations, leaving less money available for the direct employment of teachers and support staff.

## Part II: The Privatization of Special Education

The privatization of special education in Vancouver Public Schools represents the most significant and contentious operational shift in the district's recent history. This is not merely a stopgap measure for staffing shortages; it has evolved into a systemic operational model that prioritizes contract flexibility and \"on-paper\" compliance over educational quality, safety, and staff support. By outsourcing critical roles to national staffing conglomerates, the district has fundamentally altered the nature of special education delivery.

### 2.1 The Contractor Ecosystem: Soliant, Maxim, and Pioneer

The district's board minutes and financial records reveal a heavy and increasing reliance on a specific set of national staffing agencies. These are not local partners but large, often private-equity-backed corporations that specialize in capitalizing on public sector labor shortages.

#### Soliant Health: The Shadow Workforce

Soliant Health has become a ubiquitous presence within VPS. Board records indicate repeated approvals for Soliant contracts to place special services providers, including Speech-Language Pathologists (SLPs), Occupational Therapists (OTs), and School Psychologists.^4^

- **Mechanism of Entrenchment:** These contracts often originate as temporary fixes for maternity leaves or unfilled vacancies.^7^ However, board minutes show a pattern of these contracts rolling over into annual agreements or being expanded through addendums.^6^ This suggests that the district has ceased viewing these vacancies as temporary problems to be solved with recruitment, and instead views them as permanent \"outsourced\" positions.

- **Financial Impact:** While specific contract totals are often aggregated in consent agendas, comparative market data indicates that districts pay between \$70,000 and over \$100,000 annually per position for agency staff, often for a shorter work year than a district employee.^8^ The premium paid to Soliant represents a direct drain on the special education budget, contributing to the \"encroachment\" on the general fund that the administration frequently cites as a cause of the deficit.

#### Maxim Healthcare: Outsourcing the School Nurse

Maxim Healthcare is another dominant vendor, primarily utilized by VPS for nursing services.^9^

- **Scope of Services:** Maxim is frequently contracted to provide 1:1 nursing care for medically fragile students and to fill vacancies in school health rooms.

- **Cost Implications:** Accounts payable registers from VPS and comparable districts show consistent, high-volume payments to Maxim.^10^ The outsourcing of nursing services detaches medical support from the educational team. Maxim nurses report to their agency, not the school administration, creating potential conflicts regarding protocols and duty hours.

- **Accountability Gap:** As a private entity, Maxim's primary obligation is to its shareholders. This creates a structural conflict of interest where the incentive is to maximize billable hours rather than optimize student independence or integrate seamlessly into the school culture.

#### Pioneer Healthcare

Pioneer Healthcare Services appears in board documents as another provider of therapy services.^13^ The diversification of vendors---using multiple agencies simultaneously---suggests a fragmented approach to staffing. Rather than building a cohesive special education department with a unified vision, the district is piecing together its workforce through a marketplace of temporary labor.

### 2.2 The \"Inclusion\" Mandate and Resource Starvation

A critical flashpoint in the privatization dynamic is the district's implementation of \"inclusion\" models without adequate support. In late 2023, VPS announced a directive to move secondary special education students---many of whom were in self-contained classrooms due to significant behavioral or academic needs---into general education science and social studies classes.^14^

#### The Pedagogical \"Bait and Switch\"

While \"inclusion\" is a legally and morally supported goal under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), it requires robust support structures: co-teachers, modified curriculum, and, crucially, trained paraeducators. The VPS rollout has been characterized by the Vancouver Education Association (VEA) as unilateral and dangerously under-resourced.

- **Reduction of Self-Contained Units:** By pushing students into general education settings, the district can theoretically reduce the number of specialized self-contained classrooms. This allows for a reduction in the number of specialized (and expensive) special education teachers required, serving as a covert cost-saving mechanism disguised as pedagogical progress.

- **The Burden on General Education:** General education teachers, already managing large class sizes, are expected to accommodate students with complex behavioral and cognitive needs without the aid of additional paraeducators. When support *is* provided, it is increasingly likely to be a transient agency contractor who lacks familiarity with the student's specific Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP) or safety protocols.

### 2.3 The Safety Crisis and Teacher Support

The privatization and under-resourcing of special education have precipitated a safety crisis for district staff. The lack of stable, trained personnel has led to an increase in behavioral escalations and staff injuries, resulting in a surge of grievances and low morale.

#### The Breakdown of the IEP Team

A functional Individualized Education Program (IEP) team relies on long-term relationships and deep knowledge of the student. Agency staff, by definition, are transient. They may not know the triggers, de-escalation history, or communication nuances of a student. This knowledge gap drastically increases the likelihood of crisis behaviors.

- **Grievances:** The VEA has filed formal grievances regarding the lack of support and safety concerns. Specifically, the union has cited the district\'s \"projected move to circumvent the IEP team decisions\" regarding student placement.^14^ This indicates that administrative decisions about placement are overriding the professional judgment of the educators who know the students best.

- **Lack of Training:** Agency staff often do not receive the same district-specific safety training (e.g., Right Response or CPI) as permanent staff. If they do receive training, it is often expedited or generic. This leaves them ill-equipped to handle violent outbursts, effectively transferring the physical risk to the remaining permanent teachers and paraeducators who must step in to manage crises.

#### Support Deserts for Current Teachers

Current special education teachers report a profound lack of administrative support. The district utilizes a \"traveling staff\" model for many related services, where itinerant specialists (often contractors) move between multiple schools.^15^

- **Caseload vs. Workload:** While caseload numbers might technically meet contract limits on paper, the *workload*---the intensity of student needs and the administrative burden---has exploded. The \"traveling\" SLP or Psychologist may only be in a building one day a week. For the other four days, that school operates in a \"support desert,\" where teachers have no immediate recourse for consultation, crisis support, or compliance assistance.

- **Administrative Abandonment:** Teachers report that when they raise concerns about safety or workload, the administrative response is often to point to the presence of the contractors as evidence that the position is \"filled,\" ignoring the reality that a transient, part-time agency worker does not provide the same level of support as a dedicated colleague.

## Part III: Administrative Pay and the Illusion of Reduction

A central point of contention in the VPS budget crisis is the perception of \"administrative bloat.\" While the district claims to have reduced central office staffing by a higher percentage than school-based staff, a forensic examination of the organizational chart, salary schedules, and job descriptions suggests a more complex reality of **relabeling**, **repurposing**, and **insulation**.

### 3.1 The Salary of the Superintendent and Cabinet

The compensation for the Superintendent and the executive cabinet remains a significant expenditure, particularly when contrasted with the austerity demanded of school sites. The narrative of \"shared sacrifice\" is difficult to sustain when executive salaries continue to rise amidst mass layoffs of lower-paid staff.

#### Superintendent Compensation

Superintendent Jeff Snell's compensation package places him among the highest-paid public officials in Washington State.

- **Base Salary Trajectory:** Public records indicate a steep upward trajectory in the Superintendent\'s base salary. Starting at approximately \$275,000, the contract included scheduled increases rising to over **\$295,000** by the 2023-24 school year.^16^ More recent data from OSPI personnel reports lists the Superintendent\'s total salary for the 2023-24 school year at **\$356,835**.^17^

- **Total Compensation Package:** The base salary is only one component. When mandatory benefits, retirement contributions, vehicle allowances, and potential annuity payments are factored in, the total cost of the Superintendent position to the district likely exceeds \$450,000 annually.

- **Comparative Context:** This salary significantly outpaces the earnings of the Governor of Washington and most federal cabinet members. In a district facing a \$35 million deficit, where paraeducators earning \$25 an hour are being laid off, this disparity fuels intense resentment and undermines the administration\'s moral authority to implement cuts.

#### The Executive Cabinet

The \"cabinet\" level---comprising Associate Superintendents, Chief Financial Officers, and Executive Directors---also commands high six-figure salaries.

- **Salary Bands:** Executive-level positions in VPS typically range from **\$180,000 to \$240,000** annually.

- **Protection from RIF:** While the district implemented a broad Reduction in Force (RIF), the executive cabinet often remains largely intact. Reductions at this level are frequently achieved through attrition (voluntary retirements) rather than layoffs. Furthermore, when these positions are vacated, they are often refilled or \"restructured\" rather than eliminated, preserving the high-salary line item in the budget.

### 3.2 The \"Shell Game\" of Central Office Cuts

The district's assertion that it has \"repurposed\" roles rather than simply cutting them warrants deep skepticism.^1^ \"Repurposing\" is often a euphemism for preserving a high-salary FTE by changing the title and modifying the job description slightly, without generating genuine savings or reducing the administrative headcount.

#### Deputy Superintendent to Executive Director

A specific example cited by the district is the conversion of the \"Deputy Superintendent\" role into positions that \"directly support schools,\" such as \"Executive Director of Teaching and Learning\".^1^

- **Fiscal Reality:** While a Deputy Superintendent might earn \$240,000 and an Executive Director \$210,000, the \"saving\" is marginal (\$30,000) compared to the cost of the full position. The FTE remains on the books, the pension liability remains, and the office space remains occupied.

- **Title Inflation vs. Deflation:** This practice is a classic bureaucratic survival strategy. By downgrading a title slightly, the district can claim to have eliminated a high-profile role. However, if the staff member is retained at a salary that is still three to four times that of a teacher, the structural cost to the district is virtually unchanged.

- **The \"Support\" Narrative:** The district claims these repurposed roles \"directly support schools.\" However, unless these administrators are physically in classrooms delivering instruction or providing direct behavioral support to students, they remain administrative overhead. \"Supporting schools\" from a central office desk often translates to increased compliance monitoring, data collection, and directive issuance---activities that add to the workload of building-level staff rather than alleviating it.

### 3.3 Relabeling vs. Reducing: A Forensic View

A true reduction in force at the central office would involve the elimination of entire departments or the consolidation of directorates, resulting in a net decrease in the number of high-salary employees.

- **Evidence of Relabeling:** The persistence of high numbers of \"administrators\" in OSPI data, despite internal claims of cuts, suggests that individuals are being moved laterally. For example, a \"Director of Professional Development\" might become a \"Director of Strategic Initiatives.\" The FTE count remains constant, the salary remains largely unchanged, but the district claims a \"reorganization\" has occurred.

- **Impact on Schools:** Real cuts are felt viscerally in the schools: fewer custodians mean dirtier buildings; fewer secretaries mean phones go unanswered; fewer paraeducators mean less safety. If the central office \"cuts\" are merely \"repurposed\" roles, the burden of the deficit is being shifted entirely onto the operational level of the district, protecting the administrative class from the consequences of their own fiscal management.

## Part IV: The Possibility of State Takeover

The specter of a state takeover, formally known as \"Financial Oversight\" or \"Binding Conditions,\" looms over Vancouver Public Schools. While the district is not yet in the final stage of dissolution, it is on a trajectory that mirrors other Washington districts that have lost local control. The district\'s financial health indicators are flashing warning signs that, if ignored, will trigger mandatory state intervention.

### 4.1 The Mechanism of Binding Conditions (WAC 392-123-060)

\"Binding Conditions\" is a statutory status for school districts in Washington that cannot balance their budgets. It is the first step in a state intervention process that can lead to a full takeover by OSPI.

- **The Trigger:** A district enters Binding Conditions when it submits a budget with a negative fund balance or requires permission to use future receivables to pay current debts.^18^ It is effectively a declaration of insolvency.

- **The Process:** Once on Binding Conditions, the district loses significant autonomy. It must submit monthly budget status reports to OSPI and the Educational Service District (ESD 112). It is often prohibited from hiring staff, signing new contracts, or making significant purchases without external approval.

- **Current Status of VPS:** As of mid-2024, VPS is **not** formally listed on the Binding Conditions list.^20^ However, comparative analyses place VPS in the \"Financial Warning\" category or identify it as having a dangerously low \"Total Profile Score\".^21^ The heavy reliance on fund balance reserves to cover recurring operating deficits is a primary indicator of impending insolvency.

### 4.2 Financial Health Indicators: The Warning Signs

OSPI uses a \"Financial Health Indicator\" model to track solvency. VPS's metrics in these areas are deteriorating rapidly.

- **Fund Balance to Revenue Ratio:** This metric measures the district's \"savings account.\" VPS has been spending down its reserves at an unsustainable rate to maintain staffing levels and pay for outsourced contracts.^2^ Once the fund balance dips below a certain threshold (typically 3-5% of expenditures), the district is flagged as high-risk.

- **Expenditures Exceeding Revenues:** VPS has consistently spent more than it receives in state and local revenue, bridging the gap with one-time ESSER funds and reserves. With ESSER gone, this structural deficit is the primary driver of the takeover risk.

- **Cash on Hand:** This metric measures how long the district can operate if funding stops. Low cash on hand forces districts to borrow (interfund loans) or seek apportionment advances from the state. A request for an apportionment advance is often the immediate precursor to being placed on Binding Conditions.

### 4.3 The Path to Financial Oversight and Dissolution

If VPS cannot correct its trajectory through the current 9% cuts, the next steps in the state intervention process are severe and prescriptive.

- **Step 1: Binding Conditions:** The district formally admits insolvency and signs an agreement with OSPI. This agreement dictates financial targets that must be met.

- **Step 2: Financial Oversight Committee (FOC):** If the district fails to meet the binding conditions for two consecutive years, OSPI convenes an FOC. This committee reviews the district\'s finances and has the power to recommend \"Enhanced Financial Oversight.\"

- **Step 3: Enhanced Financial Oversight:** This is the effective \"state takeover.\" A Special Administrator is appointed by the state. This administrator has the authority to override the Superintendent and School Board on financial decisions, veto contracts, order the liquidation of assets, and unilaterally reduce staff.^18^

- **Step 4: Dissolution:** In extreme cases (such as the historic dissolution of the Vader School District), a district can be dissolved and its territory annexed by neighboring districts. While unlikely for a district the size of Vancouver, the existence of this statute serves as the ultimate leverage for the state to force compliance.

### 4.4 Probability Analysis for VPS

The probability of VPS entering Binding Conditions in the next 12-24 months is **moderate to high** if the current reductions do not succeed in stabilizing the fund balance.

- **Risk Factors:** Continued enrollment decline, the refusal to curb high administrative costs, and the spiraling costs of special education outsourcing are the primary risk factors.

- **The Interim Strategy:** The appointment of an Interim Superintendent, Brett Blechschmidt (the former CFO), suggests a strategic pivot to \"hunker down\" and focus purely on solvency.^23^ This indicates that the board is aware of the imminent threat and has prioritized financial survival over instructional innovation or expansion.

## Part V: Conclusion and Strategic Implications

The financial crisis at Vancouver Public Schools is not merely a budgetary math problem; it is a symptom of systemic operational misalignment. The district has attempted to maintain a pre-pandemic administrative structure and a privatized special education model in a post-pandemic reality of shrinking enrollment and flat funding.

**Key Findings:**

1.  **Privatization is a Financial Drain:** The reliance on Soliant, Maxim, and Pioneer is a \"penny wise, pound foolish\" strategy. While it may solve immediate vacancy problems, it drastically drives up the per-pupil cost of special education and destabilizes the learning environment, leading to safety risks and potential litigation.

2.  **Administrative Cuts are Illusory:** The \"repurposing\" of high-level administrators suggests a protectionist culture at the central office. True austerity has not yet reached the cabinet level in a meaningful way compared to the visceral cuts felt in classrooms. The superintendent\'s salary remains an outlier that damages trust.

3.  **Takeover is a Real Threat:** VPS is operating on the edge of insolvency. Without a radical restructuring that moves away from high-cost outsourcing and administrative bloat, the district is on a glide path toward OSPI intervention.

The Outlook:

Unless VPS leadership pivots from \"managing decline\" via outsourcing to \"restructuring for sustainability\" via direct investment in a stable, district-employed workforce, the \"Binding Conditions\" status is likely inevitable. The continued alienation of the labor force (VEA) and the community through opaque budgeting and safety failures will only accelerate the enrollment decline, creating a death spiral that ends in state receivership.
