# The Fiscal Architecture of Outsourcing: An Exhaustive Analysis of Vendor Payments to Soliant, Maxim, Pioneer, and Amergis by Vancouver Public Schools (2015--2025)

## Executive Summary

The decade spanning 2015 to 2025 represents a transformative and often turbulent era in the financial administration of Vancouver Public Schools (District No. 37). This period, bookended by the landmark *McCleary* school funding decision and the post-pandemic fiscal cliff of the mid-2020s, witnessed a fundamental restructuring of how the district procures and manages essential student services. Central to this transformation is the strategic migration from direct employment models to a reliance on specialized third-party vendor contracts.

This comprehensive research report provides a forensic examination of the district\'s financial relationships with three primary healthcare and educational staffing conglomerates: **Soliant Health**, **Maxim Healthcare Staffing** (recently rebranded as **Amergis**), and **Pioneer Healthcare Services**. The analysis posits that the expenditures associated with these vendors---buried largely within the \"Purchased Services\" (Object 7) budget category and ratified via opaque consent agendas---are not merely stop-gap measures for temporary vacancies. Rather, they represent a structural institutionalization of outsourcing, driven by the convergence of rigid state funding formulas, the implementation of the School Employees Benefits Board (SEBB), chronic labor shortages in allied health fields, and the unyielding federal mandates of the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA).

The report details how these vendors have become \"super-creditors\" to the district. Even as Vancouver Public Schools faced binding conditions and enacted cuts exceeding \$35 million for the 2024-2025 fiscal year, payments to these agencies remained largely inelastic, protected by the legal necessity of providing services to students with Individualized Education Programs (IEPs). Furthermore, the analysis explores the governance mechanisms that facilitate these expenditures, highlighting a pattern of bulk approvals that obscures the cumulative long-term costs of agency premiums from public scrutiny. By synthesizing board minutes, state audit findings, collective bargaining agreements, and budget status reports, this document offers a definitive account of the privatization of special education staffing in one of Washington State's largest school districts.

## Section 1: The Regulatory and Fiscal Crucible (2015--2019)

To understand the specific financial flows to Soliant, Maxim, and Pioneer, one must first dissect the regulatory environment that necessitated their entrenchment. The years 2015 through 2019 were characterized by a seismic shift in Washington State school finance, primarily driven by the Supreme Court's *McCleary* decision. This legal framework fundamentally altered the economic incentives for hiring versus contracting.

### 1.1 The Pre-***McCleary*** Landscape and the Levy Dependency

Prior to the full implementation of the *McCleary* remedy in 2018, Vancouver Public Schools, like many districts in the state, relied heavily on local Maintenance and Operations (M&O) levies to fund \"basic education.\" This model created significant inequities but offered districts local flexibility. In this era, the decision to hire a school nurse or a speech-language pathologist (SLP) was often a calculation based on local levy capacity.

The data indicates that even in this period (2015--2016), the district faced challenges in staffing specialized roles. The \"market assessment\" reports from this era highlight the disparity between public school salary schedules and the private sector rates for allied health professionals.^1^ For example, while a Bachelor's level employee might earn between \$42,897 and \$47,636 in base pay, private sector equivalents in healthcare often commanded significantly higher wages. This wage gap created the initial fissure that allowed agencies like Maxim Healthcare to establish a foothold. Maxim, operating essentially as a labor arbitrageur, could attract nurses by offering higher hourly rates or flexible schedules that the district, bound by rigid collective bargaining agreements (CBAs) and state prototypical school funding models, could not match.

### 1.2 The Levy Swap and the Restriction of Enrichment Funds

The legislative solution to *McCleary* involved a \"levy swap\"---increasing the state property tax to fully fund basic education while simultaneously capping the amount districts could collect from local levies. For Vancouver Public Schools, this had a profound operational impact. The legislation capped local levies at the lesser of \$1.50 per \$1,000 of assessed value or \$2,500 per pupil.^2^

This restriction fundamentally changed the accounting logic for special education services. The state's funding model for special education is capped---historically at 13.5% of student enrollment.^2^ If a district's population of students with disabilities exceeds this cap, or if the intensity of services required by those students exceeds the state multiplier, the district must cover the excess costs. In the pre-swap era, local levy dollars were fluidly used to plug this gap. Post-swap, the usage of local levy funds was strictly monitored and restricted to \"enrichment\" activities, not basic education.

However, because special education is a federal civil right, it cannot be capped in practice. This regulatory squeeze incentivized the use of \"Purchased Services\" (Object 7). When a district contracts with Soliant or Pioneer for an SLP, the expenditure is categorized differently than a salary. It becomes a fee-for-service transaction. This accounting nuance allows the district to navigate the complexities of \"prototypical school\" staffing models. If the state funding model allocates 2.5 nurses for a district but the medical reality requires 10, the district faces a choice: hire 7.5 nurses using legally restricted local funds (risking audit findings regarding the \"enrichment\" definition) or contract out the service to Maxim/Amergis as a necessary expenditure to meet IDEA compliance, which can often be justified under different funding streams, including Safety Net grants.

### 1.3 The Emergence of the \"Agency Trap\"

By the 2018-2019 school year, the market dynamics had shifted perceptibly. Board minutes and budget discussions from this period reflect a growing anxiety regarding staffing compliance. The district's budget presentations noted increases in the special education multiplier (from 0.9609 to 0.995) but acknowledged that costs were rising faster than revenues.^3^

It is during this window that the \"agency trap\" began to calcify. The agency trap describes a cycle where the inability to fill permanent positions forces a district to use contractors. The presence of contractors---often paid higher effective hourly rates than the permanent staff---creates internal pay equity friction. This friction, combined with the general stress of understaffing, leads to higher turnover among permanent staff, which in turn necessitates more agency contracts. Pioneer Healthcare Services, for instance, began appearing more frequently in district documentation during this period, offering travel staff who could be deployed quickly to fill sudden vacancies.^4^

The *McCleary* era thus effectively primed the system for outsourcing. By rigidly defining what the state would pay for (the \"prototypical\" staff) and capping what the local community could pay for (levies), the legislature inadvertently created a robust market for third-party vendors who could provide the elasticity that the rigid funding model lacked.

## Section 2: The Structural Catalyst---SEBB and Labor Market Economics (2019--2021)

If *McCleary* primed the system for outsourcing, the implementation of the School Employees Benefits Board (SEBB) in 2020 was the catalyst that entrenched it. This policy change radically altered the cost-benefit analysis of direct employment versus contracting for Vancouver Public Schools.

### 2.1 The SEBB Mechanism and Financial Liability

Prior to 2020, districts had significant latitude in determining benefit eligibility for part-time employees. The implementation of SEBB mandated that any school employee working at least 630 hours per year (approximately 3.5 hours per day) was entitled to the full employer contribution for medical, dental, and vision benefits.^3^

For Vancouver Public Schools, this was a massive financial shock. The employer cost per employee surged. In the 2019-20 budget analysis, the district projected an increase of over \$3 million solely due to SEBB implementation.^3^ The \"blended rate\" for benefits meant that hiring a part-time 1:1 health aide for a medically fragile student became prohibitively expensive. A paraeducator earning \$25 an hour for 4 hours a day would incur a benefit load costing the district over \$1,000 per month---often nearly equalling their actual take-home wages.

### 2.2 The Vendor Arbitrage Strategy

This new cost structure created an immediate arbitrage opportunity for vendors like Maxim (now Amergis) and Soliant. When the district contracts for a nurse or a behavioral technician through an agency, the district pays a \"bill rate.\" This rate is undoubtedly higher than the hourly wage of a district employee. However, the district pays *zero* SEBB contributions, *zero* state pension (DRS) contributions, and *zero* payroll taxes on that bill rate.

**Cost Comparison Analysis:**

- **Direct Hire (Pre-SEBB):** Wage + \~20% payroll taxes/pension.

- **Direct Hire (Post-SEBB):** Wage + \~20% taxes/pension + \~\$12,000/year flat benefit rate.

- **Vendor Contract:** Bill Rate (Wage + Agency Margin).

For full-time employees, direct hiring might still be cheaper. But for the fluctuating, high-turnover, or part-time roles often required in special education (such as a 1:1 nurse for a student who might move districts in three months), the vendor model became fiscally superior. The district could turn the spigot on and off without the long-tail liabilities of SEBB enrollment and pension vesting. This structural incentive explains why, despite budget pressures, the \"Purchased Services\" object in the budget continued to swell.

### 2.3 The Rise of Soliant Health in the Recruitment Void

Simultaneous with the SEBB shock, the district faced an acute shortage of specialized certified staff, particularly School Psychologists and Speech-Language Pathologists (SLPs). The 2019-2021 period saw intense competition between Clark County districts (Vancouver, Evergreen, Battle Ground) for a limited pool of talent.

Soliant Health capitalized on this by offering a recruitment reach that a single school district's HR department could not match. Soliant operates as a national brokerage, recruiting \"travel\" therapists from across the country. The district's board minutes from this era reflect a reliance on Soliant not just for leave coverage, but for filling permanent vacancies that had no applicants. By the 2020-21 school year, Soliant had secured a position as a primary vendor, with contracts frequently appearing on consent agendas for renewal.^5^ The district effectively outsourced its recruitment function for these hard-to-fill roles, paying a premium for Soliant's ability to deliver warm bodies to classrooms.

## Section 3: The Pandemic Pivot and Federal Stimulus (2020--2023)

The arrival of the COVID-19 pandemic in March 2020 disrupted every facet of public education, but it solidified the role of third-party vendors in the district's crisis response architecture. The infusion of federal stimulus funds---ESSER (Elementary and Secondary School Emergency Relief) and ECF (Emergency Connectivity Fund)---provided the liquidity to sustain and expand these vendor contracts during a period of operational chaos.

### 3.1 Soliant and the Tele-Therapy Mandate

When schools closed physically, the legal mandates of IEPs remained in force. The district was required to provide \"comparable\" services to students with disabilities in a remote environment. This was a logistical nightmare for in-house staff who were often ill-equipped for tele-practice.

Soliant Health, however, possessed an established infrastructure for tele-therapy (VocoVision). The district's reliance on Soliant shifted from on-site staffing to remote service delivery. Board documents indicate that during the 2020-2021 and 2021-2022 cycles, Soliant's contracts were essential for maintaining compliance with federal civil rights laws. The ability of a vendor to provide a licensed SLP via Zoom ensured that the district did not face a wave of \"compensatory education\" lawsuits for missed therapy minutes. This period normalized the use of remote vendor staff, a practice that persisted even after schools reopened.

### 3.2 The ECF Audit and Procurement Controls

The massive influx of federal cash brought with it significant compliance risks. An audit by the Washington State Auditor's Office (SAO) regarding the Emergency Connectivity Fund (ECF) sheds light on the district's procurement culture during this time. The audit found that Vancouver Public Schools spent over \$5.3 million on laptops without adequate documentation of \"unmet need\" for each device.^6^

While this finding was specific to hardware, it is emblematic of a broader \"crisis procurement\" mindset. Faced with urgent needs, the district bypassed standard bureaucratic checks. It is highly probable that a similar dynamic applied to staffing contracts. The urgent need to staff a classroom or provide a nurse likely superseded strict competitive bidding processes. Contracts for vendors like Pioneer and Maxim were renewed or expanded to ensure service continuity, with the federal ESSER funds acting as a temporary fiscal buffer that masked the high cost of these agreements.

### 3.3 The \"Great Resignation\" and Medical Staffing (Maxim/Amergis)

The pandemic exacerbated the nursing shortage to a critical degree. School nurses were pulled into acute care settings or left the profession entirely due to burnout. For Vancouver Public Schools, which supports a significant population of medically fragile students, this created a vulnerability.

Maxim Healthcare Staffing (later Amergis) became the default solution. The district's need for Registered Nurses (RNs) and Licensed Practical Nurses (LPNs) to accompany students with tracheostomies, ventilators, or seizure disorders is non-negotiable. If a nurse is not available, the student cannot attend school, and the district is in violation of the student's right to a Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE). Maxim/Amergis leveraged this inelastic demand. The data suggests that payments to Maxim increased during the recovery years (2022-2023) as students returned to buildings with heightened health surveillance needs. The rebranding of Maxim's educational division to **Amergis** in 2024 ^8^ reflects the lucrative nature of this specific market segment; by creating a dedicated educational brand, the company signaled its intent to deepen its integration with districts like VPS.

## Section 4: The Fiscal Cliff and Binding Conditions (2023--2025)

As the federal stimulus funds expired in 2023 and 2024, Vancouver Public Schools faced a reckoning. The \"fiscal cliff,\" combined with declining enrollment (a long-term trend exacerbated by the pandemic), pushed the district into a severe deficit.

### 4.1 The \$35 Million Deficit and Austerity Measures

For the 2024-2025 school year, the district announced budget cuts totaling approximately \$35 million.^9^ These cuts were painful and public, involving reductions in library staff, arts programs, and general education support. The district's financial position was precarious enough to warrant scrutiny comparable to districts in \"binding conditions\" (a state designation for insolvency), although VPS managed to avoid the formal designation unlike its peer, Marysville School District.^10^

### 4.2 The Inelasticity of Vendor Payments

Despite these draconian cuts, the flow of funds to Soliant, Pioneer, and Amergis remained robust. This highlights the \"super-creditor\" status of special education vendors.

- **Legal Hierarchy of Spend:** A district can legally increase class sizes (within CBA limits) or reduce custodial cycles. It cannot legally deny a prescribed therapy minute or a nursing treatment. Therefore, the vendor contracts are the last line items to be cut.

- **The Safety Net Reliance:** To mitigate these costs, the district relies heavily on the state's Safety Net program, which reimburses districts for high-cost students (costs exceeding 2.3 times the basic education amount).^2^ Vendor invoices are the primary evidence used to justify these reimbursement claims. Consequently, the high cost of Amergis or Pioneer is partially subsidized by the state, creating a perverse incentive where the district is financially shielded from the full impact of the high bill rates, reducing the urgency to negotiate harder or build internal capacity.

### 4.3 Pioneer Healthcare's Expansion

In the midst of this austerity, Pioneer Healthcare Services secured expanded agreements. Board minutes from February 2025 explicitly reference the approval of an agreement with Pioneer for the 2024-2025 school year to provide special education specialists.^11^ The specific naming of placements (e.g., Felicia Clemmons) in the board approval indicates that these are not just general staffing pools but specific, high-cost placements for which the district has no internal alternative.

Pioneer's recruitment of \"travel\" staff ^12^---offering weekly pay packages of \~\$2,000---creates a significant wage disparity. A district paraeducator might earn \$2,500 a month; a Pioneer travel tech might earn that in 10 days. This disparity, funded by the district, exacerbates the morale crisis among the remaining permanent staff who are surviving the budget cuts, further destabilizing the internal workforce.

## Section 5: Vendor Deep Dives and Forensic Analysis

### 5.1 Soliant Health: The Compliance Architect

Soliant is not merely a staffing agency; it is a compliance infrastructure provider.

- **Service Scope:** The contracts cover Speech Language Pathologists (SLPs), SLP Assistants (SLPAs), School Psychologists, and Sign Language Interpreters.^5^

- **Contract Structure:** Contracts are typically structured as Master Service Agreements (MSAs) with \"addendums\" for specific personnel. This allows the contract value to balloon throughout the year as new needs arise without requiring a new master contract vote.

- **Financial Impact:** The shift to tele-therapy during the pandemic entrenched Soliant. The district likely pays a \"technology fee\" in addition to the staffing rate for the use of the VocoVision platform.

- **Governance Trace:** Soliant contracts are consistently found in the Consent Agenda.^13^ In May 2022, the board approved multiple addendums for specific SLPAs (Land, Babb), indicating a granular reliance on the firm to plug specific holes in the org chart.

### 5.2 Maxim Healthcare / Amergis: The Medical Shield

- **Rebranding Context:** The transition from Maxim to Amergis in 2023-24 separates the K-12 business from Maxim's home health and correctional staffing lines. This is a strategic move to market \"educational expertise.\"

- **Risk Transfer:** A key value proposition of Amergis is liability shielding. If a district-employed nurse makes a medical error, the district is fully liable. If an Amergis nurse makes an error, the agency indemnifies the district. In an era of increasing litigation, the district is paying a premium for this risk transfer.

- **Financial Footprint:** Payments to Maxim/Amergis are embedded in the General Fund vouchers.^15^ The consistent appearance of these vouchers in monthly reports suggests a steady, recurring burn rate, likely associated with 1:1 student assignments that persist for the entire school year.

### 5.3 Pioneer Healthcare: The Travel Specialist

- **Role:** Pioneer appears to function as a \"closer\" for the hardest-to-fill positions, often utilizing the travel staffing model.

- **Cost Implications:** Travel staff are the most expensive form of labor, requiring stipends for housing and meals in addition to wages. The district's use of Pioneer suggests that the local labor market (Clark County/Portland Metro) has been completely exhausted for certain credentials.

- **Recent Activity:** The explicit approval of Pioneer contracts in 2025 ^11^ suggests that despite the budget crisis, the district's internal recruitment capabilities remain insufficient to meet IDEA requirements.

## Section 6: Governance, Oversight, and Transparency

The mechanisms by which these millions of dollars are approved warrant critical examination. The Board of Directors acts as the fiduciary gatekeeper, yet the evidence suggests a pattern of \"governance by consent.\"

### 6.1 The Consent Agenda Mechanism

The Consent Agenda is a parliamentary tool designed to expedite routine business. However, in Vancouver Public Schools, it is routinely used to approve multi-million dollar master contracts with Soliant, Maxim, and Pioneer.^16^

- **The Opacity of Bundling:** By bundling a \$500,000 staffing contract with minutes from the previous meeting and a donation of library books, the specific fiscal impact of the contract is obscured. There is rarely a public presentation detailing the *cumulative* year-over-year cost of these vendors.

- **Public Visibility:** A community member reviewing the agenda would see \"Approve Warrants\" or \"Approve Personnel Report.\" They would not see \"Approve \$2M Spend with Soliant.\" This lack of transparency prevents the public from assessing whether the district is getting value for money or if the \"agency trap\" is deepening.

### 6.2 Audit Findings as Indicators of Control Environment

The State Auditor's Office (SAO) has repeatedly cited the district for procurement weaknesses.

- **Finding 2023-001:** Failure to score bids properly for child nutrition contracts.^6^

- Finding 2022-001: Lack of documentation for ECF purchases.7\
  These findings paint a picture of a district administration that prioritizes execution over process. In the context of staffing, this likely means that when a principal needs a nurse, the central office prioritizes getting the nurse (via Amergis) over rigorously bidding out the contract or analyzing the long-term cost versus a direct hire. The \"emergency\" nature of special education staffing creates a perpetual state of exception where standard procurement rigor is bypassed.

### 6.3 Executive Compensation vs. Vendor Spend

The transparency regarding Superintendent compensation stands in stark contrast to vendor opacity. The salaries of Steve Webb (\$280k+) and Jeff Snell are public knowledge and subject to intense debate.^18^ Yet, the annual spend on Soliant likely dwarfs the Superintendent's salary. The disconnect between the public's focus on administrative salaries and the reality of the district's outsourced operational costs highlights a failure in financial communication. The \"administrative bloat\" often cited by critics is largely invisible, residing in Object 7 payments to out-of-state corporations rather than in the salaries of local administrators.

## Conclusion

The analysis of Vancouver Public Schools\' financial data from 2015 to 2025 reveals a district effectively held captive by the intersection of well-intentioned regulatory reforms and the harsh realities of the labor market. The *McCleary* decision, intended to fully fund education, and the SEBB implementation, designed to protect employee health, inadvertently constructed a financial architecture that rewards outsourcing.

Soliant, Maxim (Amergis), and Pioneer have evolved from supplemental service providers into essential infrastructure for the district. They provide the \"elasticity\" that the rigid state funding model cannot. However, this elasticity comes at a steep price---a premium on labor costs that drains the general fund and contributes to the deficits necessitating the \$35 million in cuts seen in 2025.

The governance of these expenditures remains a critical area of concern. The use of consent agendas to ratify these ongoing liabilities limits public oversight. Furthermore, the reliance on these vendors creates a self-perpetuating cycle: the more the district relies on high-wage agency staff, the harder it becomes to recruit permanent staff at CBA rates, ensuring that the vendors remain indispensable. As the district navigates the post-ESSER fiscal landscape, breaking this \"agency trap\" will require not just budget cuts, but a fundamental re-evaluation of how special education services are staffed, procured, and valued within the public education system.
