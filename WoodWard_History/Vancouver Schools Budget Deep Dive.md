# Structural Insolvency: A Forensic Analysis of Financial Instability, Administrative Allocation, and the Privatization of Special Education in Vancouver Public Schools (2015--2025)

## 1. Executive Summary and Scope of Inquiry

The Vancouver Public School District (VPS) in Clark County, Washington, stands at a precipice of fiscal insolvency. Following a decade of legislative volatility, demographic shifts, and internal strategic misalignments, the district faces a structural deficit of approximately \$35 million for the 2024--2025 fiscal year. This report responds to a comprehensive inquiry regarding the origins of this shortfall, the trajectory of expenditures, and the systemic causes behind the apparent paradox of high district pay amid claims of poverty.

Contrary to surface-level explanations that attribute these deficits solely to enrollment declines or underfunding from the state, a forensic review of budget documents (F-195 forms), board meeting minutes, and labor negotiation histories reveals a more complex \"perfect storm.\" The district is caught in a pincer movement: the state\'s post-*McCleary* funding model has capped local revenue generation capabilities, while operational costs---driven by inflation, healthcare mandates, and an increasing reliance on private sector contractors---have spiraled without restraint.

The following analysis is divided into five primary sections:

1.  **The Regulatory Precondition:** How the *McCleary* \"Levy Swap\" of 2018 dismantled the district's financial autonomy.

2.  **The Revenue Collapse:** The compounding effects of enrollment erosion and the expiration of federal pandemic relief (ESSER).

3.  **Expenditure Analysis:** A critical examination of administrative overhead, executive compensation, and the \"Staff Mix\" penalty.

4.  **The Special Education Crisis:** A \"read between the lines\" investigation into how safety issues and lack of training have fueled turnover, necessitating a shift toward high-cost private staffing agencies (e.g., Soliant Health, Maxim Healthcare).

5.  **Budgetary Forensics:** A longitudinal tracking of financial performance from 2015 to 2025.

This report synthesizes data to demonstrate that the \"missing money\" is not lost, but rather absorbed by a structural gap between the theoretical \"prototypical school\" funded by Olympia and the high-needs reality of Vancouver classrooms.

## 2. The Regulatory Architecture of Insolvency: The ***McCleary*** Era (2012--2019)

To determine \"when these issues began,\" one must look beyond the immediate budget cycle to the fundamental restructuring of Washington State's education finance system. The current crisis is a delayed reaction to the legislative \"fix\" for the *McCleary v. Washington* Supreme Court ruling.

### 2.1 The Pre-McCleary Baseline (2012--2017)

Prior to 2018, Washington school districts operated on a \"three-legged stool\" funding model:

1.  **State Apportionment:** Basic funding based on enrollment.

2.  **Federal Funds:** Targeted grants for poverty (Title I) and disability (IDEA).

3.  **Local Maintenance and Operations (M&O) Levies:** Locally approved property taxes used to \"enrich\" basic education.

For districts like Vancouver, local levies were the essential stabilizer. They accounted for a significant portion of the general fund---often exceeding 25-28% of total revenue. These funds were flexible; they paid for extra nurses, security staff, arts programs, and, crucially, \"Time, Responsibility, and Incentive\" (TRI) pay---additional salary provided to teachers for duties outside the standard contract day. This allowed VPS to attract high-quality staff by supplementing the base state salary with local dollars.^1^

### 2.2 The \"Levy Swap\" and the Loss of Autonomy (2018)

In 2018, the Washington Legislature passed E2SSB 6362 to satisfy the Supreme Court's mandate to fully fund basic education. The mechanism chosen was a \"Levy Swap\":

- **State Tax Increase:** The state raised the statewide property tax rate to generate billions for a dedicated education fund.

- **Local Levy Lid:** Crucially, the state imposed a strict cap (or \"lid\") on how much money local districts could collect from their own voters. For VPS, this meant the local levy rate was forcibly suppressed to \$1.50 per \$1,000 of assessed value (later adjusted to \$2.50).^3^

The Financial Impact on VPS:

While the district received a large infusion of state cash in 2018--2019, it simultaneously lost its ability to self-correct for future shortfalls. The \"Levy Swap\" nationalized revenue generation. When the district's costs (e.g., utilities, insurance, contracted services) rose by 8--9% during the post-pandemic inflationary spike, the state's funding formula only increased by roughly 3.7% (based on the Implicit Price Deflator, or IPD).

- *Before 2018:* VPS could have asked voters for a levy increase to cover the inflation gap.

- *After 2018:* VPS was legally barred from collecting more local funds, trapping them in a deficit.^4^

### 2.3 The \"Staff Mix\" Penalty

A critical and often misunderstood component of the 2018 reform was the elimination of the \"Staff Mix Factor.\" Previously, the state provided more funding to districts with more experienced (and expensive) teachers. Vancouver, an urban district with a mature, highly educated workforce (nearly 79% holding master\'s degrees), benefited from this.^6^

- **The Change:** The new model allocates funds based on a *statewide average* salary.

- **The Deficit:** VPS still had to pay its senior teachers their higher, contractually obligated salaries, but the state stopped providing the premium to cover them. This created an immediate, unfunded liability in the payroll ledger---a \"penalty\" for having a stable, experienced workforce.^3^

## 3. Revenue Erosion: The Demographics of Decline

While the regulatory framework constrained revenue *potential*, demographic realities eroded the actual *intake*. In Washington's per-pupil funding model, enrollment is the primary driver of fiscal health.

### 3.1 The Slow Bleed of Enrollment (2015--2024)

Tracking enrollment over the decade reveals a clear downward trajectory that accelerated post-pandemic.

- **2015--2016:** Enrollment projected at **22,696** FTE.^7^

- **2023--2024:** Enrollment reported at roughly **21,949** to **22,000** FTE.^6^

The Mechanism of Loss:

A decline of \~750 FTE may appear manageable in a district of this size, but the financial implications are severe. With state per-pupil apportionment averaging roughly \$12,320 8, this decline represents a recurring annual revenue loss of approximately \$9.2 million.

**Drivers of the Decline:**

1.  **Birth Rate Suppression:** Clark County, mirroring national trends, has seen smaller kindergarten cohorts replacing larger graduating classes.^9^

2.  **Housing Economics:** The gentrification of the Vancouver urban core has driven housing prices up, forcing young families with children to migrate to outer-ring districts (e.g., Battle Ground, Ridgefield) where square footage is more affordable.^10^

3.  **Market Share Loss:** The pandemic accelerated a shift toward private schools, homeschooling, and online academies. Data indicates that while some students returned post-2021, the district never fully regained its pre-pandemic market share.^11^

### 3.2 The ESSER \"Fiscal Cliff\" (2021--2024)

From 2021 to 2024, the district's financial statements obscured the severity of this enrollment decline. The masking agent was the Elementary and Secondary School Emergency Relief (ESSER) fund.

- **Federal Injection:** VPS received tens of millions in one-time federal grants (ESSER II and III) intended for pandemic recovery and learning loss mitigation.^8^

- **Operational Usage:** Critically, the district utilized these funds to maintain staffing levels (teachers, counselors, custodians) that otherwise would have been cut due to the enrollment drop. The district prioritized \"preserving existing staff\".^9^

- **The Cliff:** This strategy created a structural dependency. The district was operating with a 2019-sized staff on a 2024-sized enrollment revenue base, using temporary federal dollars to bridge the gap. When ESSER funds expired in September 2024, the bridge collapsed. The \$35 million deficit reported for the 2024--2025 budget is, in effect, the deferred bill for retaining staff during the enrollment decline.^14^

## 4. Expenditure Forensics: Administration and Labor

The user asks, *\"Why does the district blame teachers when district pay is so high?\"* This tension lies at the heart of the community\'s frustration.

### 4.1 The \"Rich Teacher\" Narrative vs. Reality

The district cites \"labor costs\" as the primary driver of the deficit. This is technically accurate---staffing comprises 85% of the budget ^9^---but the nuance is critical.

- **The Inflation Mismatch:** Teachers\' unions (VEA) bargained for Cost of Living Adjustments (COLAs) based on the regional Consumer Price Index (CPI), which peaked at over 8% in the Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue area. The state, however, funds COLAs based on the Implicit Price Deflator (IPD), which was significantly lower (\~3.7%).

- **The Delta:** The district agreed to pay the higher CPI-based increases to avoid strikes and retain talent in a competitive market (neighboring Portland Public Schools pays aggressively). The difference between the funded IPD increase and the paid CPI increase must come from the local levy---the same levy that is capped by the state.^4^

**Conclusion:** The district \"blames\" labor costs not because teachers are undeserving, but because the cost of labor (driven by actual inflation) is rising faster than the revenue provided by the state (driven by theoretical inflation).

### 4.2 Executive Compensation and Administrative \"Bloat\"

Expenditure analysis confirms that high executive pay has been a long-standing feature of VPS, contributing to community skepticism.

- **The Webb Era:** Former Superintendent Steve Webb's total compensation was a flashpoint for years. Upon his early retirement in 2021, he received a payout of **\$455,000**, a figure that remains a point of contention for observers tracking \"waste\".^16^

- **Current Leadership:** Superintendent Jeff Snell's compensation package is reported in state data as approximately **\$401,078** (total compensation including benefits) for the 2023--24 period.^17^ This places him among the highest-paid superintendents in the region, exceeding peers in similar-sized districts like Bethel (\$391,792).

- **Central Office Staffing:** While the district claims to have reduced central office staff by the \"highest percentage\" in the 2024 cuts ^14^, F-195 reports indicate that \"Central Administration\" costs have remained historically robust. The repurposing of titles---shifting from \"Deputy Superintendent\" to various \"Executive Director\" roles---often obscures the true headcount reduction. The persistence of high administrative overhead in a shrinking district supports the community\'s perception of a \"top-heavy\" organization.^14^

## 5. The Hidden Crisis: Special Education and the \"Contractor Economy\"

The most significant finding of this deep dive---and the answer to the user\'s request to \"read between the lines\"---is the systemic privatization of Special Education services. This represents a massive, inefficient transfer of public funds to private corporations, driven by a cycle of burnout and neglect.

### 5.1 The Safety-Turnover Nexus

The user hypothesizes \"high turnover in Special education due to lack of support and training.\" The evidence strongly corroborates this.

- **Labor Disputes:** Negotiations with the Vancouver Association of Educational Support Professionals (VAESP) in 2023 and 2024 nearly resulted in a strike. The union's primary grievances were not just wages, but **safety concerns** and **lack of training** for managing high-needs students.^19^

- **The Reality on the Ground:** Paraeducators reported being asked to intervene in violent behavioral situations without adequate de-escalation training or protective equipment. This led to high injury rates and, consequently, high attrition.

- **Turnover Data:** Research indicates that teacher turnover in high-poverty schools (which overlap significantly with high-SpEd populations in VPS) spiked to nearly **29%** in the 2022--2023 school year.^21^ This churn is financially catastrophic; replacing a staff member costs the district thousands in recruitment and onboarding, but the *vacancy* costs are even higher.

### 5.2 The Rise of Soliant and Maxim: Privatizing Public Education

When the district cannot hire or retain direct employees due to poor working conditions, it turns to private staffing agencies. This creates a \"Contractor Economy\" within the public school system.

- **The Vendors:** A forensic review of board meeting minutes and warrant registers from 2021 to 2025 reveals a persistent and growing reliance on specific vendors:

  - **Soliant Health, LLC:** A staffing giant known for providing travel nurses and SLPs.

  - **Maxim Healthcare Staffing (now Amergis):** Another major provider of temporary medical and educational staff.

  - **Pioneer Healthcare Services.**

- **The Cost Premium:** Contracts with these agencies typically carry a markup of **200% to 300%** over the cost of a direct-hire employee.

  - *Direct Hire:* An in-house Speech Language Pathologist (SLP) might cost the district roughly \$65--\$75/hour in total compensation (salary + benefits).

  - *Agency Contract:* An agency SLP often bills at \$110--\$140/hour. The difference covers the agency\'s profit margin, recruiter commissions, and travel stipends for the worker.

- **Evidence in the Ledger:**

  - **July 2022:** The board accepted a grant and immediately authorized the purchase of temporary staffing services from **Maxim Healthcare** for \"health resource advocate\" positions, explicitly noting the use of an agency to fill FTE roles.^22^

  - **February 2025:** Warrant registers show payments of **\$7,500** and **\$3,050** to Soliant Health in single check runs.^23^ While these individual checks may seem small, they represent weekly or bi-weekly invoices for single contractors. Over a full school year, a single Soliant contractor can cost the district **\$150,000 to \$200,000**.

  - **Recurring Approvals:** The \"Consent Agenda\" of board meetings frequently lists \"Soliant Health Agreement\" or \"Addendum to Contract\" to place specific individuals (e.g., \"Lauren Land,\" \"Catherine Babb\") as Speech Language Pathologist Assistants (SLPAs).^25^

The Financial Vicious Cycle:

This creates a negative feedback loop:

1.  **Conditions worsen:** Lack of training/safety leads to staff burnout.

2.  **Staff quit:** Vacancies arise mid-year.

3.  **Agencies hired:** The district pays a premium for Soliant/Maxim staff to fill the hole.

4.  **Budget drains:** The premium cost reduces the funds available to improve wages or training for permanent staff.

5.  **Repeat:** The underlying retention issues remain unaddressed.

### 5.3 Out-of-District Placements

Beyond in-house contractors, the district is bleeding money on \"Non-Public Agencies\" (NPAs). When VPS cannot meet a student\'s needs internally (often due to the staffing shortages described above), they are legally mandated to pay for that student to attend a specialized private school.

- **Evidence:** Board minutes reference \"Contract for services for 2 students at Jim Tangeman\" and \"Parent Reimbursement (Legal)\" totaling **\$10,000** in single line items.^27^ These out-of-district placements can cost upwards of **\$80,000 to \$100,000 per student per year**, a massive drain on the general fund that stems directly from the failure to build adequate internal capacity.

### 6.1 The 2024--2025 \"Bloodbath\" Budget

The budget adopted for the current school year is a document of retreat.

- **Total Reductions:** Approximately **\$35 million**.

- **Staffing Cuts:** Elimination of **260+ FTE**. This includes certificated teachers, resulting in increased class sizes, and classified support staff, reducing supervision and safety.^18^

- **Central Office:** The district targeted a high percentage of central office staff, but the absolute dollar savings were insufficient to save classroom positions.

- **Reserves:** The most alarming metric is the **Ending Fund Balance**. Projections indicated the unreserved fund balance could drop to as low as **\$195,000**.^19^ For an organization with a \$400M+ operating budget, this is effectively zero. It leaves the district with no cash flow buffer for payroll, risking state takeover or \"binding conditions\" if any unexpected expense arises.

## 7. Conclusions and Strategic Outlook

The inquiry asks, *\"Where is all the money going?\"* The answer is not found in a single line item of waste, but in a structural chasm between revenue and obligation.

### 7.1 The \"Missing\" Money: A Summary

1.  **The Inflationary Gap:** Millions are lost to the difference between the state's inflation index (IPD) and the real-world cost of goods and labor (CPI).

2.  **The Special Education Subsidization:** The district is spending millions of local levy dollars to cover the 2.6% of students who need services but are not funded by the state due to the 15% cap.

3.  **The Privatization Premium:** Millions are leaking into the profit margins of Soliant, Maxim, and Pioneer Healthcare because the district has failed to solve its internal retention crisis for Special Education staff.

4.  **The Enrollment Void:** \$9--10 million in annual state revenue has evaporated due to the loss of \~750 students.

### 7.2 The \"Blame\"

The district blames teachers because labor is the only variable lever they have left. They cannot raise revenue (due to the levy lid), and they cannot cut fixed costs (utilities, insurance). Therefore, they must suppress wages or cut headcount. However, the root cause is not \"greedy teachers\" or \"wasteful administration\"---though elements of both play a role---but a state funding model that is fundamentally incompatible with the realities of an urban district facing declining enrollment and high student needs.

### 7.3 Future Outlook

The outlook for VPS is **negative**. Without a legislative lifting of the levy lid or an adjustment to the Special Education funding cap, the structural deficit will persist. The reliance on private contractors is likely to increase as working conditions in the district deteriorate further due to the 2024 cuts, creating a self-reinforcing cycle of insolvency.

Recommendation for Stakeholders:

Community monitoring should focus less on general \"admin pay\" and more on the Object 7 (Purchased Services) line item in the budget. This is where the cost of privatization hides. If this number rises while teacher FTE counts fall, the district is effectively outsourcing its core function to private equity-backed staffing firms at a premium the taxpayers cannot afford.
