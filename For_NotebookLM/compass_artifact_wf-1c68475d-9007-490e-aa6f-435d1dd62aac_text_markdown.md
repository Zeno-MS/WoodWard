# Investigative toolkit for examining Vancouver Public Schools

**Vancouver Public Schools faces an accelerating fiscal crisis — depleted reserves, emergency state borrowing, and a $20.15 million projected deficit for 2026-27 — creating a rich investigative landscape.** Washington's Public Records Act provides powerful tools to examine administrative decisions, salary data, and contract approvals. This report assembles the legal frameworks, data sources, personnel backgrounds, and journalistic methodology needed to build a multi-part investigative series targeting fiscal mismanagement and administrative staffing decisions at VPS. The district's financial trajectory — three consecutive years of emergency apportionment advances, borrowing from the Clark County Treasurer, and OSPI financial warnings — provides the factual foundation for a compelling public-interest investigation.

## 1. Washington's Public Records Act opens nearly every door

The Washington Public Records Act (RCW 42.56) is among the strongest open-records laws in the nation and applies fully to school districts. Its foundational principle: **all public records must be disclosed unless a specific statutory exemption applies**, and exemptions are narrowly construed. A "public record" encompasses any writing — including emails, text messages, and electronic communications — relating to the conduct of government.

**What personnel records are public.** The critical exemption at RCW 42.56.230(3) protects only "personal information" in employee files where disclosure would violate the employee's "right to privacy." This is not a categorical exemption for personnel files. The privacy test requires that disclosure be both (1) highly offensive to a reasonable person AND (2) not of legitimate concern to the public. This is a high bar. Attorney General Opinion 1973 No. 4 directly addressed school districts, holding that "all records of a school district pertaining to the salaries of its employees and their payroll deductions must be made available for inspection and copying."

The following records are unambiguously public and must be disclosed upon request:

- **Employee names, job titles, salary and compensation amounts, and dates of employment**
- **Employment contracts** (including superintendent agreements, per RCW 28A.405.210)
- **Organizational charts, position descriptions, and staffing tables**
- **Board meeting minutes, agendas, and audio recordings** (RCW 42.30.035 requires school districts to audio-record open sessions)
- **Work email communications** on government accounts relating to government business (confirmed by *Nissen v. Pierce County*, 2015, extending to personal devices used for official purposes)

What is protected: residential addresses, phone numbers, SSNs, emergency contacts, routine performance evaluations (*Dawson v. Daly*, 1993), and application materials for non-elective positions. However, substantiated misconduct records are not exempt (*Cowles Publishing Co. v. State Patrol*, 1988), and performance reviews of chief executives (superintendents, by analogy from *Spokane Research & Defense Fund v. City of Spokane*, 2000) may be disclosable.

**Response timeline and enforcement.** Agencies must respond within **5 business days** with records, an acknowledgment and time estimate, a request for clarification, or a denial citing specific exemptions (RCW 42.56.520). Penalties for non-compliance include mandatory attorney fees for prevailing requestors and daily penalties up to **$100 per day** the requestor was denied access (RCW 42.56.550). Notably, requestors need not identify themselves or state their purpose (RCW 42.56.080).

**Executive session records.** Under RCW 42.30.110(1)(g), boards may enter executive session to evaluate employee qualifications or review performance — but all final actions on hiring, salary-setting, or discipline must occur in open public meetings. While executive session minutes are not specifically required to be kept, if notes exist, they may be subject to PRA disclosure. The purpose of each executive session must be entered into the regular meeting minutes. This means the journalist can track when personnel decisions were discussed and compare that timeline to final actions taken in open session.

**Email as a powerful investigative tool.** The *Nissen v. Pierce County* (2015) and *West v. Vermillion* (2016) decisions established that text messages and emails on personal accounts are public records if they relate to public business. Practically, a journalist can request all emails of named administrators on district accounts relating to specific topics, and can also request communications on personal accounts if they allege the messages concern district business.

## 2. OSPI S-275 data provides a searchable salary census

The OSPI S-275 personnel reporting system is an annual census of every certificated and classified employee in every Washington school district, captured as of October 1 each year. It is the single most powerful publicly available data tool for investigating school district staffing and compensation.

**Data fields available for every employee** include: full name, job title/duty code (with specific codes for superintendent, deputy superintendent, director/supervisor, principal, teacher, etc.), program code, FTE status, **total final salary**, insurance benefits, mandatory benefits, degree level, years of experience, and building assignment. The duty code system is granular — codes 11-25 cover administrative staff, 31-49 cover instructional staff, and suffixes distinguish base contracts from supplemental pay.

**Searching by name is straightforward.** OSPI explicitly states that PDF files can be searched using Ctrl+F for individual names ("Doe, John") or certificate numbers. Excel and Microsoft Access database formats allow sorting, filtering, and querying by any field. The **2024-25 preliminary S-275 database** was posted February 14, 2025, and the **2023-24 final database** was posted December 13, 2024.

**Key access points** for this data:

The primary portal is **https://ospi.k12.wa.us/safs-data-files**, which contains Access databases, Excel files, and PDFs organized by district. The interactive salary search tool at **https://fiscal.wa.gov/K12/K12Salaries** allows filtering by district and position, with a downloadable Excel file at **https://fiscal.wa.gov/K12/K12AllStaffSalaryData.xlsx**. District-level comparison data is available at **https://fiscal.wa.gov/K12/K12AverageSalariesDistrict.aspx**. Personnel Summary Reports with aggregate averages by duty type are published at **https://ospi.k12.wa.us/policy-funding/school-apportionment/school-publications/personnel-summary-reports**.

**Vancouver School District #37** (NCES ID 5309270) enrolls approximately 22,000 students across 34 schools with about 2,504 FTE staff. The district's S-275 data can be filtered using the district name or county-district code 06-037. Third-party databases like GovSalaries.com also republish this data in searchable formats. The School Data Project at **https://schooldataproject.com/vancouver%20school%20district** provides historical compensation charts broken down by administrative, principal, and teacher categories.

## 3. Brett Blechschmidt's 30-year trajectory from ESD 112 to interim superintendent

Brett Blechschmidt spent **19 years at Educational Service District 112** before joining VPS around 2014. While his specific ESD 112 titles were not publicly documented, his subsequent financial roles at VPS and the fact that ESD 112 specializes in business/finance support services for districts strongly suggest a fiscal management background. In 2025, he received the **WASA Twenty-Year Award** honoring administrators with two decades of service to public school districts.

At VPS, his titles evolved from **Chief Fiscal Officer** (2014–circa 2019) to **Associate Superintendent and Chief Financial Officer** to **Associate Superintendent and Chief Operating Officer**. During his tenure as CFO, VPS received **11 consecutive years of clean audit reports** from the Washington State Auditor's Office. However, as early as 2019, he was warning the board about structural deficits, presenting a projected $11.44 million shortfall for 2019-20 and noting that "the only way we can accomplish a break-even budget... is we have to restrict expenditure growth, which we all know means reductions in programs and services."

**On May 1, 2025**, the VPS Board appointed Blechschmidt interim superintendent for 2025-26, replacing Dr. Jeff Snell, who left to lead WASA. Both Blechschmidt and Interim Associate Superintendent Janell Ephraim agreed they would not be candidates for the permanent position.

**The fiscal crisis deepened dramatically under his interim leadership.** In August 2025, the board unanimously approved a budget drawing the fund balance from $7 million to just **$195,180** — avoiding layoffs but eliminating virtually all reserves. In November, VPS requested a **$21.4 million emergency apportionment advance** from OSPI — the largest such request any Washington district has ever made — and received only $8.7 million. On December 9, 2025, Blechschmidt announced a projected **$20.15 million deficit for 2026-27** and the board approved borrowing from the Clark County Treasurer's Office to cover cash-flow shortfalls. Clark County Treasurer **Alishia Topper** publicly warned that "county treasurers are not designed to provide long, ongoing operating loans to school districts."

Board meeting presentations by Blechschmidt are accessible through the VPS BoardDocs portal at **https://go.boarddocs.com/wa/vpswa/Board.nsf/Public**, including budget presentations from 2015-16 through the current year.

## 5. Professional services contracts operate in a regulatory gap

**The most investigatively significant finding in VPS purchasing policy** is that professional service contracts — including staffing agency agreements — are **not subject to the competitive bidding requirements** of RCW 28A.335.190. That statute applies to furniture, supplies, equipment, buildings, and construction. The Municipal Research and Services Center (MRSC) confirms that "most local governments in Washington have no statutory requirements for personal services contracts" and that the state has not established bid limits, advertising, or competitive requirements for purchased services.

VPS Board Policy 6220 (Bid or Proposal Requirements) follows the WSSDA model, with standard thresholds: no competitive bidding required under **$40,000** for goods; informal quotes from three sources required for $40,000-$75,000; formal sealed bids for purchases exceeding **$75,000**. Policy 6210 (Purchasing: Authorization and Control) authorizes the superintendent to direct expenditures within the approved annual budget, with staff who obligate the district without proper authorization potentially held personally liable.

**The critical investigative angle** is that professional services — potentially including staffing agencies, consulting contracts, and similar arrangements — can be approved by the superintendent or designee without board approval or competitive bidding, as long as they fall within the approved budget and any locally-established threshold (commonly $75,000-$100,000 at comparable districts). The exact VPS-specific dollar threshold for superintendent authority versus board approval could not be confirmed due to BoardDocs access limitations and should be obtained via a targeted public records request.

Board meeting minutes accessible through BoardDocs (**https://go.boarddocs.com/wa/vpswa/Board.nsf/Public**) are essential for tracking which contracts were approved by the board versus which were executed under delegated authority. **Warrant registers** (expenditure records approved by the board) and **purchase order files** with authorizing signatures would reveal who specifically approved each transaction. The VPS Purchasing Department, managed by **David Hurt** at 360-313-1240, handles bid management and contracted services administration.

## 6. The fiscal crisis timeline reveals a district in freefall

VPS has experienced a cascading financial deterioration that provides the factual backbone for an investigative series. The key developments form a clear chronological narrative:

**OSPI issued financial warnings** for both 2022-23 (health score 1.90) and 2023-24 (health score **1.50**, well below the 1.75 threshold). VPS is the only Clark County district to receive these warnings. The health score measures fund balance-to-revenue ratio, expenditure-to-revenue ratio, cash on hand, and budget planning adequacy.

**The district has requested emergency apportionment advances three consecutive years**: $15.5 million in 2023-24, $18.5 million in 2024-25, and $21.4 million in 2025-26 (the largest request OSPI has ever received from any district, of which only $8.7 million was approved). After OSPI's partial denial, the board authorized borrowing from the Clark County Treasurer on December 9, 2025. A **$31.4 million special education deficit** accumulated over five years is a major structural driver.

**The superintendent search concluded on January 21, 2026**, when the board selected **Dr. Ricardo "Rocky" Torres-Morales**, currently Associate Superintendent at Seattle Public Schools, to begin July 1, 2026. He was chosen from four finalists after a process managed by Ray and Associates that attracted 40 applicants nationwide. Torres-Morales began his career as a bilingual special education teacher in New York City before serving as a school administrator in Cleveland.

Other recent developments include the **Flex Academy closure** (announced December 2025, prompting a student walkout on December 18), a **Title IX investigation** opened by the U.S. Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights on January 14-15, 2026, and the November 2025 board election in which incumbent Wendy Smith won reelection. The current board comprises Wendy Smith (Chair), Kathy Decker, Kyle Sproul, Sandra Zavala-Ortega, and Tracie Barrows.

**OSPI's escalation framework** is a key factor to monitor: under WAC 392-123-060, if a district cannot produce a balanced budget, OSPI can impose "binding conditions." Two consecutive years without a satisfactory financial plan can trigger a Financial Oversight Committee with authority to recommend enhanced oversight, appointment of a special administrator, or even district dissolution. VPS has not yet been placed on binding conditions, but the trajectory — three years of emergency advances, depleted reserves, and growing deficits — tracks toward that threshold.

## 7. How to structure this as a multi-part investigative series

Award-winning school district investigations share a consistent architecture: they pair **rigorous data analysis with compelling human narratives**, release findings across **3-5 focused installments**, and time publication to moments when decision-makers can act.

**The gold standard is the Tampa Bay Times' "Failure Factories" (2015 Pulitzer)**, which spent a year developing findings before dividing them into installments, each functioning as a standalone story and a chapter in the larger narrative. The team filed a records request for a dataset they'd never seen before but that logic dictated must exist — a technique directly applicable to investigating VPS fiscal decisions. Other models include the Philadelphia Inquirer's "Assault on Learning" (seven parts, 300+ interviews, 2012 Pulitzer for Public Service) and the Chicago Tribune/ProPublica "The Price Kids Pay" (2022 IRE Award), which built a unique database from hundreds of records requests.

**Recommended series architecture for VPS:**

The first installment should present the broadest, most dramatic finding — likely the scope of the fiscal crisis and the emergency borrowing arrangement. Part 2 should examine specific administrative decisions and who made them, using employment contracts, delegation-of-authority policies, and board meeting records to trace decision-making chains. Part 3 should focus on the personnel dimension — HR staffing decisions, professional service contracts, and the regulatory gap that allows significant spending without competitive bidding. A fourth installment could examine the impact on students and staff, including the 262 positions cut in 2024, the Flex Academy closure, and declining services. A final installment should assess accountability and the incoming superintendent's challenges.

**Release strategy principles.** Align installments with board meeting cycles (typically twice monthly) so officials face public questioning immediately after each story. Publish midweek for maximum engagement. Create "social sidebars" — digestible, shareable visualizations of salary data, budget trends, and spending comparisons — between installments. Build a comprehensive fact-checking file organized by assertion. Seek comment from all subjects via email with "seeking your comment" subject lines, which both fulfills journalistic ethics and ensures officials learn findings before publication.

**Legal protection is robust.** School superintendents and high-ranking administrators are "public officials" under *New York Times v. Sullivan*, requiring proof of "actual malice" for defamation claims. Fair reporting privilege protects accurate reporting on board proceedings. All assertions should be backed by documents or multiple on-record sources.

## Conclusion: a systematic records strategy can build a compelling case

This investigation has several structural advantages. Washington's Public Records Act provides near-universal access to salary data, employment contracts, email communications, and financial records. The OSPI S-275 database offers a searchable, employee-level compensation census that can immediately quantify administrative staffing decisions and salary trends. The regulatory gap for professional services contracts — not subject to competitive bidding — is an underreported dimension that could reveal how significant spending decisions were made without public scrutiny.

**The most productive initial public records requests** should target: (1) employment contracts for Blechschmidt and Fish specifically; (2) organizational charts and staffing tables for the past five years; (3) all professional service contracts and staffing agency agreements over $25,000; (4) warrant registers showing expenditure approval chains; (5) board policies 6210 and 6220 with any administrative procedures; and (6) email communications between named administrators regarding budget decisions and contracted services. The 5-business-day response clock starts immediately upon receipt, and non-compliance triggers mandatory attorney fees and daily penalties.

The incoming superintendent's July 1, 2026 start date creates a natural publication window: releasing findings before Torres-Morales takes office informs the community about challenges he inherits and creates pressure for structural reform. The financial trajectory — from 11 consecutive clean audits under Blechschmidt's fiscal leadership to emergency state borrowing and a $20 million projected deficit — demands public explanation of what changed and who made the decisions that led to this outcome.
