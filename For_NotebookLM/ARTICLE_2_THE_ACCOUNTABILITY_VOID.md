# THE ACCOUNTABILITY VOID

## How Vancouver Public Schools Auto-Renewed a $30 Million Corporate Pipeline While Classrooms Burned

*By WoodWard | Project WoodWard — Investigative Series, Part 2 of 3*

---

## I. THE LEDE

It takes less than ninety seconds for the Vancouver Public Schools Board of Directors to spend millions of dollars. The mechanism is called the "Consent Agenda"—a block of administrative items presented as routine, voted on as a single package, and almost never debated. It is where the district approves minor facility repairs, routine instructional material adoptions, and the surplus of old buses.

It is also where the district constructed a $30 million pipeline to a single private healthcare staffing corporation.

In Part 1 of this series, *The Austerity Paradox*, this investigation documented how Vancouver Public Schools (VPS) paid $11.89 million to private staffing agencies in the 2024-25 fiscal year—the exact same year it declared a $35 million deficit and eliminated 260 district positions. The lion's share, $10.97 million, went to Amergis Healthcare Staffing, a company formerly known as Maxim Healthcare Services before it rebranded in the wake of a $150 million federal fraud settlement. 

Agency spending now consumes 25.13% of the district's entire Purchased Services (Object 7) budget. It represents nearly 3 cents of every single public dollar spent by VPS—across all buildings, all utilities, all salaries, all supplies, and all programs. 

But a $30 million corporate pipeline does not build itself. It relies on the deliberate or severely negligent failure of human resources and financial oversight, aided by a Board of Directors that rubber-stamps multi-million dollar contracts without public scrutiny.

This is the story of the accountability void. It is the story of how the district's central administration expanded its own ranks, protected its own salaries, and quietly surrendered the district's special education labor force to a private vendor—all while looking parents in the eye and telling them there was simply no money left.

---

## II. THE CONSENT AGENDA AND PROCUREMENT RED FLAGS

Between 2016 and 2025, the Vancouver Public Schools Board of Directors authorized 82 separate contract actions tied to Maxim, Amergis, and Soliant Health. 

At no point in those nine years did the board materials presented to the public note that Amergis was the successor entity to Maxim Healthcare Services. At no point did the board publicly debate whether outsourcing special education staff at a 15% to 30% premium was fiscally responsible. At no point did the board publicly acknowledge that a company with a criminal fraud conviction on its corporate record had become one of the district's largest unquestioned expenditures.

In Washington State, public school districts are bound by RCW 28A.335.190, which requires competitive bidding for purchases over $75,000. However, "professional services"—which includes staffing agencies—exist in a gray area, often exempted from strict competitive bidding requirements. 

But exemption from state bidding laws does not equate to exemption from basic fiduciary duty. 

By utilizing the consent agenda for these contracts year after year, the district's central administration ensured that the growth from a $377,585 vendor relationship in 2020-21 to an $11.89 million vendor relationship in 2024-25 occurred entirely in the dark. The contracts were structured with auto-renewing terms and uncapped rate increases. The board simply approved the warrants each month.

Good governance requires that vendor relationships of this magnitude be periodically re-bid, heavily scrutinized, and actively managed. Instead, VPS treated a $30 million six-year expenditure as routine administrative paperwork.

## III. THE HR FAILURE AND THE SHELL GAME

To understand how the district became so dependent on Amergis, one must look at the failure to hire and retain internal staff.

Special education (SPED) teachers, speech-language pathologists (SLPs), and paraeducators are demanding roles. They require competitive compensation and robust administrative support. When a district fails to provide either, positions go vacant, burnout accelerates, and turnover spikes. 

Instead of aggressively recruiting and compensating internal SPED staff to close the gap, the district's Human Resources department effectively surrendered the function to the private market. Every vacant position became another billing opportunity for Amergis, which charges the district 45% to 118% more for an SLP than what a fully-loaded internal hire would cost. 

While the SPED labor force eroded, the central administration flourished. 

Prior to the massive cuts in 2024-25, the central office had ballooned to 480.63 Full-Time Equivalents (FTEs). When the $35 million deficit forced the district's hand, VPS announced a reduction of 47.57 FTEs from the central office. 

But a closer look reveals a shell game. The cuts were heavily concentrated among support personnel, not upper-level executives. The administrators charting the course remained untouched. Executive salaries continued their upward trajectory, even as the district claimed poverty to the teachers' union and the public.

Consider the compensation of top administrators during the austerity period:
- **William Oman**, District Administrator: $213,989 (FY 23-24) to $221,754 (FY 24-25)
- **Janell Ephraim**, District Administrator: $212,226 (FY 23-24) to $219,432 (FY 24-25)
- **James Gray**, District Administrator: $200,081 (FY 23-24) to $213,966 (FY 24-25)
- **Jeff Fish**, HR Executive: $219,087 (FY 23-24) to $217,873 (FY 24-25)

The leadership team that failed to retain internal staff, oversaw the massive expansion of the Amergis pipeline, and presented the resulting fiscal crisis as an unavoidable tragedy, suffered no systemic financial consequences.

## IV. THE CFO WHO BECAME SUPERINTENDENT

The financial collapse of Vancouver Public Schools was not an overnight hurricane; it was a slow-moving train wreck visible years in advance—and the person at the financial controls never changed.

**Brett Blechschmidt** served as the district's Chief Financial Officer for nearly a decade. During that entire period—spanning the modest $377,585 in agency spending in 2020-21 through its explosion to $11.89 million by 2024-25—Blechschmidt controlled the district's budget narrative. He presented the F-195 budget documents to the board. He oversaw the Object 7 line items that concealed the staffing agency growth. He managed the inter-fund borrowing that kept the district solvent when the fund balance dropped to $195,180.

The expiration of $64.5 million in federal COVID-19 relief (ESSER funds) was known as the "ESSER Fiscal Cliff." Finance officers across the country warned of the danger of using one-time federal dollars to plug ongoing operational holes. Yet under Blechschmidt's financial stewardship, VPS drove straight off the cliff. The district relied on emergency inter-fund borrowing for three consecutive years just to make payroll, placing VPS perilously close to "Binding Conditions"—the state's version of receivership, where OSPI takes direct control of a district's finances.

When Superintendent Jeff Snell resigned, the board did not conduct a national search for new leadership. It promoted Brett Blechschmidt to **Interim Superintendent**.

The implications are extraordinary. The person who served as the district's top financial officer during the entire period of unchecked agency spending growth—the person whose office processed every Amergis warrant, approved every Object 7 budget line, and presented every austerity narrative to the board—was rewarded with the top job.

Blechschmidt now controls both the budget and the organizational response to questions about the budget. The CFO who oversaw the financial architecture that produced the $30 million agency pipeline is now the superintendent responsible for explaining it.

Meanwhile, **Jeff Fish**, the district's HR executive compensated at $217,873 in FY 24-25, presided over the department that failed to recruit and retain internal SPED staff—the very failure that drove the district's dependency on Amergis. Every unfilled SLP position, every vacant paraeducator slot, every SPED teacher who left for the private sector represented both a recruitment failure under Fish's watch and another billing opportunity for the agency pipeline that Blechschmidt's office funded without question.

The contrast is inescapable. The administrators who built and funded the pipeline suffered no consequences. The CFO was promoted. The HR director kept his salary. The 260 classroom employees who lost their jobs had no such protection.

---

## V. THE ACCOUNTABILITY VOID

When a public school district finds itself wiring $11.89 million a year to a single vendor cluster while class sizes explode and libraries close, the public deserves answers.

They deserve to know if the district's internal HR department ever ran continuous, aggressive recruitment campaigns before outsourcing the work.
They deserve to know exactly which board meeting formally authorized the transition of tens of millions of dollars from Maxim Healthcare to Amergis.
They deserve to know why a contract of this magnitude was never subjected to a public, competitive bidding process.

Most importantly, they deserve to know who is accountable.

But in the current structure of Vancouver Public Schools, accountability is what happens to teachers when test scores drop. It is what happens to paraeducators when the budget runs dry. It is not, apparently, what happens to the executives who built the pipeline, or the board that approved it ninety seconds at a time.
