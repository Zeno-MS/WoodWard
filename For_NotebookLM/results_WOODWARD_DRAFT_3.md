# THE STRANGULATION OF PUBLIC EDUCATION:
## How Vancouver Public Schools Funded a $28 Million Corporate Pipeline While Cutting 260 Classrooms

*By WoodWard*
*Draft 3 — Investigative Report*

---

Entering the 2024-25 school year, the narrative from Vancouver Public Schools administration was unrelenting: a $35 million structural deficit would force painful austerity. Middle school librarians were eliminated. Interventionists were reassigned. More than 260 public education positions were slashed from the district’s budget.

The stated reason was declining enrollment and a squeeze in state funding. 

The unstated reality—buried deep within the district's "Object 7" accounting lines—was that the Human Resources and Business Services departments had quietly surrendered their core functions to for-profit staffing corporations. 

In that exact same 2024-25 austerity year, the district wired **$27,904,823** to a single private healthcare staffing corporation known as Amergis Healthcare Staffing.

Amergis did not emerge from nowhere. It is the rebranded successor to Maxim Healthcare Services, a company that paid a record $150 million in 2011 to resolve federal criminal and civil charges of Medicaid fraud. 

This is not a story of an unavoidable budget shortfall. It is a story of catastrophic administrative failure at the highest levels of district operations—specifically within the Human Resources and Financial divisions—resulting in the structural privatization of public education.

---

### THE DIVERGENCE: Classrooms Cut, Contracts Kept

Public school accounting uses a coding system where "Object 2" and "Object 3" represent the salaries of direct-hire teachers and classified staff. These are the budgets that bled to cover the $35 million shortfall. 

But "Object 7" represents "Purchased Services"—money paid directly to outside vendors.

The data reveals a stark, engineered divergence. According to OSPI F-195 budget reports, Vancouver's Object 7 spending consumed 10.4% of the district's general fund in 2022-23. By the 2024-25 austerity year, Object 7 spending jumped to **$47.3 million**, consuming **11.67%** of the entire district budget. 

While the district claimed poverty to the community, it found the money to increase its purchased services budget by more than $5 million in a single year. 

---

### THE REBRAND AND THE RUBBER STAMP

No single vendor benefits from this structural shift more than Amergis Healthcare Staffing. The financial trajectory of this single corporate entity within VPS is staggering. 

- **2021-22:** VPS paid Maxim $5.3 million.
- **2022-23:** VPS paid Maxim $14.4 million.
- **2023-24:** The handoff year. Maxim was paid $8.06 million, while the newly rebranded Amergis debuted with $5.4 million.
- **2024-25:** The austerity year. Maxim payments zeroed out ($46k), and Amergis billed the district **$27.9 million**.

The invoices quintupled in three years seamlessly. This scale of spending largely avoided public scrutiny because it was consistently placed on the Board's "Consent Agenda"—a mechanism designed for routine, non-controversial items (like approving meeting minutes) that allows the board to pass dozens of items simultaneously, with a single, un-debated vote. 

Millions of dollars in privatized labor contracts were routinely rubber-stamped without public debate.

---

### THE AGENCY TRAP: 30% Premiums on Public Dollars

Why does it matter if a paraprofessional, nurse, or speech-language pathologist (SLP) wears a VPS badge or an Amergis badge? **The Premium.**

Based on an analysis of regional contractor bill rates compared against internal VEA collective bargaining agreement salaries (fully loaded with benefits and pensions):

- An internal paraprofessional costs the district approximately $51,300 annually. An agency paraprofessional bills at roughly $58,425 — an **$7,125 per-employee premium (13.8%)**.
- An internal Speech-Language Pathologist (SLP) costs approximately $108,000. An agency SLP bills at roughly $124,687 — a **$16,687 premium (15.4%)**.
- A Behavior Tech carries a staggering **31.9% premium**, costing the district $18,112 more per year than a direct hire.

This gap is pure "Lost Efficiency." Translated across $27.9 million in a single year, the district is burning the exact millions it claims it needs to balance the budget simply to cover the corporate markup of a middleman.

---

### ADMINISTRATIVE INEPTITUDE OR SOMETHING WORSE?

The "Agency Trap" does not build itself. It requires the systemic failure of the executives paid to manage district recruitment and finances. 

The structural reliance on Amergis raises unavoidable questions about the performance and integrity of the departments led by **Jeff Fish**, Executive Director of Human Resources, and **Brett Blechschmidt**, the district’s Chief Financial Officer.

Fish’s HR department is charged with recruiting and retaining public educators. The ballooning of the Amergis contract to $27.9 million indicates his department is either so historically inept that it can no longer successfully hire its own employees, or it has functionally abandoned the effort in favor of outsourcing. 

In corporate settings, a manager who outsources $28 million of their core function to a single external vendor at a massive premium would immediately trigger audits for kickbacks or sweetheart deals. The rapid, un-flagged expansion of the Amergis relationship—alongside a complete lack of internal capacity building—makes it a valid question for public oversight.

Similarly, Blechschmidt has directed the district’s finances for over a decade. His office prepared the budgets that claimed a $35 million deficit and required the termination of 260 direct-hire staff, while simultaneously routing an escalating $47.3 million annually into Object 7 without aggressively auditing the "premium" being lost to vendors like Amergis.

When district financial leadership presents austerity as unavoidable while maintaining inelastic, uncapped payments to private staffing agencies at massive markups, it ceases to be a budget problem. It becomes an accountability problem.
