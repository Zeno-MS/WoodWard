# THE AGENCY TRAP: Structural Privatization at Vancouver Public Schools

## How a $35 Million Deficit Coexists with Explosive Uncapped Spending on Private Staffing Corporations

*By WoodWard*
*Draft 2 — Investigative Report*

---

In the 2024-25 school year, Vancouver Public Schools (VPS) cut more than 260 classroom positions, eliminated middle school librarians, and pointed to a $35 million structural deficit to justify the austerity. 

Yet, in that exact same fiscal year, the district paid a single private healthcare staffing corporation **$27,904,823**. 

That corporation is Amergis Healthcare Staffing — the rebranded successor to Maxim Healthcare Services, a company that paid a record $150 million in 2011 to resolve federal criminal and civil charges of Medicaid fraud. 

This is not a story about a budget deficit. It is a story about *structural privatization* — how a district's fiscal crisis is driven, in part, by millions in elastic, uncapped payments to private staffing corporations whose costs are structurally shielded from the austerity measures imposed on public educators.

This investigation, built on a forensic analysis of OSPI financial data, state salary databases, and hundreds of pages of district meeting minutes, reveals a system where the costs of providing legally mandated special education services have been quietly outsourced to for-profit agencies, primarily through the obscure mechanism of unanimous "consent agenda" board votes.

---

### THE DIVERGENCE: Classrooms Cut, Contracts Kept

Public school accounting is notoriously complex, but the mechanism hiding these payments is simple: **Object 7**. 

In Washington state school finance, "Object 2" and "Object 3" represent the salaries of direct-hire teachers and classified staff. When a district claims a budget crisis, these are the objects that bleed. 

"Object 7" represents "Purchased Services" — the money paid to outside vendors.

The data reveals a stark divergence. According to OSPI F-195 budget reports, Vancouver's Object 7 spending consumed 10.4% of the district's general fund in 2022-23. By the 2024-25 austerity year, Object 7 spending jumped to **$47.3 million**, now consuming **11.67%** of the entire district budget. 

While the district was unable to find the money to retain middle school librarians, it found the money to increase its purchased services budget by more than $5 million in a single year.

---

### THE REBRAND AND THE HANDOFF

No single vendor benefits from this structural shift more than Amergis Healthcare Staffing (formerly Maxim Healthcare Services). 

The financial trajectory of this single corporate entity within VPS is staggering. Based on an analysis of district payment records:
- **2021-22:** VPS paid Maxim $5.3 million.
- **2022-23:** VPS paid Maxim $14.4 million.
- **2023-24:** The handoff year. Maxim was paid $8.06 million, while the newly rebranded Amergis debuted with $5.4 million.
- **2024-25:** The austerity year. Maxim payments zeroed out ($46k), and Amergis billed the district **$27.9 million**.

The transition was seamless. The invoices effectively quintupled in three years. Yet, an analysis of 53 distinct Vancouver School Board meetings between 2016 and 2025 reveals how this scale of spending avoided public scrutiny.

Our analysis identified **82 separate contract authorizations** tied to Amergis/Maxim. The vast majority of these multi-million dollar agreements were passed via the "Consent Agenda" — a mechanism designed for routine, non-controversial items (like approving minutes or minor equipment surpluses) that allows the board to pass dozens of items simultaneously, with a single, un-debated vote. 

Millions of dollars in privatized labor contracts were routinely rubber-stamped, categorized alongside the approval of instructional materials and minor facility repairs. 

---

### THE PREMIUM AND LOST EFFICIENCY

Why does it matter if a paraprofessional, nurse, or speech-language pathologist (SLP) wears a VPS badge or an Amergis badge? **The Premium.**

When a district cannot hire a direct public employee — due to burnout, non-competitive wages, or the prohibitive localized costs of the SEBB healthcare mandate — they turn to the agencies. 

But the agencies do not work at cost. Based on an analysis of regional contractor bill rates compared against internal VEA collective bargaining agreement salaries (fully loaded with benefits and pensions):

- An internal special education paraprofessional costs the district approximately $51,300 annually. An agency paraprofessional bills at roughly $58,425 — an **$7,125 per-employee premium (13.8%)**.
- An internal Speech-Language Pathologist (SLP) costs approximately $108,000. An agency SLP bills at roughly $124,687 — a **$16,687 premium (15.4%)**.
- A Behavior Tech carries a staggering **31.9% premium**, costing the district $18,112 more per year than a direct hire.

This gap is the "Lost Efficiency." For every $1 million paid to Amergis for paraprofessionals, the district is burning enough cash to have hired an additional two full-time educators. Extrapolated across $27.9 million in a single year, the district is hemorrhaging the exact millions it claims it needs to balance the budget.

This creates the **Agency Trap**: The district under-compensates internal roles to save money. The roles go vacant. Federal law mandates the services (IDEA). The district is forced to hire the agency at a 15-30% premium. The ballooning agency costs drain the general fund. The district announces a budget deficit and freezes internal hiring wages, perpetuating the cycle.

---

### THE LEADERSHIP PARADOX

As the agency trap deepened, executive compensation at VPS followed the exact trajectory of the contractor spending.

Superintendent Jeff Snell arrived in the 2021-22 school year with a base salary of $292,369. By the 2024-25 fiscal year — as the district eliminated 260+ classroom positions and Amergis billing hit $27.9 million — Snell’s salary rose to **$412,544**. 

A $120,000 increase over three years, presiding over a systemic shift of public education dollars into private corporate hands.

### QUESTIONS FOR THE DISTRICT

*(Note: These questions must be submitted to the district for a Right of Reply before publication)*

1. How does the district justify increasing "Object 7" purchased services spending to 11.67% of the budget ($47.3M) in the exact same year it cited a $35M deficit to cut 260+ classroom positions?
2. Did the Board of Directors formally debate the transition from Maxim to Amergis, and was a competitive re-bid conducted for the $27.9 million 2024-25 contract?
3. Why are multi-million dollar private staffing contracts routinely placed on the Consent Agenda rather than separated as Action Items for public discussion?
4. What is the district's specific plan to rebuild internal staffing capacity and break reliance on the 15-30% premium charged by private agencies?
