# How contractor dependency harms students with disabilities

**Vancouver Public Schools spends millions on agency staff who lack training, cycle through classrooms, and leave students with disabilities worse off — a pattern replicated in districts nationwide that research now causally links to declining outcomes, rising restraint use, and systemic IDEA violations.** The district's reliance on staffing firms like Maxim to fill paraeducator vacancies, combined with a $31.4 million special education deficit over five years, has created conditions where students receive a fraction of their mandated IEP minutes, untrained security officers perform prohibited restraints, and permanent staff burn out at rates five to twelve times the clinical norm. Washington's historic SB 5263 funding cap repeal in May 2025 was supposed to help — but VPS's structural deficit has swallowed the new money without any public plan to convert contractor positions to permanent hires. The causal chain from staffing instability to classroom harm is no longer theoretical. A 2025 CALDER study using Washington State data provides the first causal estimates showing special education teacher turnover has an **outsized negative impact on test scores for students with disabilities specifically** — and VPS sits at the center of this crisis.

---

## The churn machine: what contractor dependency looks like in practice

The national scope of contractor dependency in special education is staggering. Over **60% of school districts** used staffing agencies to fill special education and related service positions in 2023, according to the AASA Staffing Survey. Agencies charge districts **$70 to $125 per hour** — translating to $110,000–$150,000 annually per worker — while district-employed staff in equivalent roles often earn less than half that amount including benefits. New York City's Department of Education maintained **141 active contracts at a cost of nearly $900 million** in FY2024 for special education services alone, per United Federation of Teachers analysis. The financial incentive structure is perverse: districts pay more for less-qualified, less-stable staff because they cannot recruit permanent employees at wages they're willing to offer.

Special education teacher turnover runs at approximately **12–15% annually**, roughly double the general education rate of 6–8%. Paraeducator attrition is even more alarming. A CALDER Working Paper analyzing Washington State data found paraeducator attrition hit **23% after the 2021–22 school year** — more than double the 8% rate recorded after 2008–09. In Washington, approximately 5,800 paraprofessionals (40% of the workforce) left their positions by the end of the most recent school year analyzed. Full-time paraeducator salaries average just under **$30,000** — about half a Washington special education teacher's salary — and in urban areas, rent consumes up to 70% of that income.

The result is a rotating cast of adults in classrooms designed for the students who most need consistency. EdWeek documented in June 2022 that contract workers "lack the institutional knowledge that comes with working in the same school community for a long time," with critics calling the practice "frankly an act of desperation." A rural Vermont administrator captured the trajectory: her district went from needing one contracted position a decade ago to five currently. Forty-five states reported shortages in special education during 2024–25, making it the single most frequently reported shortage area nationwide.

## Inside Vancouver Public Schools: where the staffing-compliance nexus breaks down

VPS's specific history illustrates how contractor dependency and staffing gaps translate directly into compliance failures and student harm. The evidence spans nearly a decade of documented incidents, investigations, and complaints.

**The restraint crisis.** In the 2016–17 school year, VPS restrained students **1,110 times** — an average of once per hour, every school day — more than virtually any large district in Washington or Oregon, per OPB's investigative reporting. By 2022–23, VPS ranked **first in the state** with 776 restraints, 53 student injuries, and 137 staff injuries. Neighboring Evergreen School District, slightly larger in enrollment, recorded just 93 restraints that same year. OPB's March 2023 investigation revealed that VPS's District Resource Officers continued using **prohibited prone and supine restraints** more than a year after Washington banned them in September 2021. The critical staffing detail: while DROs were district employees rather than contractors, **no officers were certified under the Crisis Prevention Institute program** that district policy required of paraeducators and teachers performing restraints. DROs could carry tasers, pepper spray, handcuffs, and body armor, but lacked the one certification specifically designed to ensure safe physical intervention with students. Twenty-five of approximately 40 identified restraint incidents occurred at the Jim Tangeman Center, which serves students with the most severe emotional and behavioral disabilities.

**The Bailey case.** The 2018 due process complaints filed by parents Cara Bailey and Sarah McPartland exposed the staffing-harm connection with painful specificity. Bailey's son Colin, who has autism and is mostly nonverbal, was restrained almost daily during the 2015–16 school year at Hough Elementary and isolated in a "quiet room" for over two hours on one occasion. He was later diagnosed with PTSD by psychiatrist Dr. Daniel Lam. Bailey alleged the district **failed to provide her son with the one-on-one paraeducator required by his IEP**, substituting vaguer "additional adult support" that could mean less than full-time attention. As educator Kathy Forbes told OPB: knowing each child, noticing what's happening, and intervening before escalation "require training, experience and sufficient staff."

**OSPI Complaint SECC 21-32.** When a parent filed a citizen complaint with OSPI in April 2021, the resulting investigation found that the district delivered only **960 of the 1,575 weekly special education minutes** specified in the student's December 2020 IEP. OSPI's decision recorded the district's admission that "there was not a concerted plan to supplement the in-person instruction with remote special education services." The complaint documents reveal a telling staffing pattern: the parent reported that "substitute teachers made it difficult" to address her child's needs and raised concerns about "the Student's confusion over the number of substitute teachers that he had." Six of twelve IEP goals showed no progress; four showed slow progress.

**The AG investigation.** Washington's Attorney General concluded a yearlong investigation in June 2019, finding VPS disciplined students with disabilities at **13.5% versus 4.7%** of nondisabled peers — and disciplined Black students at more than double the rate of white students. While the AG's findings did not explicitly cite staffing as a cause, the remedial actions — hiring a $150,000 consultant from UCLA's Center for Civil Rights Remedies, creating four new positions including a special services compliance manager — implicitly acknowledged the district lacked adequate staffing and training infrastructure to prevent discriminatory outcomes.

**Confirmed contractor use.** In March 2024, The Columbian reported that VPS confirmed contracting with **Maxim Healthcare Staffing** to fill more than a dozen vacant paraeducator positions. VPS Director of Special Services Jami Phelps described it as a pandemic-era solution: "We've been very lucky that coming out of the pandemic we've been able to contract with agencies to fill out needs that we weren't able to get through our own channels." She described it as "not a long-term model." VPS maintains approximately 500 paraeducator positions total.

## The death spiral: how shortages consume those who remain

The mechanism through which staffing shortages become self-reinforcing is now well-documented across multiple professional disciplines. When positions go unfilled or are filled by rotating contractors, remaining permanent staff absorb impossible caseloads, burn out, and leave — creating more vacancies and accelerating the cycle.

**School-based SLPs** carry a median actual caseload of **50 students**, but report a manageable caseload of only 40, per ASHA's 2024 Schools Survey — a 25% overload. The gap is wider in states with acute shortages: Indiana's SLPs carry 78 students against a manageable threshold of 50. School psychologists face a national average ratio of **1:1,065**, more than double the NASP-recommended 1:500. The National Association of School Psychologists estimates the country would need **45,000–63,000 additional school psychologists** to meet the recommended ratio. Special education teachers spend only **32.4% of their day** providing actual instruction; the rest is consumed by IEP development, progress tracking, compliance paperwork, and paraeducator supervision.

The burnout data is alarming. A 2023 study of 490 special educators published in ScienceDirect found over **60% scored at the "dangerous" level on the Maslach Burnout Inventory's emotional exhaustion scale**. Approximately 38% met clinical criteria for generalized anxiety disorder and 38% for major depressive disorder — rates **five to twelve times higher** than a normative adult sample. An IES/NCSER study found 91% of special educators reported the pandemic had a moderate to extreme impact on their stress levels. Half of all special educators leave the profession within five years.

Washington State data confirms the pattern locally. A 2024 state audit found that **at least 1.5% of special education instruction statewide** is provided by underqualified teachers — nearly three times higher than any other content area. A 2021 University of Washington study found that approximately 20% of vacant teaching positions were for special education, a rate three times higher than elementary education. As Washington State University's Josh Taylor told the Washington State Standard: "Districts hire teachers without special education certifications because turnover and vacancies are so high, and they need staff — but then those teachers leave because they aren't well-prepared. **That becomes this nasty, cyclical problem.**"

At VPS specifically, the cycle reached its most visible expression when the board approved **$35 million in budget cuts and 262 staff positions** in March 2024. Special education paraeducator Christopher Smith told the board: "As you burn out your staff, there are limited replacements. This is not a renewable resource at this point." By June 2025, the VEA filed a grievance alleging the district had quietly eliminated more than 20 additional certificated positions through attrition — what the union called a "secret" reduction-in-force. VEA President Jamie Anderson reported core high school classes exceeding 40 students, with middle school ELA classes topping 42, costing the district **$2.75 million in contractual overload payments** in a single year.

The SEBB mandate, which took effect January 1, 2020, added structural pressure. Washington's Joint Legislative Audit and Review Committee found districts paid an estimated **31–44% more per month** for health benefits under SEBB, with state funding covering only 76% of reported costs. For any employee working 630 or more hours per school year, eligibility jumped from 70% to 97%. The financial incentive to use third-party contractors — whose benefits are the agency's responsibility, not the district's — became unmistakable.

## What students experience when the adults keep changing

The research on how staffing instability affects students with disabilities, particularly those with autism, is unambiguous. A seminal study by Sulek et al. (2017) in *Research in Autism Spectrum Disorders* established that inconsistent staffing "is problematic in organisations, with the potential to impact both staff and services provided," with cascading negative effects on service quality, therapeutic relationships, and organizational infrastructure. Students with autism respond to consistency: teachers emphasize the importance of predictable patterns, routines, clear expectations, and consistent language. These students are "generally more at-risk for poorer-quality student-teacher relationships than both neurotypical students and students with other disabilities."

When IEP services go undelivered, the consequences compound rapidly. **Academic regression accelerates** because students with disabilities often lose skills faster than peers — a week without services can translate to months of lost progress. Behavioral challenges intensify because many IEP services address behavioral needs directly; without consistent support, minor issues escalate into major disruptions. Courts have consistently held that staff shortages do not excuse failure to provide a Free Appropriate Public Education. A November 2024 Pennsylvania ruling involving an autistic student who did not receive IEP-mandated services affirmed this principle; a separate hearing officer specifically rejected a district's "impossibility of performance" defense based on inability to hire qualified personnel.

The restraint-training connection is perhaps the most dangerous link in the chain. The GAO's landmark 2009 report on seclusions and restraints found that "more often than not, teachers and staff who used seclusion and restraint in abusive ways **had not been properly trained**." Wisconsin's Department of Public Instruction, which recorded a **15–17% increase** in restraint and seclusion incidents between 2022–23 and 2023–24, drew a direct line to staffing. State consultant Tim Peerenboom stated: "We do know that our higher needs students are working in situations where there are staffing shortages — and there's also a good bit of turnover in those programs." Illinois's State Board of Education investigation of Chicago Public Schools found untrained employees involved in **21 restraint incidents**, with one special education teacher noting that security officers "might not know students or their needs like their classroom teachers." Federal Civil Rights Data Collection from 2021–22 shows students with disabilities — 14% of enrollment — account for **68% of students secluded and 76% physically restrained** nationwide.

VPS's most recent flashpoint is the Flex Academy closure announced in December 2025 as part of $7 million in planned reductions. The alternative high school served 86 students, more than half with IEPs. Parents launched a GoFundMe to hire an attorney, arguing the district violated Washington law requiring public notice and hearings before school closures. Parent Bruce Anderholt told KGW: "Those laws have very specific requirements and very specific protections, which have been ignored by the district." Parent Erin Stepanek addressed the board directly: "You're asking children who struggle with change, predictability and anxiety to observe the greatest loss with the least support." A parent of an autistic student wrote to The Columbian that proposed alternatives "offer fewer than 2.5 hours of weekly instruction and rely heavily on online coursework and artificial intelligence-based platforms."

## After the cap repeal: new money, same trajectory

Washington's passage of SB 5263 in May 2025 — approved 97–0 in the House and 48–0 in the Senate — represented the most significant special education funding reform in state history. The law removes the **16% enrollment cap** that had denied districts state funding for special education students exceeding that threshold, increases the funding multiplier to approximately 1.53–1.64 times the basic education allocation, and directs an estimated **$870 million** in additional funding to districts over four years. Advocates specifically stated the legislation could enable districts to hire permanent staff instead of relying on contractors.

At VPS, the money appears to have been absorbed into existing deficits rather than targeted for staffing conversion. Interim Superintendent Brett Blechschmidt, appointed May 2025, acknowledged the state increased special education funds but stated it was **"still not enough to cover the costs and meet the needs of those students."** The 2025–26 budget drew the fund balance from $7 million down to $195,180 to avoid layoffs. VPS requested a **$21.4 million advance** on state funding — the largest request from any Washington district — and received only $8.7 million. By December 2025, the board approved borrowing from the Clark County Treasurer's Office through interest-bearing registered warrants.

No public commitment from Blechschmidt was found regarding converting agency positions to permanent hires. No board discussion was documented addressing how SB 5263 funds would be strategically allocated to reduce contractor dependency. No strategic plan for reducing purchased services spending exists in publicly available VPS documents. The district does not publish Object 7 expenditure data or contractor spending breakdowns on its public website. A projected **$20.15 million deficit for 2026–27** means further cuts are planned — not position conversions.

Dr. Ricardo "Rocky" Torres-Morales, selected as permanent superintendent in January 2026 (starting July 2026), brings a background as a bilingual special education teacher in New York City's DOE. He has not yet made public statements about VPS staffing strategy, contractor dependency, or SB 5263 funding allocation.

The unions have been more direct. VAESP's September 2025 strike authorization — supported by **80% of the 800-member classified workforce** — cited turnover, staffing levels, and training as top grievances. President Chipo Sowards articulated the core argument: "We want the employees, people who are experienced, who are skilled, who know those kids; kids respond better to stability." A tentative agreement was reached September 16, averting a strike, but terms were not publicly disclosed.

## Districts that found a way out

The contractor dependency cycle is not inevitable. Several districts and states have demonstrated viable exit strategies, though none are quick fixes.

**San Francisco USD** represents the most ambitious current effort. The district estimates it will pay **$42.1 million in 2025–26** to non-public programs, independent agencies, and consultants for special education. Its February 2026 tentative agreement with United Educators of San Francisco explicitly committed to **reducing reliance on non-union contractors**, establishing a joint educator working group with budget authority, setting caseload goals with $1,000-per-semester overage stipends, and giving special education paraeducators an additional 5% salary increase. Oakland USD's state fact-finding report crystallized the financial logic: "When you contract out that position, you're actually costing the district nearly double the amount of money."

**Hawaii's $10,000 annual SPED differential**, implemented in fall 2020, provides the most rigorously studied recruitment incentive. CALDER research found it reduced the proportion of vacant SPED positions by **32%** relative to general education. But the effect came almost entirely from general education teachers switching into SPED roles rather than improved retention — described by researchers as "robbing Peter to pay Paul." Working conditions, caseload management, and administrative support matter as much or more than pay for keeping teachers in place.

**Grow-your-own pipelines** offer a longer-term structural solution. Chicago Public Schools' teacher residency program recruits current teaching assistants from special education classrooms, providing salary, benefits, and discounted tuition during an intensive one-year program; it now trains **200 staff per year**, approximately 80% Black and Latinx. Indiana's ESSER-funded program has produced over **600 licensed special education teachers** since 2021. Florida's Para-to-Teacher program provides full tuition, textbook, and certification exam support for paraprofessionals earning SPED degrees. Washington State's own PESB is recognized as a national leader in grow-your-own infrastructure, with partnerships between Puget Sound districts and educator preparation programs, though VPS's participation in these efforts is undocumented.

**Teletherapy** addresses geographic and staffing barriers for specific related services. ASHA's Practice Portal describes the effectiveness of telepractice in schools as "well documented," with research showing outcomes comparable to in-person delivery for many service types. Presence, the market leader, employs over 2,000 licensed clinicians serving 10,000+ schools across 47 states. The approach still requires on-site support staff, limiting its ability to solve the paraeducator shortage, but it can meaningfully reduce reliance on contracted SLPs and occupational therapists.

NCTQ's analysis of 148 large districts found that only **six offer SPED teachers additional pay exceeding $5,000 per year**, and over 90% pay more for master's degrees than for filling special education vacancies — despite no evidence that master's degrees improve teaching effectiveness. The research consensus points toward a multi-pronged approach: permanent salary differentials of 7.5–14% of base salary, aggressive grow-your-own pipelines, reduced non-instructional workload, protected caseload limits, and strong administrative support. Each element is necessary; none is sufficient alone.

## Conclusion: the causal chain is documented, the exit strategy is known

The evidence assembled here establishes a clear causal chain operating at VPS and nationally. Structural underfunding creates vacancies. Vacancies drive contractor dependency. Contractors arrive without district-specific training in crisis intervention, de-escalation, or individual student needs. They cycle through classrooms, disrupting the consistency that students with autism and behavioral disabilities require most. Remaining permanent staff absorb impossible caseloads, burn out at clinical rates, and leave — widening the vacancies that created the problem. IEP minutes go undelivered. Restraints increase. Students develop PTSD. Parents file due process complaints. Districts pay settlements and legal fees that further drain the budget available for permanent hiring.

What distinguishes the VPS situation is not the presence of any single failure but the **documented accumulation of failures over nearly a decade** — from the 1,110 restraints in 2016–17, to the Bailey family's PTSD diagnosis, to the prohibited restraints continuing fifteen months after the state ban, to the OSPI complaint revealing 615 undelivered weekly IEP minutes, to the 262 positions eliminated in 2024, to the Flex Academy closure affecting students with IEPs in December 2025 — all while the district contracted with Maxim to fill paraeducator vacancies it could not staff internally and drew its fund balance to $195,180.

The strategies for breaking this cycle are well-documented and being implemented elsewhere. SFUSD's $42 million contractor-to-permanent conversion commitment, Hawaii's salary differentials, Chicago's 200-teacher-per-year residency pipeline, and federal teletherapy platforms all demonstrate viable paths. What remains absent at VPS is any publicly articulated plan to pursue them. SB 5263's $870 million in new statewide funding created the fiscal opening. Whether VPS — under its incoming superintendent, its financially constrained board, and its increasingly vocal unions — will use that opening to break the cycle or continue funding it remains the defining question for the district's 3,700 special education students.
