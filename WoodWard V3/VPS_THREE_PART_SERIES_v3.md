# PART I: THE AUSTERITY PARADOX
## How Vancouver Public Schools Cut 217 Classroom and Support Jobs While Preserving Its Executive Salary Architecture

### The Night the Cuts Came Down

On the evening of March 12, 2024, dozens of Vancouver community members filed into the school board meeting knowing what was coming but hoping they were wrong. The Vancouver Public Schools Board of Directors was about to vote on $35 million in budget reductions — roughly nine percent of the district's $406 million annual budget — eliminating what public communications would describe as "over 260 positions."

Paraeducator Christopher Smith stepped to the microphone and challenged the administrators seated at the dais. "As you burn out your staff, there are limited replacements," he said. "This is not a renewable resource. Have you been in a classroom with 40 or more students?" **[PROOF — The Columbian, March 13, 2024]**

Vancouver Education Association President Jamie Anderson was blunter: "Cutting 100-plus classroom teachers — I don't see how that minimizes impact to the classroom." **[PROOF — The Columbian, March 13, 2024]**

The board voted unanimously to approve the cuts.

Within days, students at Fort Vancouver High School walked out in protest.

The district was in the third year of what would become an unprecedented financial spiral — the only district in Clark County to receive a state financial warning, the only one to require emergency borrowing from the state for three consecutive years, and by December 2025, the only one to resort to borrowing from the county treasurer to make payroll.

But the question that remained unanswered that night — the question this series seeks to address — is not whether the cuts were necessary. Real fiscal pressures demanded real reductions. The question is how those cuts were distributed, who made the decisions, and whether the district's own framing of its austerity accurately reflected where the financial pain landed.

### Two Numbers, Two Stories

**[PROOF — OSPI S-275 data]** Washington school districts report staffing annually to the Office of Superintendent of Public Instruction through the S-275 personnel database, which captures actual assigned full-time equivalents — real people on the payroll, not budgeted vacancies.

Those state records show:

- **2023–24 Total District FTE:** 2,736.83
- **2024–25 Total District FTE:** 2,519.90
- **Net Reduction:** 216.93 FTE (7.93%)

The district's public communications referenced "over 260 positions." The state payroll record reflects approximately 217 FTE removed from active assignment. The gap reflects the distinction between budgeted positions — which include unfilled vacancies and partial roles — and actual staffed assignments. Both metrics are standard in school district accounting. They measure different things.

But the composition of that 217-person reduction is where the story diverges from the district's public framing.

In subsequent budget materials, VPS made a specific claim:

> "The percentage of staff cut at Central Office was the highest of any employee group during the reduction in force."
> — Vancouver Public Schools, 2025–26 Budget Information page

**[PROOF — vansd.org budget page]** That statement is mathematically supportable — if you define "Central Office" geographically.

### The Geographic Frame

OSPI location codes show that employees assigned to the district's primary administrative site (Location Code 1014) declined as follows:

- **2023–24 Location 1014 FTE:** 480.63
- **2024–25 Location 1014 FTE:** 433.82
- **Net Reduction:** 47.57 FTE (10.13%)

Central Office shrank by 10.13 percent. The district overall shrank by 7.93 percent. On a percentage basis, the district's claim holds.

But OSPI coding reveals *what kinds of jobs* were eliminated at that location.

### Where the Axe Actually Fell

**[PROOF — S-275 duty root analysis, verified by Antigravity/Neo]** The largest reductions at Location 1014 occurred in the following classifications:

- **Other Support Personnel (Root 40):** –17.58 FTE
- **Service Workers — custodial, food service (Root 95 series):** –15.28 FTE
- **Crafts/Trades (Root 64):** –12.10 FTE
- **Other District Administrators (Root 13):** –4.59 FTE

The majority of Central Office reductions were concentrated in support and operational roles — positions averaging approximately **$48,070** in annual compensation.

By contrast, cabinet-level administrators (Duty Roots 11–13 combined) declined modestly:

- **2022–23:** 28 administrators
- **2024–25:** 23 administrators
- **Compensation pool:** $5.854M → $5.451M (–6.89%)

The executive tier's contraction of 6.89 percent was slightly *less* than the district's overall 7.93 percent reduction. The executive compensation structure contracted modestly and remained within established six-figure salary bands — an average of approximately **$183,884** per administrator. No structural downward re-banding of administrative compensation occurred.

This is not a story about executives avoiding all sacrifice. Five positions were eliminated. Administrators took nine unpaid furlough days. Superintendent Jeff Snell accepted a 15.2 percent pay cut.

**The asymmetry lies in the composition.** When the district told the public that Central Office absorbed "the highest percentage" of cuts, it was aggregating $48,000-a-year custodians and $183,000-a-year executives into a single geographic bucket. The percentage claim is accurate. The *impression* it creates — that administrative leadership bore the deepest pain — requires context that the district did not volunteer.

### Austerity by Salary Tier

**[PROOF — S-275 payroll analysis, cross-verified]** When the reductions are stratified not by geography but by compensation band, the distribution of financial impact becomes visible:

**Executive tier (Roots 11–13):**
- 2023–24 payroll: $4,964,868
- 2024–25 payroll: $4,442,442
- Reduction: –$522,426 (10.5%)

**Teacher tier (Roots 31–33):**
- 2023–24 payroll: $123,776,655
- 2024–25 payroll: $121,675,728
- Reduction: –$2,100,927 (1.7%)

**Support tier (Roots 91–97):**
- 2023–24 payroll: $63,545,872
- 2024–25 payroll: $63,467,602
- Reduction: –$78,270 (0.1% — net payroll flatlined despite headcount cuts because mandatory step increases and COLAs for remaining staff offset the savings)

In raw dollars, the instructional core generated **four times more payroll savings** ($2.1 million) than the executive tier ($522,000). The support tier — where headcount dropped significantly — generated almost no net savings because contractual raises for surviving staff consumed the reductions.

### The Deputy Superintendent That Wasn't

One element frequently cited in public discussions of administrative "streamlining" was the elimination of a Deputy Superintendent position. The district stated it had "repurposed previous roles, such as Deputy Superintendent, to positions that directly support schools."

**[PROOF — S-275 records, three fiscal years]** State personnel records tell a different story:

- **2022–23:** 0.00 FTE coded to Deputy/Assistant Superintendent (Duty Root 12)
- **2023–24:** 0.00 FTE
- **2024–25:** 0.00 FTE

The position had been vacant for at least three fiscal years. The elimination of a position that had been vacant for multiple years generated no new payroll savings in the 2024–25 cuts.

### The TOSA Question

**[PROOF — S-275 coding, VPS budget page]** Teachers on Special Assignment (TOSAs) physically assigned to the District Office are coded under instructional duty roots (Root 33: "Other Teacher") rather than administrative categories. Approximately 9–10 FTE at Location 1014 carried instructional classifications despite working at the central office building.

The district's public analysis explicitly excludes TOSAs from its definition of "Central Office" staff. This coding is consistent with OSPI conventions. But because classification is tied to duty root rather than work location, the geographic percentage claims aggregate support and executive roles while excluding instructional staff assigned to the same building. This definitional choice affects how percentage comparisons are calculated.

### Who Made These Decisions

Two administrators held the authority and institutional responsibility for how the 2024 reductions were structured.

**Brett Blechschmidt, then-Associate Superintendent and Chief Operating Officer/Chief Financial Officer,** was the district's primary budget architect. With 30 years in education administration — 19 at ESD 112, 11 at VPS — Blechschmidt had served as the board's lead financial presenter for years, delivering annual budget recommendations, consensus budgets, and financial updates. **[PROOF — board meeting records, Columbian reporting]** He presented the $35 million reduction package to the board. He identified the five structural drivers: recurring deficits, enrollment decreases, increased staffing costs, special education underfunding ($31.4 million gap over five years), and rising operating costs.

In May 2025, the board appointed Blechschmidt interim superintendent for 2025–26, with an agreement he would not seek the permanent position. He now presides over a district whose fund balance stands at **$195,180** — down from $7 million — with a projected **$20.15 million deficit for 2026–27**.

Blechschmidt's signatory authority on vendor contracts is not explicitly documented in publicly available records, but his position as the district's highest-ranking financial officer gave him functional authority over fiscal operations, including the purchasing department managed by David Hurt. **[UNRESOLVED — exact contract approval thresholds require public records request]**

**Jeff Fish, Executive Director of Human Resources since spring 2022,** holds a JD from Catholic University of America and spent his career in labor and legal roles — HR Legal Counsel at Portland Public Schools, positions at Clackamas ESD and Legal Aid Services of Oregon. His background is distinctively legal for an HR director.

**[STRONG INFERENCE]** Fish's public profile at VPS is remarkably low. No media quotes, interviews, public statements, or attributed board presentations by Fish were located in any regional outlet — The Columbian, Clark County Today, KATU, or OPB. His name does not appear in publicly available coverage of VPS labor negotiations with VEA, VAESP, or SEIU, despite his role as the district's top HR official. Coverage consistently attributes staffing and bargaining commentary to Superintendent Snell or Communications Director Jessica Roberts.

Fish oversees a five-person HR department responsible for recruiting the roughly 2,500-person workforce of a $406 million organization. His role in the decision to use private staffing agencies — Soliant, Amergis/Maxim, Pioneer — rather than recruiting permanent staff through internal HR channels is one of the central unresolved questions of this investigation. **[UNRESOLVED — requires public records request for emails, contract approvals, and HR staffing reports]**

### The Macro Context: Real Pressures on a Real District

**[PROOF — legislative records, OSPI data]** Any honest examination of VPS's crisis must acknowledge the external forces that constrained the district's choices.

Over the past decade, enrollment declined by roughly 750 students, directly reducing per-pupil state revenue. Federal ESSER relief funds ($64.5 million across three rounds) expired. The McCleary "levy swap" capped local revenue authority. State allocations tied to the Implicit Price Deflator lagged CPI inflation. K-12's share of the state general fund dropped from 52.4 percent in 2019 to 43.1 percent in 2024. Special education, where federal IDEA mandates require services regardless of funding levels, accumulated a $31.4 million shortfall over five years at VPS.

The 2025 Legislature provided some relief: SB 5263 eliminates the special education funding cap, and HB 2049 increases local levy authority. These are genuine structural pressures shared, to varying degrees, by every district in the state.

But the specific decisions VPS leadership made — where to cut, whom to preserve, and where to redirect money — were choices, not inevitabilities. The composition of those choices is the subject of this series.

### The Larger Question

The district eliminated approximately 217 actual FTE from the state payroll system during a $35 million austerity cycle. The distribution of that contraction — weighted heavily toward lower-compensated and classroom-facing roles while the executive salary architecture remained largely intact — matters.

But headcount alone does not resolve the central tension in Vancouver Public Schools' finances.

Because while staffing declined, another category in the budget expanded.

In the district's F-195 financial statements, Object 7 — Purchased Services — totaled approximately **$47.3 million** in 2024–25. That category includes tens of millions directed to private healthcare staffing agencies — the same agencies filling positions that the district's own Human Resources department, under Jeff Fish's leadership, was responsible for recruiting.

The question is not simply who was cut.

It is where the money flowed next.

# PART II: THE ACCOUNTABILITY VOID
## How $11.9 Million Flowed to Private Staffing Agencies in the Same Year Classrooms Contracted

### The Parallel Economy

In the same fiscal year that Vancouver Public Schools eliminated 217 positions from its payroll — 113 of them classroom teachers — the district directed approximately **$11.89 million** to a single entity: Amergis (formerly Maxim Healthcare Staffing), a national staffing agency headquartered in Dallas, Texas. **[SUSPICION — figure derived from investigation analysis; requires confirmation as final paid amount vs. encumbered]**

Additional payments flowed to Soliant Health and Pioneer Healthcare Services. Together, private staffing agencies consumed a significant share of Object 7 — Purchased Services — which totaled approximately **$47.3 million** in 2024–25. **[SUSPICION — Object 7 total derived from F-195 analysis; vendor-specific breakout not yet confirmed via primary documents]**

This is not, by itself, evidence of wrongdoing. School districts across Washington rely on contract staffing to fill hard-to-recruit positions, particularly in special education. Speech-language pathologists, school psychologists, Board Certified Behavior Analysts, and specialized paraeducators are in acute regional shortage. Federal law under IDEA mandates that districts provide these services regardless of whether they can hire permanent staff.

The questions are about scale, governance, and alternatives.

### The Procurement Blind Spot

**[PROOF — RCW 28A.335.190, MRSC analysis]** Under Washington state law, formal competitive bidding is required for goods purchases exceeding $75,000 and public works projects exceeding $100,000. But professional service contracts — the category under which staffing agencies operate — are **not subject to the same competitive bidding requirements.**

The Municipal Research and Services Center, which advises Washington local governments, confirms that the state has established no statutory bid limits, advertising requirements, or competitive procurement mandates for professional services. Districts set their own policies.

**[UNRESOLVED]** VPS Board Policy 6220 (Bid or Request for Proposal Requirements) follows the WSSDA model with standard thresholds. Board Policy 6210 (Purchasing: Authorization and Control) authorizes the superintendent to direct expenditures within the approved budget. The specific dollar threshold at which VPS requires board approval for service contracts versus superintendent or designee authority could not be confirmed from publicly available records. This is a critical governance question: *at what point does a staffing agency contract become large enough to require a board vote rather than an administrative signature?*

If the threshold is $75,000 — a common level at comparable districts — then a $11.89 million annual vendor relationship could have been structured as a series of smaller authorizations that never individually triggered board scrutiny.

**[STRONG INFERENCE]** Staffing agency contracts with Soliant, Amergis/Maxim, and Pioneer were not located in publicly available board meeting minutes, bid postings, or consent agendas. This does not prove they were never brought to the board — VPS BoardDocs, which houses detailed meeting minutes, blocks automated access. But it raises the question of whether cumulative vendor spending of this magnitude received the level of governance oversight that its scale warrants.

### The Consent Agenda Mechanism

**[STRONG INFERENCE — documented in multiple board meeting minutes]** When vendor contracts do appear before the VPS Board, they are typically included in the consent agenda — a bundle of routine items approved in a single vote, often in under sixty seconds, without individual discussion. This is standard practice at school boards statewide and is not inherently problematic. But when individual approvals of $50,000 or $75,000 accumulate to $30 million cumulatively, the consent mechanism creates structural opacity: no single agenda item reveals the cumulative scale of the vendor relationship.

### The Premium

**[SUSPICION — requires rate sheet confirmation from MSA]** National staffing agencies charge districts a bill rate that includes the worker's pay plus a margin covering the agency's overhead, profit, benefits, liability insurance, and recruitment costs. Industry estimates for healthcare education staffing place that margin at 15 to 30 percent above what a district would pay a direct-hire employee in salary plus benefits.

If VPS pays an average premium of even 20 percent on $11.89 million, that represents approximately **$4.65 million** annually in agency margin — money that does not reach a classroom, does not compensate a therapist or paraeducator, and does not build internal capacity for future recruitment.

Put differently: the agency premium alone may exceed what the district saved by eliminating the Deputy Superintendent position, cutting 16 clerk positions, and reducing 29 custodial staff combined.

**[UNRESOLVED]** The actual premium cannot be calculated without the Master Service Agreement (MSA) between VPS and Amergis, which would contain contracted hourly bill rates. A public records request under RCW 42.56 for this document is the single most important outstanding action in this investigation.

### The HR Recruitment Question

This brings the investigation back to Jeff Fish.

**[STRONG INFERENCE]** National staffing agencies maintain active, ongoing job postings in Vancouver, WA for positions including school psychologists, speech-language pathologists, and special education teachers. These are positions that fall directly within the recruitment purview of VPS's Human Resources department.

The fundamental governance question is: **why does a $406 million school district with a five-person HR department led by a JD-credentialed executive director rely on private staffing agencies to fill core instructional and therapeutic positions — and at what point did that reliance become a structural feature rather than an emergency measure?**

Several possible explanations exist, and each carries different implications:

**Explanation A — Labor market failure.** The regional shortage of specialized education professionals is genuine, well-documented, and shared by districts across the Pacific Northwest. If VPS cannot recruit permanent staff despite vigorous efforts, agency contracting is a reasonable — if expensive — necessity. Under this explanation, Fish's department is doing its best in an impossible market.

**Explanation B — Systemic underinvestment in recruitment.** A five-person HR department serving 2,500 employees may lack the capacity for sustained, specialized recruitment campaigns targeting competitive professional labor markets. If the district chose not to invest in recruitment infrastructure — competitive signing bonuses, relocation packages, accelerated onboarding — then the agency dependency is a self-reinforcing cycle: understaffed HR cannot recruit, so the district pays agencies, which consumes budget that could fund recruitment, which keeps HR understaffed.

**Explanation C — Structural incentive misalignment.** Agency contracts are categorized as "purchased services" (Object 7), not "salaries and benefits" (Objects 2 and 3). In a district under pressure to reduce its salary expenditure line — which is what RIF headcount reductions accomplish — shifting labor costs from the salary line to the purchased services line can make the books *appear* to show austerity while total labor-related spending remains flat or increases.

**[UNRESOLVED]** Without internal HR recruitment reports, vacancy data, and Fish's communications regarding staffing strategy, it is not possible to determine which explanation — or combination — applies.

### Brett Blechschmidt's Dual Authority

**[STRONG INFERENCE]** As CFO and later interim superintendent, Blechschmidt occupied a position of unusual authority over the financial architecture that enabled agency spending.

He oversaw the fiscal services department including the purchasing department. He presented the budget that directed Object 7 expenditures. He was the primary communicator to the board on financial matters. And as interim superintendent from May 2025 onward, he held the district's highest administrative authority over all contracts and expenditures.

The question is not whether Blechschmidt acted improperly — there is no evidence of that. The question is whether the governance structure provided adequate oversight of a vendor relationship that may represent the single largest expenditure category in the district's operating budget outside of direct employee compensation. A single administrator controlling both the financial presentation to the board and the operational authority to execute contracts creates a structural accountability gap, regardless of that administrator's integrity.

### Audit Findings

**[PROOF — State Auditor reports]** The Washington State Auditor's Office issued findings against VPS in both 2021 and 2024 for procurement compliance violations — breaking a streak of 13 consecutive clean audits. The specific nature of these findings — whether they relate to staffing agency contracts, general purchasing practices, or other procurement categories — requires verification from the complete audit reports.

That a district in financial crisis, dependent on emergency state borrowing, also lost its clean audit status for procurement compliance is a fact that merits public attention.

### What Needs to Be Known

Before this part of the series can reach full publication status, the following must be confirmed:

1. **The $11.89 million figure** — is this final paid, encumbered, or estimated? What fiscal year? Does it include or exclude Soliant and Pioneer?
2. **The MSA with Amergis** — rate structures, term length, auto-renewal clauses, liability provisions
3. **Object 7 trend data (2018–2025)** — did purchased services increase during the austerity year while salaries decreased?
4. **Board approval records** — were staffing agency contracts brought to the board? At what threshold?
5. **HR recruitment efforts** — what specific steps did Fish's department take to fill positions directly before resorting to agencies?

# PART III: THE REGULATORY CLIFF
## Three Years of Emergency Borrowing, a County Treasurer's Warning, and the Road to State Intervention

### The Borrowing Spiral

**[PROOF — OSPI records, Columbian reporting]** Vancouver Public Schools has required emergency state apportionment advances for three consecutive years — the only Clark County district to do so. The escalation is striking:

- **2023–24:** $15.5 million requested and granted
- **2024–25:** $18.5 million requested and granted
- **2025–26:** $21.4 million requested — **the largest apportionment advance request OSPI has ever received from any Washington district** — but only $8.7 million was approved

When the state said no to $12.7 million of the request, VPS turned to the Clark County Treasurer.

On December 9, 2025, the board authorized borrowing from the Clark County Treasurer's Office via interest-bearing registered warrants, with borrowing capacity ranging from $6 million to $16.5 million depending on the month. **[PROOF — Columbian, December 10, 2025]**

Clark County Treasurer Alishia Topper did not simply approve the arrangement. She issued a public warning:

*"This is the third consecutive year Vancouver Public Schools has needed a large apportionment advance, and your fund balance has been in decline. While the state bears a large share of responsibility, the district's decisions have also contributed to the situation."*

She added that *"county treasurers are not designed to provide long, ongoing operating loans to school districts."*

**[PROOF — Columbian, December 10, 2025]** This is not a partisan critique. The county treasurer's statement reflects a bipartisan institutional recognition that VPS's crisis has both external causes and internal ones.

### The Fund Balance: From $7 Million to $195,180

**[PROOF — VPS budget documents, Columbian reporting]** In August 2025, the board unanimously approved a 2025–26 budget that drew the fund balance from $7 million to **$195,180** — effectively zero reserves for a $406 million operation. The strategy avoided another round of layoffs but eliminated virtually all financial cushion.

Blechschmidt, now interim superintendent, acknowledged the situation at the December 2025 board meeting: "When we've exhausted nearly all of our cash reserves, at some point in time, we need to reverse that flow." He outlined an additional $7 million in reductions and announced the closure of Flex Academy, a small alternative high school. Students at Flex staged a walkout on December 18 to protest the closure.

The projected deficit for 2026–27 is **$20.15 million.**

### The State's Escalation Framework

**[PROOF — OSPI framework, WAC 392-123-060]** OSPI monitors district financial health through a scoring system that measures fund balance-to-revenue ratio, expenditure-to-revenue ratio, cash on hand, and budget planning adequacy. VPS received financial health scores of **1.90** (2022–23) and **1.50** (2023–24), both well below the **1.75 threshold** that triggers a formal financial warning. VPS is the only Clark County district to receive such a warning.

The escalation pathway beyond warnings is significant. Under WAC 392-123-060, if a district cannot produce a balanced budget, OSPI can impose "binding conditions." Two consecutive years without a satisfactory financial plan can trigger a Financial Oversight Committee with authority to recommend enhanced oversight, appointment of a special administrator, or even district dissolution.

VPS has not yet been placed on binding conditions. But the trajectory — three years of emergency advances, depleted reserves, growing deficits, and procurement audit findings — tracks in that direction.

### The Staffing Paradox: Growth During Decline

**[PROOF — NCES data, Larry Roe analysis published in Clark County Today]** Community critic Larry Roe — a PhD chemist and retired Intel engineering manager who ran for the VPS board in both 2023 and 2025 — has published detailed financial analyses documenting a structural imbalance:

VPS's total employee count grew from **2,225 in 2014 to 3,300 in 2024 — a 48.3 percent increase** — while enrollment dropped from 23,065 to 22,014, a loss of over 1,000 students. By comparison, neighboring Evergreen School District grew staffing only 5.8 percent over the same period while losing 4,225 students.

Roe's analysis also found:
- Over three recent school years, VPS spending exceeded tax receipts by $9 million, $15 million, and $7 million respectively
- Per-student spending doubled over ten years while test scores declined
- Property taxes rose approximately 254.8 percent from 2014 to 2024
- In 2023–24, $8.5 million collected in the Capital Projects Fund was transferred to the General Fund

Even The Columbian's editorial board, while endorsing Roe's opponent, acknowledged him as "a strong candidate" and validated concerns about the decision to spend reserves down to $195,180.

### The Administrative Footprint After Cuts

**[PROOF — NCES data, OSPI data]** After the 2024 cuts, VPS reports 21.31 district administrator FTEs — a 25 percent reduction from 28.55 in 2022–23. The ratio of 0.98 administrators per 1,000 students is below the peer average of approximately 1.10, and well below Clark County neighbor Evergreen (1.43 per 1,000).

But this NCES category is narrowly defined. When administrative support staff are included, VPS's total central office footprint is 78.30 FTE — 3.1 percent of all staff. Central administration expenditures were $14.8 million (5.4 percent of total), below the statewide average of 5.9 percent.

These numbers tell a more nuanced story than either the district or its critics would prefer. The district's administrative ratio is genuinely lean by one measure. But the 48 percent total staffing growth during enrollment decline — and the question of whether eliminated "positions" were quietly reclassified or recreated under different titles — remains unresolved.

### The Human Cost

**[PROOF — board meeting testimony, news reporting]** Behind the financial data are people.

The 50 elementary and 63 secondary teachers eliminated in 2024 represent larger class sizes, fewer course offerings, and reduced individual attention for students. The 33 TOSAs cut represent lost instructional coaching and curriculum support. The 10 counselors represent diminished mental health services during a period of escalating student need.

The VEA filed a formal grievance on June 3, 2025, challenging what it described as "secret" staffing cuts affecting 20 or more additional certificated positions beyond the original 262. **[PROOF — Columbian, June 10, 2025]**

In fall 2025, VAESP classified staff — the paraeducators, custodians, and support workers who keep schools functioning — voted over 80 percent to authorize a strike before reaching a tentative agreement. **[PROOF — KGW, 2025]**

In January 2026, the U.S. Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights opened a Title IX investigation into VPS. **[PROOF — federal records]**

And on the ground, positions that were eliminated from the district payroll did not simply disappear. Many were filled — at premium rates — by the same staffing agencies whose contracts constitute the investigation's central concern.

### A New Superintendent Inherits the Crisis

**[PROOF — Clark County Today, VPS announcement]** On January 21, 2026, the board selected **Dr. Ricardo "Rocky" Torres-Morales**, currently Associate Superintendent at Seattle Public Schools, to begin July 1, 2026. He was chosen from four finalists after a national search that attracted 40 applicants.

Torres-Morales will inherit a district facing a $20.15 million deficit, nearly zero reserves, an active state financial warning, a county borrowing arrangement, a Title IX investigation, union grievances, and a community that has watched property taxes rise 255 percent in a decade while test scores declined.

He will also inherit the administrative structures, vendor relationships, and governance practices that produced this outcome. Whether he has the authority and the will to examine them — and whether the board will support that examination — will determine whether the cycle breaks or repeats.

### What Accountability Looks Like

This investigation does not allege fraud, corruption, or criminal conduct. The evidence does not support such claims.

What the evidence does support is a pattern of institutional decision-making in which:

1. **Budget cuts fell disproportionately on lower-compensated employees** while the executive salary architecture remained largely intact.

2. **Tens of millions in public education dollars flowed to private staffing agencies** through a procurement category exempt from competitive bidding, with insufficient public documentation of who authorized these contracts and whether alternatives were pursued.

3. **The district's public framing of its own austerity** relied on geographic percentage calculations that aggregated $48,000 support staff with $183,000 executives — creating an accurate statistic that conveyed a misleading impression.

4. **Key administrators held authority without proportionate visibility.** Blechschmidt controlled financial presentation and operational spending authority simultaneously. Fish controlled HR recruitment with virtually no public accountability for outcomes.

5. **External oversight mechanisms are approaching their limits.** Three years of emergency borrowing, two consecutive audit findings, and a county treasurer's public warning represent institutional signals that the district's governance capacity is under serious question.

The incoming superintendent, the school board, and the community now face a choice: accept the structural status quo and manage the next round of cuts, or undertake the kind of transparent, independent financial examination that Clark County's largest school district has avoided.

The public records exist. The S-275 data is searchable. The F-195 reports are downloadable. The consent agendas are archived. The vendor contracts are obtainable under Washington's Public Records Act.

The question has never been whether the information is available.

It is whether anyone will look.
